# Producers/Consumers

## Learning Goal

- I can define producer and consumer.
- I can identify producers and consumers in a simple scenario.
- I can use clues from a label or receipt to explain an answer.

## Introduction

Think about a loaf of bread in a grocery store. Someone grew the grain, someone baked the bread, someone shipped it, and someone bought it. A single product can connect many people in a market.

In this lesson, you will use a product label and a receipt to tell the difference between a producer and a consumer. Your job is to explain each role with evidence from the source.

## Key Vocabulary

**producer**: a person or business that makes or provides a good or service
**consumer**: a person who buys or uses a good or service
**label**: printed information attached to a product
**receipt**: a record of something that was bought
**exchange**: when goods, services, or money move between people

## Source 1: Product Label

A label might show:

- the name of the bakery
- the type of bread
- where it was made

This source helps students identify a producer because it gives clues about who made or provided the product.

## Source 2: Store Receipt

A receipt might show:

- the item bought
- the price paid
- the date of purchase

This source helps students identify a consumer because it shows that someone paid for and received the product.

## Using the Sources Together

Observation:

- The label points to the maker of the bread.
- The receipt points to the purchase of the bread.

Reasonable explanation:

- The bakery acts as a producer because it made the bread.
- The shopper acts as a consumer because that person bought the bread.

For example, the bakery name on the bread label points to the producer, while the total on the receipt points to the consumer who bought it.

Students should use source details instead of guessing from background knowledge alone.

## Why the Roles Are Different

Producers and consumers are connected, but they do not do the same job. Producers make or provide goods and services. Consumers buy or use them. A person can even be both in different situations, but the source must show which role matters in that moment.

## Summary

A label can help identify a producer. A receipt can help identify a consumer. When students study both sources, they can explain how goods move through an exchange and who is doing what.

Before giving an answer, ask:

- Who made or provided the item?
- Who bought or used the item?
- What clue in the source supports that role?
