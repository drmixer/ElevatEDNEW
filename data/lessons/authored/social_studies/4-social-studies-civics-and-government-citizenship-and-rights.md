# Citizenship & Rights

## Learning Goal

- I can identify what a civic source says about a right.
- I can explain how responsibilities help protect rights.
- I can support a claim with evidence from a short document.

## Introduction

A democracy depends on more than people having rights. It also depends on people knowing how to use those rights in ways that protect the community. That is why civic learning asks students to read real documents and connect them to real situations.

In this lesson, you will examine a short First Amendment excerpt and a chart of civic scenarios. Your job is to explain what right a source protects and how responsible action helps that right work in daily life.

## Key Vocabulary

**democracy**: a system of government in which people take part in public decisions
**right**: a protected freedom
**responsibility**: an action that helps protect the rights of others and the health of the community
**petition**: a request to leaders asking for action or change
**evidence**: a detail from a source that supports a claim

## Source 1: First Amendment Excerpt

A short First Amendment excerpt might mention:

- freedom of speech
- freedom of religion
- freedom of assembly
- the right to petition

This source matters because it names civic protections directly. It gives students stronger evidence than a general opinion about fairness or rules.

## Source 2: Civic Scenario Chart

A chart of scenarios might include:

- students writing leaders about a safer crosswalk
- a group meeting peacefully after school
- classmates sharing different ideas in discussion

This source helps students apply document language to concrete civic action.

## Making a Source-Based Claim

Observation:

- The document protects certain freedoms.
- The chart shows people using those freedoms in community situations.

Reasonable claim:

- When students write a respectful letter to leaders, they are using the right to petition.

Supporting explanation:

- Responsibility matters because people must use rights in ways that respect safety, fairness, and the rights of others.

The strongest answers connect a scenario to exact source language instead of only saying, "It feels fair."

## Why Rights Need Responsibilities

Rights are powerful because they protect participation. Responsibilities are important because they keep participation from becoming harmful or unfair. Speaking, assembling, and petitioning are civic rights. Listening, respecting others, and using peaceful methods are civic responsibilities that help those rights work in practice.

## Summary

A civic source helps students identify protected rights. A scenario chart helps them apply those rights to real community action. Together, the sources show that democracy depends on both freedom and responsibility.

Before making a claim, ask:

- What right does the document name?
- Which scenario matches that right?
- What evidence from the source supports my answer?
