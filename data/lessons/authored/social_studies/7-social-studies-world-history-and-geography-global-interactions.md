# Global Interactions

## Learning Goal

- I can interpret a source showing regional interaction.
- I can explain one consequence of global exchange.
- I can support a claim about interaction with evidence.

## Introduction

Ideas, products, and people can move across the world through trade, migration, conquest, and communication. Those movements create opportunities, but they can also spread conflict, disease, or inequality.

In this lesson, you will examine a map and a supporting source to explain how global interactions spread change across regions.

## Key Vocabulary

**global interaction**: connection among regions through movement or exchange
**migration**: the movement of people from one place to another
**exchange**: the movement of goods, ideas, beliefs, or technologies
**consequence**: an effect or result
**network**: a system of connected places or people

## Source 1: Migration or Trade Map

The map may show:

- routes between regions
- arrows marking movement
- major connection points

This source directly shows where exchange happened and how widespread the connections were.

## Source 2: Supporting Source

A short case study, travel account, or trade description explains what moved and why it mattered. This source can show consequences the map alone cannot reveal.

## Evidence and Consequences

Observation:

- The map shows repeated contact across multiple regions.
- The supporting source describes a concrete result of that contact.

Reasonable claim:

- Global interaction changed societies by spreading something important across regional boundaries.

For example, if the map shows repeated trade contact and the supporting source describes a technology or belief moving with merchants, students can explain how exchange reshaped more than one region.

Students should be prepared to explain both positive and negative effects if the evidence supports them.

## Summary

Global interactions become easier to explain when students use more than one source. A map shows the network. A supporting source shows the human consequences of that network. Together, they help students make stronger claims about exchange and change.

When studying interaction, ask:

- What connection does the map show?
- What moved across that network?
- What result followed from that movement?
