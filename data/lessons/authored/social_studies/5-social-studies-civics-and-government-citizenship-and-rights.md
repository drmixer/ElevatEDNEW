# Citizenship & Rights

## Learning Goal

- I can identify a right protected in a founding document.
- I can explain how citizens can use rights responsibly.
- I can support a civic claim with source evidence.

## Introduction

In a democracy, citizens need ways to express disagreement without violence. Founding documents protect important freedoms, but those freedoms work best when people use them responsibly and peacefully.

In this lesson, you will examine a Bill of Rights excerpt and a petition scenario. Your job is to identify which right is involved, explain how it can be used responsibly, and support your answer with evidence from the source.

## Key Vocabulary

**Bill of Rights**: the first ten amendments to the United States Constitution
**civic right**: a protected freedom connected to public life
**petition**: a formal request asking leaders to act
**responsible action**: behavior that respects laws, safety, and the rights of others
**evidence**: a source detail that supports a claim

## Source 1: Bill of Rights Excerpt

A short excerpt may mention:

- freedom of speech
- freedom of the press
- the right of the people to assemble
- the right to petition the government

This source matters because it gives students exact document language for a civic claim.

## Source 2: Petition Scenario

A scenario might describe:

- students gathering signatures for cleaner parks
- a peaceful rally asking leaders to repair sidewalks
- a letter campaign about school safety

This source gives a modern example of how a right can be practiced.

## Claim, Evidence, and Responsibility

Observation:

- The document protects ways citizens can express concerns.
- The scenario shows people asking leaders for change.

Reasonable claim:

- The citizens are using the right to petition and speak about a public issue.

Responsible action:

- They should use peaceful methods, accurate information, and respectful communication.

The strongest answers cite the document directly and explain how responsibility protects the public good.

## Why Civic Rights Matter

Civic rights allow people to participate in government even when they disagree with leaders. Petitioning, speaking, assembling, and publishing ideas are all ways citizens can influence public life. These freedoms are powerful because they let people act without losing the protection of law.

## Summary

A founding document helps students identify protected civic rights. A petition scenario shows how those rights can be used in public life. Together, the sources show that strong citizenship includes both freedom and responsible action.

Before making a claim, ask:

- What right does the document protect?
- How does the scenario show that right in action?
- What evidence from the source supports my answer?
