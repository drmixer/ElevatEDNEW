# Local/State Government

## Learning Goal

- I can compare the responsibilities of local and state government.
- I can use a chart to classify government actions.
- I can explain which level of government should address a specific issue.

## Introduction

If a city bus route is late every day, local leaders may need to act. If a state highway system needs funding, state leaders may need to act. Public problems are often easier to solve when students understand which level of government has the right scope and responsibility.

In this lesson, you will analyze a powers chart and a sample budget problem. Your job is to decide whether local or state government should lead the response and justify that choice with source evidence.

## Key Vocabulary

**local government**: government responsible for one city, town, or county
**state government**: government responsible for the entire state
**budget**: a plan for how money will be spent
**scope**: the size or reach of a problem
**evidence**: a source detail that supports a conclusion

## Source 1: Powers Chart

A powers chart might sort examples such as:

- city bus routes
- local fire protection
- statewide highways
- state university funding

This source helps students compare responsibilities by category.

## Source 2: Budget Problem

A short budget scenario might describe:

- a city deciding whether to expand a bus route
- a state deciding how to fund a highway repair plan

This source adds a decision-making context. It shows that government action is often tied to money, scale, and who benefits.

## Using Scale and Responsibility

Observation:

- The bus route serves one community directly.
- The highway plan affects many communities across the state.

Reasonable conclusion:

- The bus route is mainly a local government issue.
- The highway plan is mainly a state government issue.

Students should explain why the choice fits the scope, not only label the answer.

## Why Government Levels Differ

Local governments can respond closely to community needs. State governments handle systems and policies that connect many communities. Understanding that difference helps students explain why public decisions are organized at more than one level.

## Summary

A powers chart shows how responsibilities differ between local and state government. A budget problem shows how those responsibilities guide real decisions. Together, the sources help students defend which level of government should act.

Before answering, ask:

- How large is the problem?
- Who is affected?
- What source detail proves which level should lead?
