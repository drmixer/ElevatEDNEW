# Markets (Intro)

## Learning Goal

- I can explain how buyers and sellers influence a market.
- I can interpret a simple market table.
- I can use source evidence to explain a price change.

## Introduction

Why might strawberries cost more this week than last week? A good economic answer starts with evidence. Students should study numbers, then read context, and only then explain what likely happened in the market.

In this lesson, you will examine a market table and a short article about changing prices. Your job is to identify what the sources show directly and use that evidence to explain a price change.

## Key Vocabulary

**market**: a system where buyers and sellers exchange goods or services
**buyer**: a person who purchases a good or service
**seller**: a person or business offering a good or service
**price change**: an increase or decrease in cost over time
**evidence**: a detail from a source that supports an explanation

## Source 1: Market Table

A table might show:

- strawberry prices last week and this week
- how many boxes were available each week
- how quickly the berries sold out

This source shows numeric evidence students can compare directly.

## Source 2: Short Article

A short article may explain that:

- storms damaged part of the crop
- fewer berries reached the market
- many buyers still wanted to purchase them

This source gives context that helps explain the pattern in the table.

## Building an Economic Explanation

Observation:

- The table shows fewer boxes available.
- The table also shows a higher price.
- The article explains why supply changed.

Reasonable explanation:

- When fewer strawberries reached the market but buyers still wanted them, the price rose.

Students should use both numeric and written evidence in the same answer.

## Why Economic Claims Need Sources

Economic questions often sound simple, but weak answers jump straight to opinion. Strong answers begin with visible evidence. A table shows the pattern. An article explains part of the cause. When students combine both, they can explain a market change more clearly and more accurately.

## Summary

Markets change when conditions change. A table shows patterns in price and quantity. A short article adds information about why that pattern may have happened. Together, the sources help students explain a price change with evidence.

Before answering, ask:

- What changed in the table?
- What cause does the article suggest?
- How do both sources support my explanation?
