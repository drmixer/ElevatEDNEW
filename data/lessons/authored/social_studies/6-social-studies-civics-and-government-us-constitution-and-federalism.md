# US Constitution & Federalism

## Learning Goal

- I can identify powers described in a constitutional source.
- I can explain the idea of separate and shared powers.
- I can support a claim about federalism with evidence from a source.

## Introduction

Suppose a state wants to set its own driver's license rules, but the national government wants one safety rule for everyone. Which level of government should decide? The Constitution does not put every power in one place. Instead, it divides authority between the federal government and the states.

In this lesson, you will use a classroom paraphrase of constitutional ideas and a powers chart to understand federalism. Your job is to see how the Constitution spreads power out and why that system can create both order and debate.

## Key Vocabulary

**Constitution**: the document that organizes the United States government
**federalism**: the system that divides power between the national government and the states
**federal power**: a power given to the national government
**state power**: a power kept by the states
**shared power**: a power both levels of government use

## Source 1: Constitutional Ideas in Paraphrase

A classroom paraphrase of constitutional ideas might say:

- Congress can coin money and manage relations with other countries.
- States can run local elections and manage many local services.
- Some powers, such as collecting taxes or enforcing laws, are shared.

What this source directly shows:

- some powers are national
- some powers stay with states
- some powers overlap

This is the basic structure of federalism. The source does not solve every real-world question, but it does show that the system is designed to divide authority rather than place all decisions in one government office.

## Source 2: Federal-State Powers Chart

A powers chart helps organize evidence by category.

Examples:

- **Federal**: printing money, making treaties
- **State**: setting many school rules, issuing driver's licenses
- **Shared**: taxation, law enforcement, public safety

The chart is useful because it turns a complex constitutional idea into a visible pattern. Students can compare a real issue to the categories instead of guessing from memory.

## Applying the Sources to a Case

Consider this question: Who has the stronger role in setting driver's license rules?

The chart suggests that states have the stronger day-to-day role because licenses are usually issued by state governments. But the constitutional idea of shared power reminds us that the federal government may still shape safety expectations in some situations.

A strong answer sounds like this:

- The strongest role belongs to the states because issuing licenses fits a state responsibility.
- The evidence comes from the powers chart category for state responsibilities.
- Federalism matters because not every issue is solved by only one level of government forever.

## Balance and Tension

Federalism creates balance because it prevents all power from sitting in one place. It also lets state governments respond to local needs. At the same time, federalism can create tension because some issues affect the whole country and local communities at once.

That is why students should avoid oversimplified claims such as "the federal government does everything important" or "states decide everything themselves." The sources show a more careful picture: power is divided, and sometimes it overlaps.

## Summary

Federalism is the constitutional system that divides authority between the national government and the states. A constitutional paraphrase shows that different levels of government hold different powers. A powers chart helps students classify those powers and support claims with evidence.

When you analyze a federalism question, ask:

- What power is involved?
- Which level of government does the source connect to that power?
- Is the power separate, shared, or disputed?
