# Medieval to Early Modern

## Learning Goal

- I can interpret maps and documentary evidence in context.
- I can explain one major transformation from the era.
- I can support a claim about change with evidence.

## Introduction

The shift from medieval to early modern history was not one event. It involved changing trade systems, stronger states, new contacts across regions, and major changes in belief and power. Historians understand these transformations by comparing geographic and written evidence.

In this lesson, you will analyze maps and documentary evidence. Your task is to explain one major transformation from the era using evidence from the sources.

## Key Vocabulary

**transformation**: a major change over time
**documentary evidence**: written historical sources used to support a claim
**state**: an organized political system with authority over territory
**context**: the larger historical situation around a source
**evidence**: the details that support a claim

## Source 1: Historical Map

A map may show:

- expanding trade routes
- changing political boundaries
- centers of power or belief

This source directly shows patterns of connection and control.

## Source 2: Documentary Evidence

A written source may describe trade, reform, authority, or exchange. It adds perspective and detail to the geographic pattern shown on the map.

## Building a Historical Claim

Observation:

- The map shows widening connections or shifting power.
- The documentary source explains how people understood or responded to those changes.

Reasonable claim:

- The era was transformed by changing networks, beliefs, or state power.

The strongest claims connect the written evidence to the geographic pattern instead of treating each source as separate information.

## Summary

The medieval to early modern world changed through connected historical processes. Maps show where those changes happened. Documentary evidence shows how people experienced and described them. Together, the sources support stronger claims about historical transformation.

Before making a claim, ask:

- What pattern appears on the map?
- What does the document reveal about that pattern?
- What transformation do the sources support together?
