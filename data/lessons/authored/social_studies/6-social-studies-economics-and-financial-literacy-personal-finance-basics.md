# Personal Finance Basics

## Learning Goal

- I can read a basic budget source.
- I can distinguish needs from wants in a spending plan.
- I can use budget evidence to explain a financial choice.

## Introduction

Imagine a student has `$25` for the week. The student wants snacks, a game download, and school supplies, but also wants to save for a class trip. The money is limited, so not every choice can happen at once.

In this lesson, you will read a weekly budget and spending log. Your job is to use evidence from the sources to explain which choices fit the plan and which choices create a problem.

## Key Vocabulary

**budget**: a plan for how money will be earned, spent, or saved
**need**: something important to daily life or responsibilities
**want**: something a person would like but does not have to buy
**savings**: money kept for a future goal or emergency
**trade-off**: giving up one choice in order to make another

## Source 1: Weekly Budget

A sample weekly budget might show:

- Money in: `$25`
- School supplies: `$6`
- Snack money: `$5`
- Savings for trip: `$8`
- Entertainment: `$4`

What the budget directly shows:

- how much money is available
- where the money is supposed to go
- that saving is part of the plan, not just extra money left over

## Source 2: Spending Log

Now compare the plan to a spending log:

- Monday: `$4` snack
- Wednesday: `$7` game card
- Friday: `$6` school supplies

The spending log helps students compare planned choices to real choices. It may show when a person goes over a category or spends money meant for savings.

## Using the Evidence

Observation:

- The budget plans only `$4` for entertainment.
- The spending log shows `$7` spent on a game card.

Reasonable conclusion:

- The student spent more on entertainment than the budget planned.

Not supported:

- "The student is bad with money forever."

Good financial reasoning stays close to what the evidence shows. The source can support a claim about this budget decision, but not a sweeping judgment about the person's character.

## Needs, Wants, and Trade-Offs

School supplies are easier to classify as a need because they connect to school responsibilities. A game card is more clearly a want. When money is limited, needs and savings goals often come before extra wants.

That is where trade-offs matter. If the student spends too much on entertainment, the budget may no longer support the class-trip savings goal. A smart revision might reduce snack or entertainment spending to protect the savings plan.

## Summary

Personal finance sources help students explain choices with evidence instead of impulse. A budget shows the plan. A spending log shows what actually happened. Together, they help students classify needs and wants, identify trade-offs, and make stronger financial recommendations.

Before making a budgeting claim, ask:

- What did the budget plan?
- What did the spending log show?
- Which choice supports the goal best?
