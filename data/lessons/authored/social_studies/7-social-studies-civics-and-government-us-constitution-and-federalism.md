# US Constitution & Federalism

## Learning Goal

- I can identify powers assigned to different levels of government.
- I can explain how federalism structures decision-making.
- I can support a claim with constitutional and chart evidence.

## Introduction

Imagine a major hurricane hits several states. Local officials need to protect people quickly, state leaders organize emergency response, and the federal government may provide money or military support. This kind of situation shows why the Constitution does not place every public duty in one office or one level of government.

In this lesson, you will analyze constitutional ideas and a powers chart to explain how federalism creates both balance and conflict. Your task is to use evidence, not opinion, to explain which level of government has the strongest role in different cases.

## Key Vocabulary

**enumerated powers**: powers specifically given to the federal government
**reserved powers**: powers kept by the states
**shared powers**: powers used by both levels of government
**federalism**: the division of power between national and state governments
**jurisdiction**: the authority to make decisions or enforce rules in a certain area

## Source 1: Constitutional Structure

A classroom source based on constitutional ideas may organize power this way:

- the federal government handles national defense, currency, and treaties
- states manage many local systems such as elections and licensing
- both levels may tax, enforce laws, and protect public safety

This source shows that the Constitution creates categories of power. It does not say every real-world case is simple. Instead, it provides a structure students can use to analyze disputes.

## Source 2: Federal-State Powers Chart

A powers chart turns the constitutional structure into a practical tool. For example:

- **Federal**: coin money, declare war, regulate national trade
- **State**: hold many elections, issue licenses, manage many state institutions
- **Shared**: taxation, courts, law enforcement

The chart helps students explain claims with evidence. Instead of saying, "I think the federal government should do it," a student can point to the category the power fits best.

## Case Study: Disaster Response

Consider a disaster-response case. A hurricane damages homes, roads, and hospitals in one state. Which level of government has the strongest role?

The best answer is usually not "only one level." Local and state governments often act first because they are closest to the emergency. Federal support may become necessary when the damage crosses local capacity or affects national systems.

A strong claim sounds like this:

- The state has the strongest immediate role in organizing the first response.
- The evidence comes from the chart showing state authority over many internal systems.
- Federal support can still matter because some powers and responsibilities overlap.

## Why Federalism Creates Conflict

Federalism creates balance by limiting concentrated power and allowing states to respond to local conditions. It also creates conflict because some problems do not fit neatly into one category. Disputes happen when leaders disagree about whether a power is national, state, or shared in practice.

That is why good constitutional reasoning must stay evidence-based. Students should support claims with the source structure instead of relying only on what outcome they personally prefer.

## Summary

Federalism divides power among federal and state governments. Constitutional structure shows the categories of power, while a powers chart helps students apply those categories to real situations. Some issues are easy to classify, while others create overlap and debate.

When you study federalism, ask:

- What kind of power is involved?
- What evidence connects that power to a level of government?
- Does the case involve separate powers, shared powers, or both?
