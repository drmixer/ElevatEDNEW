# Reconstruction to WWII

## Learning Goal

- I can interpret a source from within its historical context.
- I can explain one major turning point or tension in the era.
- I can support a claim with evidence from multiple sources.

## Introduction

The period from Reconstruction through World War II includes struggles over rights, industrial growth, reform, economic crisis, and war. To understand the era well, students need to connect written and visual sources to a larger historical timeline.

In this lesson, you will analyze a timeline, a public message source, and a short document. Your task is to explain one major turning point or tension from the era using evidence from more than one source.

## Key Vocabulary

**turning point**: a development that changes what happens next
**tension**: a serious conflict or disagreement
**public message**: a source designed to influence a broad audience
**context**: the larger historical situation around a source
**multiple sources**: more than one piece of evidence used together

## Source 1: Timeline

The timeline may include:

- Reconstruction policies
- industrial expansion
- reform movements
- economic crisis
- movement into world war

This source provides sequence and context.

## Source 2: Public Message Source

A poster, speech, or government message may reveal how leaders or groups wanted the public to respond to an issue. This source is useful for showing perspective, purpose, and public priorities.

## Source 3: Short Document

A written excerpt may provide more direct evidence about policy, conflict, or debate. Students can compare this written source to the public message and then place both on the timeline.

## Building a Claim

Observation:

- The timeline shows a period of repeated national change.
- The public message source highlights one major concern.
- The document provides direct language about that concern.

Reasonable claim:

- The sources together show one major turning point or tension in how Americans responded to national change.

For example, if the timeline shows economic crisis and the public message source urges one response to it, students can explain how that moment became a turning point in national policy.

Students should support the claim with at least one detail from two different sources, then use the timeline to explain why the issue mattered in that moment.

## Summary

Reconstruction through World War II is best understood through multiple kinds of evidence. Timelines provide sequence. Public messages reveal priorities and persuasion. Written documents add direct historical language. Together, they support stronger claims about turning points and tension.

Before making a claim, ask:

- What issue do the sources share?
- What detail from each source supports the claim?
- Where does the timeline place that issue?
