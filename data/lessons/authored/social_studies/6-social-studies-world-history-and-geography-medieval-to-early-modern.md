# Medieval to Early Modern

## Learning Goal

- I can use map evidence to identify a historical connection.
- I can interpret a short source in context.
- I can support a claim about change over time with evidence.

## Introduction

The medieval and early modern eras were shaped by trade, belief systems, and shifting political power. Historians often study this period by pairing maps with written sources so students can see both connection and perspective.

In this lesson, you will examine a map and a short historical excerpt. Your job is to explain one important change from the era using evidence from both sources.

## Key Vocabulary

**medieval**: the historical period between ancient times and the early modern era
**early modern**: the period when trade, states, and global connections expanded in new ways
**context**: the time, place, and situation around a source
**perspective**: the point of view reflected in a source
**change over time**: how conditions develop across periods

## Source 1: Historical Map

A map of the era may show:

- trade routes
- political boundaries
- important cities or centers of belief

The map directly shows geographic connections. It helps students understand where power, wealth, and ideas moved.

## Source 2: Historical Excerpt

A short excerpt might describe a traveler, ruler, merchant, or religious institution. The source reveals what one person or group noticed and valued in that moment.

That means the source is useful, but it must be read in context. Students should ask who created it and what part of the world it reflects.

## Using Map and Text Together

Observation:

- The map shows long-distance connections between regions.
- The excerpt describes how trade, belief, or authority mattered to people in the time period.

Reasonable claim:

- Expanding connections helped reshape societies during the move from medieval to early modern times.

The strongest answers use the map for structure and the excerpt for detail or perspective.

## Summary

The medieval to early modern period was shaped by changing connections. Maps show where those links existed. Written sources show how people experienced or understood them. Together, the sources help students explain historical transformation with evidence.

Before making a claim, ask:

- What connection does the map show?
- What does the excerpt add?
- How do the two sources together show change over time?
