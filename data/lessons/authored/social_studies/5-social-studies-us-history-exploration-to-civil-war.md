# Exploration to Civil War

## Learning Goal

- I can place key events in order and context.
- I can identify one cause of growing sectional conflict.
- I can use evidence from multiple sources to support a claim.

## Introduction

As the United States expanded, arguments grew over power, economy, and slavery. Historians explain this period by connecting maps, timelines, and written sources instead of treating events as isolated facts.

In this lesson, you will examine a westward-expansion map, a timeline, and a short source set. Your job is to explain how expansion contributed to sectional conflict and support your answer with evidence from more than one source.

## Key Vocabulary

**sectional conflict**: disagreement between different regions of the country
**expansion**: growth in land, settlement, or political power
**context**: the larger time and situation around an event
**source set**: a small group of sources studied together
**evidence**: a detail from a source that supports a claim

## Source 1: Map and Timeline

These sources may show:

- new territory added to the United States
- major events linked to westward growth
- dates that place those events in sequence

Together, they help students see where and when change happened.

## Source 2: Short Historical Excerpts

A source set might include:

- a statement from a political leader
- a line from a newspaper
- a short account of debate over slavery in new territories

These sources add perspective and conflict to the broader context.

## Using Multiple Sources for a Claim

Observation:

- The country gained new territory over time.
- The written sources show disagreement about what those new territories should allow.

Reasonable claim:

- Expansion increased sectional conflict because each new territory raised questions about power and slavery.

Students should support that claim with at least one map or timeline detail and one written-source detail.

## Why Context Matters

A single event rarely causes a major conflict by itself. Historians look at patterns over time. Maps and timelines show those patterns. Written sources reveal how people argued about them. Together, the sources help students explain how the nation moved closer to the Civil War.

## Summary

The road from exploration to the Civil War includes growing conflict over expansion and power. A map and timeline provide historical context. A short source set reveals the disagreements within that context. Together, they support a stronger historical claim.

Before making a claim, ask:

- What changed over time?
- What disagreement do the written sources reveal?
- Which details from multiple sources support my answer?
