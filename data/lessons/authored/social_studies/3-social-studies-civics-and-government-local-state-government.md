# Local/State Government

## Learning Goal

- I can identify a job of local government.
- I can identify a job of state government.
- I can use details from a chart or map to justify an answer.

## Introduction

Suppose a stop sign falls down near your school. Now suppose a state park needs new safety rules. Both problems involve government, but the same level of government may not handle both jobs.

In this lesson, you will read a town-services chart and a state map. Your job is to decide which problems are handled close to home and which ones are handled across the whole state.

## Key Vocabulary

**local government**: government that serves a town, city, or county
**state government**: government that serves the whole state
**service**: a job or action that helps people in a community
**community**: the people and places around where you live
**symbol**: a picture or mark on a map that stands for something

## Source 1: Town Services Chart

A town-services chart might list:

- fixing neighborhood streets
- running the public library
- collecting trash
- caring for local parks

This source helps students notice that local government usually handles nearby services people use every day.

## Source 2: State Map

A state map might show:

- the state capitol
- state parks
- major highways
- regions across the whole state

This source reminds students that state government works on issues that affect many communities, not just one neighborhood.

## Sorting the Evidence

Observation:

- The chart focuses on jobs close to home.
- The map shows places and systems that stretch across the state.

Reasonable conclusion:

- Repairing a playground or library roof is usually a local government job.
- Managing a statewide park system or statewide road rules fits state government better.

For example, fixing one town's library roof is local because it serves a nearby community, while setting rules for state parks is statewide because many communities use them.

Students should explain why, not just name a category. The best answers use words like `close to home`, `whole state`, or `many communities`.

## Why the Two Levels Matter

Communities need different levels of government because some problems are small and local, while others affect a much larger area. Local leaders can respond to nearby community needs. State leaders can coordinate decisions that need to be more consistent across the state.

## Summary

Local government and state government both serve people, but they do not do the same jobs. A services chart shows nearby needs. A state map shows the larger area state government must serve. Students should use source details to explain which level fits the problem.

Before giving an answer, ask:

- Is this problem close to home or across the state?
- What detail from the chart or map helps me decide?
- Why does that level of government make sense?
