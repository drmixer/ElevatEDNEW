# Ancient Civilizations

## Learning Goal

- I can use map evidence to explain why civilizations developed in certain places.
- I can compare what different artifacts reveal about society.
- I can support a historical inference with source evidence.

## Introduction

Ancient civilizations did not appear randomly. Geography shaped where people could farm, trade, travel, and build cities. Artifacts reveal how those societies organized life, belief, work, and communication.

In this lesson, you will compare maps and artifacts from ancient civilizations. Your job is to explain how environment and human choices helped shape civilization life.

## Key Vocabulary

**civilization**: a large organized society
**artifact**: an object made or used by people in the past
**environment**: the natural surroundings of a place
**innovation**: a new method, tool, or idea
**inference**: a conclusion based on evidence

## Source 1: Civilization Maps

Maps may show:

- river valleys
- trade routes
- settlement patterns

These sources help students explain why certain places supported large societies.

## Source 2: Artifact Comparison

Artifacts might include:

- a writing tablet
- a tool or weapon
- a religious image or decorated container

These sources help students compare what societies valued or needed.

## Comparing the Evidence

Observation:

- The maps show where people had access to water or trade routes.
- The artifacts show evidence of work, belief, or communication.

Reasonable inference:

- Civilizations often developed where geography supported farming or exchange.
- Artifacts reveal how people organized daily life and important ideas.

Students should compare sources instead of listing unrelated facts.

## Why Comparison Strengthens History

A single artifact can reveal one detail. A map can reveal geographic context. Comparing several sources allows students to recognize patterns. Historians use those patterns to explain how environment, belief, and innovation shaped societies over time.

## Summary

Ancient civilizations can be understood through both geography and material evidence. Maps show where and why civilizations developed. Artifacts show what those societies made, used, and valued. Together, the sources support stronger historical inference.

Before answering, ask:

- What does the map explain about place?
- What does each artifact reveal?
- How do the sources work together to support my inference?
