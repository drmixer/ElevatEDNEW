# Reconstruction to WWII

## Learning Goal

- I can interpret a source from within its historical context.
- I can explain one major tension or turning point from the era.
- I can support a historical claim with evidence from multiple sources.

## Introduction

From Reconstruction to World War II, the United States changed through industrial growth, reform movements, conflict over rights, and new roles in the world. Political cartoons and documents from the era can be powerful sources, but they only make sense when students place them in context.

In this lesson, you will analyze a timeline, a political cartoon, and a short document. Your job is to explain one major tension or turning point in the era using evidence from more than one source.

## Key Vocabulary

**political cartoon**: a visual source that comments on public issues using symbols or exaggeration
**turning point**: a development that changes what happens next
**tension**: a serious conflict or disagreement
**context**: the larger time, place, and situation around a source
**multiple sources**: more than one piece of evidence used together

## Source 1: Timeline

The timeline may show:

- Reconstruction debates
- industrial growth
- reform movements
- economic crisis
- movement toward world war

The timeline gives chronological structure so students can locate the other sources accurately.

## Source 2: Political Cartoon

A political cartoon may criticize corruption, inequality, reform failure, or government action. Students should first identify what is directly visible:

- symbols
- labels
- exaggerated features

Then they should ask what issue the cartoon is commenting on.

## Source 3: Short Document

A speech, law excerpt, or public statement adds written evidence. This source can clarify a debate the cartoon only suggests visually.

## Using Multiple Sources

Observation:

- The cartoon highlights a conflict or criticism.
- The document names a policy or public concern.
- The timeline places both inside a broader era of change.

Reasonable claim:

- The sources together show one major tension in how Americans responded to political and social change.

For example, if a political cartoon criticizes inequality and a document names the related policy debate, students can explain one major tension in the era with evidence from both sources.

Students should not rely on the cartoon alone. The best explanations compare the visual source to the written one and then place both in time.

## Summary

Reconstruction through World War II is best studied through multiple types of evidence. A timeline provides sequence. A political cartoon reveals criticism or tension. A document gives direct language from the era. Together, they help students build more careful historical claims.

Before making a claim, ask:

- What does each source directly show?
- What issue connects the sources?
- How does the timeline place that issue in context?
