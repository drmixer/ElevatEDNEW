# Local/State Government

## Learning Goal

- I can compare responsibilities of local and state government.
- I can use a chart to classify examples correctly.
- I can explain why one level of government fits a problem better than the other.

## Introduction

Not every public problem belongs to the same level of government. A city playground, a statewide road rule, and a town library all require decisions, but they do not all belong to the same public office or budget.

In this lesson, you will examine a powers chart and a state map. Your job is to compare local and state government by looking at who is affected, how large the problem is, and what the source shows.

## Key Vocabulary

**local government**: government that serves a city, town, or county
**state government**: government that serves the whole state
**responsibility**: a job or duty a government is expected to handle
**scale**: how large an area or population is affected
**public service**: work government does for the community

## Source 1: Powers Chart

A powers chart might sort examples such as:

- local roads
- public libraries
- city parks
- state highways
- statewide licensing rules
- state parks

This source helps students compare responsibilities side by side instead of treating all government work as one category.

## Source 2: State Map

A state map may show:

- where cities are located
- the state capitol
- major roads that connect regions

The map adds place and scale. It shows why a statewide issue is different from a neighborhood issue.

## How To Justify the Choice

Observation:

- Some services affect one community directly.
- Other services connect people across many cities and regions.

Reasonable explanation:

- Repairing a city playground fits local government because the issue is limited to one community.
- Setting statewide license rules fits state government because the issue needs one larger system.

Students should cite source details such as the chart category or the wider area shown on the map.

## Why Scale Matters

Government decisions are often shaped by scope. Local government can respond closely to nearby needs. State government can coordinate rules and services across a much larger area. Understanding scale helps students explain why one level of government is better positioned to act.

## Summary

A powers chart helps students classify government responsibilities. A state map helps them think about the size of the problem and who is affected. Together, these sources support stronger explanations of local and state government.

Before answering, ask:

- How big is the issue?
- Who is affected by it?
- Which source detail shows why this level of government fits?
