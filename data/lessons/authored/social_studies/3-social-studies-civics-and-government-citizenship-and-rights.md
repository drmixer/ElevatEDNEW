# Citizenship & Rights

## Learning Goal

- I can identify one right named in a civic source.
- I can explain how a responsibility helps protect rights in a community.
- I can use a detail from a short source to support an answer.

## Introduction

Imagine that your class is making a poster for a school citizenship wall. One card says, "Share your ideas respectfully." Another says, "Walk in the hallway." A third says, "Clean up your space after art." Those ideas are not all the same kind of civic rule.

In this lesson, you will read a kid-friendly rights poster and a short paraphrase of the First Amendment. Your job is to figure out which ideas are rights, which are rules, and which are responsibilities. Then you will use source evidence to explain why responsibilities help protect rights.

## Key Vocabulary

**right**: a freedom people are protected to have
**rule**: a direction people are expected to follow
**responsibility**: an action that helps a community work well
**evidence**: a detail from a source that supports an answer
**First Amendment**: part of the Bill of Rights that protects important freedoms like speech

## Source 1: Rights Poster

A simple rights poster might list ideas such as:

- sharing opinions respectfully
- practicing a religion
- meeting peacefully

This source helps students notice that rights protect freedoms. The poster also shows that rights are about what people are allowed to do, not just what they are told to do.

## Source 2: First Amendment Paraphrase

A kid-friendly paraphrase of the First Amendment might explain that the government cannot unfairly stop people from speaking, practicing religion, gathering peacefully, or asking leaders to solve problems.

This source matters because it gives a reason those freedoms count as rights. The document does not list every classroom rule. It explains protections people have in a democracy.

## Using the Evidence

Observation:

- The poster lists freedoms people can practice.
- The amendment paraphrase explains that these freedoms are protected.

Reasonable explanation:

- A right is different from a rule because a right protects freedom.
- A responsibility helps people use that freedom in a safe and fair way.

Example:

- Students may have the right to share ideas.
- They also have the responsibility to listen and speak respectfully so other people can share ideas too.

## Rights and Responsibilities Work Together

Rights are strongest when people understand how to use them responsibly. If everyone speaks at once, nobody is really heard. If people use hurtful words, the community becomes less fair and less safe. Responsibilities do not erase rights. They help protect the rights of everyone.

## Summary

Citizenship means knowing the difference between a right, a rule, and a responsibility. A rights poster shows important freedoms. A First Amendment source explains why those freedoms are protected. Responsibilities matter because they help a community protect those freedoms for all.

Before giving an answer, ask:

- What freedom does the source name?
- Is this a right, a rule, or a responsibility?
- What detail from the source proves it?
