# Supply and Demand (Intro)

## Learning Goal

- I can describe what supply and demand mean in a source-based context.
- I can interpret a basic market chart.
- I can use chart evidence to explain a price change.

## Introduction

Why do concert tickets sometimes cost much more a few days before a show? The answer often has to do with how many tickets are available and how many people want them.

In this lesson, you will examine a price-and-quantity chart. Your job is to identify what the source directly shows and then explain whether supply, demand, or both help explain the price pattern.

## Key Vocabulary

**supply**: how much of a good or service sellers are willing to offer
**demand**: how much of a good or service buyers want to buy
**quantity**: how much of something is available or purchased
**market**: a system where buyers and sellers exchange goods or services
**price**: the amount paid for a good or service

## Source 1: Price-and-Quantity Chart

A simple chart might show:

- Week 1: 100 tickets left, `$20`
- Week 2: 40 tickets left, `$35`
- Week 3: 10 tickets left, `$50`

What the chart directly shows:

- ticket quantity goes down over time
- price goes up over time

The chart does not directly tell us why, but it gives important evidence about what changed in the market.

## Observation Before Explanation

Students should first name what the chart shows before jumping to economic terms.

Observation:

- fewer tickets are available in later weeks
- the price is higher when fewer tickets remain

Reasonable explanation:

- a limited supply and strong demand may be pushing the price upward

For example, when tickets drop from 100 to 10 while the price rises from `$20` to `$50`, limited supply with strong demand is a reasonable explanation.

Unsupported claim:

- "The seller is being unfair."

That claim may or may not be true, but the chart alone does not prove it. Good economic reasoning starts with the visible pattern in the source.

## Connecting Supply and Demand

Supply refers to how many tickets are available for sale. Demand refers to how strongly buyers want those tickets. When many people want a limited number of tickets, the market often responds with higher prices.

This does not mean every price change has the same cause. Students should learn to say:

- what the chart shows directly
- what economic idea best explains that pattern
- what the chart cannot prove by itself

## Summary

Market charts help students connect numbers to economic ideas. A chart can show a pattern in quantity and price. Then students can use that evidence to explain how supply and demand may be shaping the outcome.

Before making an economic claim, ask:

- What changed in the chart?
- Which part of the market does that suggest?
- What does the source not tell me yet?
