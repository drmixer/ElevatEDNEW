# Elections & Civic Participation

## Learning Goal

- I can identify what a ballot and turnout chart directly show.
- I can explain why participation matters in a democracy.
- I can support a civic claim with evidence from an election source.

## Introduction

Imagine that two towns hold the same school-board election. In both towns, the winning candidate receives 300 votes. In the first town, 320 people voted. In the second town, 1,000 people voted. The result looks the same at first, but the turnout tells a different story about how many people took part.

In this lesson, you will examine two election sources: a sample ballot and a turnout chart. Your job is to notice what each source directly shows, then explain what that evidence suggests about participation and representation.

## Key Vocabulary

**ballot**: the list of offices or questions voters respond to in an election
**turnout**: the number or percent of eligible voters who actually vote
**candidate**: a person running for office
**measure**: a proposal that voters can approve or reject
**participation**: taking part in civic life, such as voting, speaking at meetings, or contacting leaders

## Source 1: Sample Ballot

A sample ballot can tell you what choices voters have to make. A ballot might include:

- one school-board race with two candidates
- one city-council race
- one measure asking whether the town should fund a new library room

What the ballot directly shows:

- what offices are being decided
- how many choices appear on the ballot
- whether voters are choosing people, measures, or both

What the ballot does not show by itself:

- which choice is best
- why people voted the way they did
- whether many or few people participated

## Source 2: Turnout Chart

Now look at a turnout chart for the same election:

- Town A: 320 of 400 eligible voters cast ballots
- Town B: 320 of 1,000 eligible voters cast ballots

The chart directly shows that the same number of ballots were cast in both towns. It also shows that participation was much higher in Town A than in Town B.

That matters because turnout helps us judge how much of the community took part in the decision. A result based on broad participation may reflect more community involvement than a result shaped by a smaller share of eligible voters.

## Using the Evidence

Careful civic reasoning separates observation from inference.

Observation:

- The ballot includes a school-board race and a library measure.
- Town A has higher turnout than Town B.

Reasonable inference:

- More of Town A's eligible voters took part in choosing leaders and deciding the measure.

Not supported:

- "Town B did not care about the election."

The chart does not explain motives. It only shows the participation pattern. Good civic analysis uses the source for what it can prove and avoids claims the source cannot support.

## Why Participation Matters

Participation matters because elections are one way communities make decisions together. When people vote, they help decide who represents them and what public actions move forward. Voting is not the only civic action, but it is one important way citizens take part in public life.

Low turnout does not automatically make an election invalid, but it does raise questions about how many voices were included. That is why turnout charts are useful evidence: they show whether participation was broad, narrow, or somewhere in between.

## Summary

Election sources help students answer different civic questions. A ballot shows what choices voters face. A turnout chart shows how many people participated. When we combine those sources, we can explain not only what the election was about, but also how strongly the community took part.

When you analyze election evidence, ask:

- What does the source directly show?
- What conclusion does that evidence support?
- What claim would go beyond the evidence?
