# Markets (Intro)

## Learning Goal

- I can describe how buyers and sellers interact in a market.
- I can use a table to compare prices and quantities.
- I can explain how a market source helps answer an economic question.

## Introduction

If orange prices rise after a week of storms, students should not guess blindly about the cause. They should first examine the source. A table and short article can show what changed in the market and suggest why.

In this lesson, you will analyze a market price table and a short market blurb. Your job is to identify direct observations first, then build a simple evidence-based explanation.

## Key Vocabulary

**market**: a system where buyers and sellers exchange goods or services
**buyer**: a person who purchases a good or service
**seller**: a person or business offering a good or service
**quantity**: how much of something is available or sold
**evidence**: information from a source that supports an explanation

## Source 1: Market Table

A market table might show:

- fruit prices before a rainy week
- fruit prices after a rainy week
- how many boxes of fruit are available

This source directly shows changes in price and quantity. It gives students visible evidence before they use economic words such as `supply` or `demand`.

## Source 2: Market Blurb

A short market blurb might explain that heavy rain damaged part of a crop or delayed deliveries.

This source adds context. It helps students move from observation to explanation, but only after they have noticed what the numbers show.

## Observation Before Explanation

Observation:

- One fruit costs more after the rainy week.
- Fewer boxes are available.

Reasonable explanation:

- The market may have less of that fruit available, so buyers are seeing a higher price.

Students should avoid unsupported claims such as "the store was greedy." The sources show market change, not hidden motives.

## Why Sources Matter in Economics

Economic reasoning is stronger when students can point to numbers and text together. A table shows what changed. A short article explains part of the situation behind that change. Using both sources helps students answer an economic question with evidence instead of opinion.

## Summary

Markets respond to changing conditions. A table shows patterns in price and quantity. A short article adds context for those patterns. Together, the sources help students explain what happened in the market.

Before answering, ask:

- What changed in the table?
- What clue does the article add?
- What explanation fits the evidence best?
