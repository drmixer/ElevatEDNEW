# Tradeoffs & Constraints

## Learning Goal
- I can explain how constraints limit engineering decisions.
- I can identify tradeoffs between competing design goals.
- I can support a design choice by comparing evidence across multiple criteria.

## Introduction
In engineering, there is rarely one perfect solution. A design that is stronger may cost more. A design that is lighter may be less durable. A design that works well in one condition may be less safe or less practical in another.

This is why engineers evaluate tradeoffs under real constraints. In this lesson, ask:

1. What limits the design?
2. Which design goals compete with one another?
3. How can evidence support the best overall choice?

## Key Vocabulary
**Constraint**: A limit on a design, such as cost, time, materials, or size.

**Tradeoff**: A compromise in which improving one feature may reduce another.

**Criteria**: The features a successful design must achieve.

**Durability**: How well a design holds up during use.

**Efficiency**: How well a design works with limited waste.

**Recommendation**: A supported engineering choice.

## Phenomenon: Designing an Emergency Shelter
Student teams are asked to design a temporary emergency shelter model for an area after a severe storm. Their shelter must:

- resist wind from a fan test
- use low-cost materials
- be assembled quickly
- provide enough interior space for supplies

After testing, teams notice that the strongest structures are sometimes heavier and slower to assemble, while the fastest structures are often less stable.

The question is:

How do engineers choose among designs when no option is best in every category?

## Constraints Shape the Solution
Constraints are not extra details. They are part of the engineering problem itself.

In the shelter challenge, important constraints include:

- limited cost
- limited build time
- available materials
- required stability and interior space

A design that ignores any of these may fail even if it performs well in one category.

## Tradeoffs Are Part of Real Engineering
Tradeoffs happen when improving one feature affects another.

Examples:

- adding more support beams may improve strength but increase cost and build time
- using lighter materials may speed assembly but reduce stability
- increasing interior space may make the structure harder to reinforce

Engineering decisions become stronger when students explain these tradeoffs clearly instead of pretending one design is perfect.

## Worked Example 1: Choosing Between Two Shelter Designs
Shelter A:

- survives the highest wind test
- costs the most
- takes the longest to assemble

Shelter B:

- survives a slightly lower wind test
- stays within budget
- assembles quickly enough for emergency use

Which design may be the better recommendation?

A strong answer is:

"Shelter B may be the better recommendation if it still meets the required stability criterion, because it balances safety with cost and speed."

Reasoning:

- the strongest design is not automatically the best engineering choice
- if Shelter B meets the minimum performance target, its lower cost and faster assembly may make it more practical in the real situation

## Worked Example 2: Recognizing a Tradeoff
Suppose students add extra cardboard walls to increase shelter strength. The revised design performs better in wind tests, but the interior storage space becomes too small.

What tradeoff occurred?

"The team improved strength but reduced usable interior space."

That answer is strong because it names both sides of the tradeoff and connects them to the design goals.

## Worked Example 3: Supporting a Recommendation With Evidence
What should a strong engineering recommendation include?

It should include:

- the key criteria
- the important constraints
- the test results
- an explanation of why the chosen design is the best overall fit

Example:

"We recommend Shelter B because it stayed under budget, assembled in the target time, and survived the required wind test. Shelter A was stronger, but its higher cost and slower build time made it less useful for the emergency scenario."

## Common Mistake: Choosing the Highest Score in Only One Category
Students often say:

"This design had the best wind score, so it wins."

That is too simple.

A stronger method is:

1. check whether the design meets the required criteria
2. compare how it performs under the constraints
3. explain the tradeoffs before making the recommendation

## Summary
Engineering decisions depend on balancing criteria, constraints, and tradeoffs.

Key ideas from this lesson:

1. constraints define what counts as a usable solution
2. tradeoffs happen when improving one feature affects another
3. strong design recommendations use evidence across multiple criteria, not just one best score

When you analyze an engineering problem, ask what the limits are, what tradeoffs appear, and which design best fits the full situation.
