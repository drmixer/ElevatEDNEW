# Ratios and Proportional Reasoning (Grade 6)

## Learning Goal
- I can explain what a ratio compares and keep the quantities in the correct order.
- I can find equivalent ratios by scaling both parts of a comparison.
- I can use tables and unit rates to solve proportional reasoning problems.

## Introduction
Ratios compare two quantities in a structured way.

A recipe might compare cups of juice to cups of water. A map might compare inches to miles. A speed might compare miles to hours.

The important idea is not just "two numbers." A ratio tells how one quantity changes with another quantity. When that comparison stays consistent, the relationship is proportional.

In this lesson, focus on three questions:

1. What exactly is being compared?
2. How can I scale the ratio without changing its meaning?
3. When is a unit rate the fastest way to solve the problem?

## Key Vocabulary
**Ratio**: A comparison of two quantities, such as `3 to 5`, `3:5`, or `3/5`.

**Equivalent ratios**: Ratios that name the same comparison because both parts were scaled by the same factor.

**Unit rate**: A rate written for `1` unit, such as `2.5 muffins per cup`.

**Proportional relationship**: A relationship in which equivalent ratios keep the same multiplicative comparison.

**Constant of proportionality**: The number that tells how much of one quantity goes with `1` unit of another quantity.

## Understanding What a Ratio Means
A ratio must name the two quantities in order.

If the ratio of cats to dogs is `3:5`, that means:

- for every `3` cats, there are `5` dogs
- the first number belongs to cats
- the second number belongs to dogs

It does not mean:

- there are always `8` animals total
- the numbers can be switched without changing the meaning

This is a common mistake. `3:5` cats to dogs is not the same as `3:5` dogs to cats. The labels matter.

## Worked Example 1: Scaling an Equivalent Ratio
Suppose the ratio of cats to dogs at a shelter is `3:5`. If there are `18` cats, how many dogs are there?

Start by asking: how did `3` become `18`?

`3 x 6 = 18`

So the ratio was scaled by `6`. To keep the ratio equivalent, scale the dogs by `6` too:

`5 x 6 = 30`

So there are `30` dogs.

Why this works:

- the comparison `3:5` stays the same
- both parts were multiplied by the same factor
- the relationship remains proportional

A quick table makes the pattern easier to see:

| Cats | Dogs |
| --- | --- |
| 3 | 5 |
| 6 | 10 |
| 9 | 15 |
| 18 | 30 |

Each row is an equivalent ratio.

## Worked Example 2: Finding a Unit Rate
A recipe uses `4` cups of flour for `10` muffins. How many muffins per cup of flour?

The words "per `1`" suggest a unit rate.

Divide the total muffins by the total cups:

`10 / 4 = 2.5`

So the unit rate is `2.5 muffins per cup`.

This tells us:

- every `1` cup of flour makes `2.5` muffins
- the constant of proportionality is `2.5`

Once you know the unit rate, you can solve other questions quickly.

If a baker uses `8` cups of flour, multiply:

`8 x 2.5 = 20`

So `8` cups of flour make `20` muffins.

## Table, Unit Rate, or Scale Factor?
These are three valid tools. The best one depends on the problem.

- Use a **scale factor** when one value changes cleanly into another.
- Use a **table** when you want to see several equivalent ratios and track the pattern.
- Use a **unit rate** when the question asks for "per `1`" or when you want a reusable comparison.

Example:

If `12` out of `30` bikes are mountain bikes, then the percent can be found by writing the ratio as a fraction:

`12/30 = 0.4 = 40%`

That works because percent means "out of `100`," and `0.4` is the same as `40/100`.

## How To Recognize a Proportional Relationship
A relationship is proportional when:

- the ratios stay equivalent
- the unit rate stays constant
- one quantity is always a constant multiple of the other

Look at these pairs:

- `2` notebooks cost `$6`
- `5` notebooks cost `$15`
- `8` notebooks cost `$24`

Find the unit price:

- `6 / 2 = 3`
- `15 / 5 = 3`
- `24 / 8 = 3`

The unit rate is always `$3 per notebook`, so the relationship is proportional.

Now compare that to:

- `2` notebooks cost `$6`
- `5` notebooks cost `$14`

The unit rates are different:

- `6 / 2 = 3`
- `14 / 5 = 2.8`

Because the multiplicative comparison changes, the relationship is not proportional.

## Common Mistake: Additive Thinking Instead of Multiplicative Thinking
Students often notice how much was added instead of how much was multiplied.

Suppose the ratio is `4:6`, and you want an equivalent ratio with first term `12`.

Incorrect idea:

- "`4` became `12`, so I add `8`."
- then add `8` to `6` and get `14`

That gives `12:14`, which is not equivalent.

Correct idea:

- ask what number multiplies `4` to get `12`
- `4 x 3 = 12`
- then multiply `6 x 3 = 18`

So the equivalent ratio is `12:18`.

Ratios depend on multiplication, not just addition.

## Try the Reasoning
Use the questions below as a self-check.

1. If `5` tickets cost `$20`, what is the unit price?
2. If a map scale is `1 inch : 8 miles`, how many miles does `3` inches represent?
3. If `9` red beads match `12` blue beads, is `3:4` an equivalent ratio? Why?

Strong answers should name:

- what is being compared
- which method you used
- why the ratio stayed the same

## Summary
Ratios and proportional reasoning are about consistent multiplicative comparisons.

Remember these checks:

- keep the quantities in the correct order
- scale both parts of a ratio by the same factor
- use a unit rate to compare "per `1`"
- test proportionality by checking whether the unit rate stays constant

When you solve a new ratio problem, do not ask only, "What changed?" Ask, "How was this scaled?" That question leads you to the structure of the relationship.
