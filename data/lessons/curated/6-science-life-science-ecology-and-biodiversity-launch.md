# Ecology & Biodiversity

## Learning Goal
- I can explain how living things in an ecosystem depend on one another.
- I can describe how biodiversity can help an ecosystem stay stable.
- I can use evidence from a change in an ecosystem to support a scientific claim.

## Introduction
An ecosystem is more than a list of organisms. It is a system of connected living things and nonliving conditions. If one part changes, many other parts may be affected too.

In this lesson, ask:

1. What happens when one part of an ecosystem changes?
2. Why does biodiversity matter?
3. How can evidence from the system support a claim about stability or disruption?

## Key Vocabulary
**Ecosystem**: A community of organisms and their environment interacting as a system.

**Biodiversity**: The variety of living things in an ecosystem.

**Food web**: A model showing feeding connections among organisms.

**Habitat**: The place where an organism lives.

**Resilience**: The ability of a system to keep functioning or recover after change.

**Disturbance**: An event that changes an ecosystem.

## Phenomenon: One Species Declines in a Pond
Scientists study a pond over time and observe:

- one kind of insect becomes much less common
- some fish that feed on those insects also decrease
- algae increase because grazing organisms are less common
- the pond looks different after several months

The question is:

How can a change in one part of a pond affect the whole ecosystem?

## Ecosystems Are Connected
In an ecosystem, organisms are connected by:

- food
- shelter
- competition
- environmental conditions such as water and light

Because of these connections, one disturbance can spread through the system.

## Why Biodiversity Matters
Biodiversity matters because ecosystems with more kinds of organisms often have more ways to keep functioning.

If one species declines:

- another species may sometimes fill a similar role
- food web links may continue instead of breaking completely
- the system may recover more easily after disturbance

This does not mean biodiversity prevents all damage. It means it can help the system stay more stable.

## Worked Example 1: Tracing a Food-Web Effect
Suppose a pond loses many aquatic insects.

What is a likely effect?

"Fish that depend on those insects for food may also decrease."

Reasoning:

- fewer insects means less food
- lower food availability can affect the next level in the food web

## Worked Example 2: Comparing Two Ecosystems
Grassland A has many plant species. Grassland B depends mostly on one plant species.

Which grassland may be more resilient during a dry period?

"Grassland A may be more resilient because its biodiversity gives the system more ways to keep functioning."

## Worked Example 3: Human Impact
Suppose a wetland is partly drained and nearby flooding becomes worse.

What is a reasonable claim?

"The wetland likely helped manage water, so changing the ecosystem reduced an important system function."

This example shows that ecosystems also affect human communities.

## Common Mistake: Treating Biodiversity Like Decoration
Students sometimes think biodiversity only means having more organisms to observe.

A stronger method is:

1. ask what role each organism plays
2. consider what happens if one role is weakened
3. explain how biodiversity affects the whole system

## Summary
Ecology is about system connections, not isolated organisms.

Key ideas from this lesson:

1. changes in one part of an ecosystem can spread through the system
2. biodiversity can help support resilience
3. strong ecological claims use evidence about food webs, habitats, and system change

When you study an ecosystem, ask how the parts are connected and what pattern the evidence shows.
