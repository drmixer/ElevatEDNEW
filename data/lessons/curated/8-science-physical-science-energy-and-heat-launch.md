# Energy & Heat

## Learning Goal
- I can explain how thermal energy moves by conduction, convection, and radiation.
- I can use evidence to describe how energy transfer changes the temperature of objects or systems.
- I can compare different designs or materials based on how well they reduce or increase heat transfer.

## Introduction
A metal spoon in hot soup warms up. A blacktop parking lot gets hotter than nearby grass. A thermos keeps a drink warm much longer than a paper cup.

These are all energy-transfer phenomena. Heat does not "stay still." Thermal energy moves from warmer places to cooler ones, and the path it takes depends on the material and the system.

In this lesson, ask:

1. Where is the energy moving?
2. Which transfer process explains the pattern?
3. What evidence shows why one design works better than another?

## Key Vocabulary
**Thermal energy**: The energy associated with the motion of particles in matter.

**Conduction**: Transfer of thermal energy through direct contact.

**Convection**: Transfer of thermal energy by the movement of fluids such as liquids or gases.

**Radiation**: Transfer of energy by electromagnetic waves, without requiring direct contact.

**Insulator**: A material that slows the transfer of thermal energy.

**System**: A set of interacting parts considered together.

## Phenomenon: Which Cup Keeps Water Warmest?
Students pour the same amount of hot water into three containers:

- a metal cup
- a paper cup
- an insulated travel cup

After 20 minutes, they measure the water temperatures and observe:

- the metal cup loses heat fastest
- the paper cup loses heat more slowly
- the insulated cup keeps the highest temperature
- the outside of the metal cup feels warm quickly

The question is:

Why do the cups transfer energy at different rates?

## How Heat Moves
Thermal energy moves from warmer objects or regions to cooler ones.

The three main transfer processes are:

- **Conduction** through direct contact, such as a hot pan warming a metal spoon
- **Convection** through moving air or liquid, such as warm water rising in a pot
- **Radiation** through electromagnetic waves, such as sunlight warming a surface

In most real systems, more than one process happens at once. The scientific job is to identify which process best explains the evidence you observe.

## Worked Example 1: Conduction in the Cup Test
Why does the metal cup lose heat quickly?

A strong explanation is:

"The metal cup allows thermal energy to transfer efficiently by conduction from the hot water to the cup and then to the surrounding air."

Evidence:

- the outside of the metal cup feels warm quickly
- the water temperature drops faster than in the other cups

Reasoning:

Those observations suggest energy is moving out of the water efficiently. Metal is a good conductor, so it transfers energy more quickly than the paper or insulated container.

## Worked Example 2: Convection in Air
A classroom heater is near the floor, but warm air is soon felt higher in the room.

What process best explains this?

A strong answer is:

"Convection explains the pattern because warmer air rises and cooler air sinks."

This is a system explanation:

- air near the heater gains thermal energy
- that air becomes less dense and rises
- cooler air moves in to replace it
- the circulation spreads heat through the room

## Worked Example 3: Radiation and Surface Heating
Two trays are placed in sunlight for the same amount of time. One is painted black and one is covered with reflective foil.

Afterward, the black tray is much hotter.

What is the best explanation?

"Radiation from sunlight transferred energy to both trays, but the black surface absorbed more of that energy while the reflective surface sent more energy away."

This example shows that material and surface properties affect how a system responds to radiant energy.

## Worked Example 4: Evaluating an Insulation Design
A student wants to design a lunch container that keeps food warm longer.

Choice A uses a thin metal wall.

Choice B uses a double wall with trapped air between layers.

Which design is better and why?

A strong claim is:

"Choice B is better because trapped air slows conduction and can reduce overall heat transfer."

This is an evidence-based design explanation. The student is not only naming a material. The student is connecting the structure of the system to how energy moves.

## Common Mistake: Saying Heat Is a Substance That Gets Trapped
Students often say things like:

"The heat stays inside because the cup traps it."

That language can hide the real science. A stronger explanation asks how the design changes energy transfer.

Use this method:

1. identify the warmer and cooler parts of the system
2. decide whether conduction, convection, or radiation best explains the pattern
3. explain how the material or design speeds up or slows down transfer

## Summary
Energy and heat are best understood by tracking how thermal energy moves through a system.

Key ideas from this lesson:

1. thermal energy moves from warmer places to cooler ones
2. conduction, convection, and radiation explain different transfer patterns
3. materials and designs can increase or reduce heat transfer

When you explain a heat phenomenon, do not stop at "it got hotter" or "it cooled down." Explain where the energy moved and what process caused that change.
