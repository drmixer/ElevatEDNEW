# Similarity & Congruence

## Learning Goal
- I can tell whether two figures are congruent or similar.
- I can use transformations to explain why figures are congruent.
- I can find a scale factor when figures are similar.

## Introduction
In middle-school geometry, two figures can be related in two different ways. Sometimes they are exactly the same size and shape. Other times they have the same shape, but one figure is a scaled version of the other.

That distinction matters because it changes what you are allowed to conclude. If figures are **congruent**, every matching side length and angle measure is the same. If figures are **similar**, the matching angles stay the same, but the side lengths change by a constant scale factor.

As you read, focus on two questions:

1. Does the transformation keep the size the same?
2. Do the corresponding side lengths match exactly or change by one constant ratio?

## Key Vocabulary
**Congruent**: Figures that have the same shape and the same size.

**Similar**: Figures that have the same shape, but not necessarily the same size.

**Corresponding parts**: Sides or angles that match in the same relative position in two figures.

**Transformation**: A movement of a figure, such as a translation, rotation, or reflection.

**Scale factor**: The number you multiply by to enlarge or reduce a figure.

**Dilation**: A transformation that changes the size of a figure by a scale factor.

## When Figures Are Congruent
Two figures are congruent when every pair of corresponding sides has the same length and every pair of corresponding angles has the same measure.

One way to justify congruence is with a rigid transformation:

- a **translation** slides a figure
- a **rotation** turns a figure
- a **reflection** flips a figure

These transformations do not change side length or angle measure. If one figure can be moved onto the other with a translation, rotation, or reflection, the figures are congruent.

## Worked Example: Showing Congruence With a Translation
Triangle `A` has vertices `(1, 2)`, `(4, 2)`, and `(1, 5)`.

Triangle `B` has vertices `(5, 2)`, `(8, 2)`, and `(5, 5)`.

We want to decide whether the triangles are congruent.

### Step 1: Compare the change from each point in Triangle A to the matching point in Triangle B.
- `(1, 2)` moves to `(5, 2)`
- `(4, 2)` moves to `(8, 2)`
- `(1, 5)` moves to `(5, 5)`

Each point moves `4` units to the right.

### Step 2: Name the transformation.
Moving every point `4` units to the right is a **translation**.

### Step 3: Use the transformation to make a conclusion.
A translation is a rigid transformation, so it keeps lengths and angles the same.

That means Triangle `A` maps exactly onto Triangle `B`.

So, the triangles are **congruent**.

## When Figures Are Similar
Two figures are similar when:

- corresponding angles are equal
- corresponding side lengths are proportional

This means one figure can be made from the other by a dilation, sometimes followed by a translation, rotation, or reflection.

To check similarity with side lengths:

1. match the corresponding sides
2. write the side-length ratios in the same order
3. check whether every ratio is equal

If every ratio is the same, that common ratio is the scale factor.

## Worked Example: Finding a Scale Factor
Triangle `P` has side lengths `3`, `4`, and `5`.

Triangle `Q` has side lengths `6`, `8`, and `10`.

We want to decide whether the triangles are similar and, if they are, find the scale factor from Triangle `P` to Triangle `Q`.

### Step 1: Match corresponding sides.
- shortest sides: `3` and `6`
- middle sides: `4` and `8`
- longest sides: `5` and `10`

### Step 2: Write the ratios in the same order.
- `6 / 3 = 2`
- `8 / 4 = 2`
- `10 / 5 = 2`

### Step 3: Interpret the ratios.
All three ratios are equal to `2`.

That tells us the side lengths are proportional, so the triangles are **similar**.

The scale factor from Triangle `P` to Triangle `Q` is **2**.

### Step 4: State what the scale factor means.
Every side in Triangle `Q` is `2` times the length of the matching side in Triangle `P`.

## Common Mistake: Congruent Or Similar?
A common mistake is to see that two figures "look alike" and call them congruent.

Use this check:

- if the figures have the same shape **and** the same size, they are **congruent**
- if the figures have the same shape but one is enlarged or reduced, they are **similar**

Another common mistake is to compare side lengths in the wrong order. The ratios only help if the sides are truly corresponding.

For example, comparing `6 / 3` and `8 / 5` would not make sense if those sides do not match the same positions in the figures.

## Summary
Use these rules to decide which relationship two figures have:

- **Congruent** figures have equal corresponding sides and angles.
- **Similar** figures have equal corresponding angles and proportional corresponding sides.
- Translations, rotations, and reflections preserve congruence.
- A dilation changes size by a scale factor and is often used to explain similarity.

When you solve a geometry problem, first decide whether you are checking for an exact match or a constant scale change. That choice tells you whether to prove congruence or similarity.
