# Academic Vocabulary, Greek/Latin Roots

## Learning Goal
- I can break academic words into roots, prefixes, and suffixes.
- I can use word parts and context together to infer the meaning of an unfamiliar word.
- I can explain why one meaning guess is stronger than another.

## Introduction
By Grade 7, academic words appear in science, history, math, and literature all the time. Many of those words are built from Greek and Latin parts that carry meaning across subjects.

When readers notice those parts, unfamiliar words stop looking random. They start looking like clues.

In this lesson, focus on three questions:

1. Which word parts already give me meaning?
2. How do those parts combine?
3. Does my predicted meaning fit the sentence?

## Key Vocabulary
**Root**: The core part of a word that carries its main meaning.

**Prefix**: A word part added to the beginning of a word.

**Suffix**: A word part added to the end of a word.

**Infer**: To make a strong meaning guess based on evidence.

**Context**: The surrounding words and ideas that help explain meaning.

## Word Focus: Common Academic Roots
Here are some roots that appear often in Grade 7 academic vocabulary:

- **struct** = build
- **chron** = time
- **graph** = write or record
- **dict** = say
- **bio** = life

You can spot these roots inside larger words:

- **construct** = build together
- **chronological** = related to time order
- **graphing** = recording or showing information
- **contradict** = speak against
- **biology** = the study of life

Knowing the root does not always give the perfect final definition, but it gives readers a strong starting point.

## How Skilled Readers Infer Meaning
When you meet a new word, a strong process looks like this:

1. break the word into meaningful parts
2. recall what those parts suggest
3. test the predicted meaning in the sentence
4. revise if the context points in a different direction

This process is stronger than guessing from only one clue.

For example, if you see the word **chronological**, the root **chron** points to time. The suffix **ical** tells you the word is describing something. A strong first guess is "arranged by time."

Then context helps confirm whether that guess works.

## Model Analysis: Using Roots and Context Together
Read this sentence:

"The museum guide arranged the events in chronological order so visitors could follow the history clearly."

A strong meaning prediction for **chronological** is:

"arranged in time order"

Why is that prediction strong?

- **chron** points to time
- the sentence mentions "events" and "history"
- "order" confirms that the word describes sequence

A weak prediction would be "important" because that does not connect to the root or to the idea of sequence in the sentence.

## Worked Example 1: Contradict
Read this sentence:

"The second source seemed to contradict the first article's claim about when the bridge was built."

Break apart the word:

- **contra** = against
- **dict** = say

Strong inference:

"Contradict means to say or show the opposite of another claim."

That fits both the word parts and the sentence, which compares two sources that do not match.

## Worked Example 2: Biodegradable
Read this sentence:

"The packaging was labeled biodegradable, which meant it would break down naturally over time."

Break apart the word:

- **bio** = life
- **de** = down or away
- **grade** relates to step or break down in this word family
- **able** = capable of

Strong inference:

"Biodegradable means able to break down naturally through living processes."

The context phrase "break down naturally" helps sharpen the meaning beyond what the word parts alone provide.

## Common Mistake: Trusting Only One Part
Sometimes students notice one familiar root and stop too early.

Example:

"Graphical must mean only math because graphs are in math class."

That is too narrow.

A stronger explanation is:

"Graphical usually means related to drawing, recording, or visual display, depending on the context."

This is better because it:

- uses the broader root meaning
- stays open to different subjects
- avoids locking into one guess too soon

## When Context Should Change Your First Guess
Word parts can point you in the right direction, but context helps refine the answer.

Suppose a reader sees the word **construction** in two sentences:

1. "The construction of the bridge took three years."
2. "The poem's construction creates a feeling of tension."

In both cases, **struct** still connects to building. But the context changes what is being built:

- a physical structure in the first sentence
- the design of a text in the second sentence

Strong readers stay flexible enough to adjust the meaning to the context.

## Summary
Academic vocabulary becomes easier when you treat words like evidence.

The key ideas from this lesson are:

1. roots, prefixes, and suffixes offer clues about meaning
2. context confirms, narrows, or corrects your first inference
3. the strongest meaning guess explains both the word parts and the sentence

When you meet an unfamiliar academic word, do not guess randomly. Break it apart, test the clues, and choose the meaning that best fits the context.
