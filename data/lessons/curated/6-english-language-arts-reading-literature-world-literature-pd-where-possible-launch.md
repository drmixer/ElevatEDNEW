# World Literature (PD where possible)

## Learning Goal
- I can identify how setting and cultural details shape a story.
- I can explain how a character's perspective changes across an excerpt.
- I can support a theme using details from the text.

## Introduction
World literature often shows readers a particular place, tradition, or community. Strong readers notice those details and ask what larger idea the story is developing through them.

In this lesson, you will read a short excerpt about a student helping at a festival market. As you read, focus on three questions:

1. Which details matter most in the setting?
2. How does the character feel at the beginning, and how does that change?
3. What idea does the story develop through that change?

## Key Vocabulary
**Setting**: The time and place of a story.

**Perspective**: A character's way of seeing and understanding events.

**Tradition**: A practice passed down over time.

**Motif**: A repeated image or detail that helps build meaning.

**Theme**: An important idea or message in a story.

## Text Focus: Literary Excerpt
Read the excerpt below. The paragraph numbers will help you point to the strongest evidence.

### Excerpt from "Lantern Evening"
**Paragraph 1.** When Mei arrived at the market square, her grandfather was already hanging paper lanterns above the food stalls. The square smelled like broth, oranges, and wet stone after the afternoon rain. Mei set her homework folder under the table and promised herself she would help for only a little while.

**Paragraph 2.** Every year, the first lanterns were lit in silence before the music began. Mei used to like that part. Now she mostly noticed how long it took. She still had math problems to finish before morning.

**Paragraph 3.** "Carry these cups to the tea stand," her grandfather said. As Mei crossed the square, neighbors greeted her by name. At the tea stand, Mrs. Lin pointed up to a row of painted lanterns. "Your aunt made those during the year of the flood," she said. "People still remember them."

**Paragraph 4.** Mei looked up. The lanterns were not perfect. One leaned slightly to one side, and another had a brush line you could still see in the painted river. But for the first time that evening, the square did not feel slow to her. It felt full of many years at once.

**Paragraph 5.** When her grandfather asked if she needed to leave, Mei touched the edge of her homework folder and pushed it farther back. "I can stay through the music," she said.

## Tracking the Character's Perspective
At first, Mei sees the market as an interruption.

Evidence:

- she plans to help for only a little while
- she keeps thinking about her homework
- she notices how long the tradition takes

These details show that she is present in the setting, but not fully connected to it.

By the end of the excerpt, her perspective changes.

Evidence:

- the square feels "full of many years at once"
- she pushes her homework folder farther back
- she chooses to stay longer

Those details show that she begins to value the event differently.

## Setting Details and Theme
The setting details help build the meaning of the story:

- the paper lanterns
- the market square after rain
- neighbors greeting Mei
- the memory of the flood-year lanterns

These details show that the market is not only a place where people buy things. It is also a place of memory and community.

A strong theme is:

"Traditions can help people feel connected to something larger than themselves."

## Model Analysis: Best Evidence for Theme
Suppose the question is:

"Which detail best supports the idea that Mei begins to value the tradition?"

A strong choice is this line from Paragraph 4:

"It felt full of many years at once."

Why is this strong evidence?

- It shows a change in Mei's thinking.
- It connects the market to memory and history.
- It helps explain why she chooses to stay.

A weaker choice would be the description of the wet stone square in Paragraph 1. That helps build the setting, but it does not show her changing perspective as directly.

## Common Mistake: Naming the Setting Without Interpreting It
A weak response might say:

"The story takes place at a market with lanterns."

That identifies the setting, but it does not explain what the setting does in the story.

A stronger response explains how the setting helps develop the theme.

## Summary
When you read world literature:

1. notice the important setting and cultural details
2. track how the character responds to them
3. identify the perspective shift
4. connect the details to a larger theme

In this lesson, the market mattered because it changed how Mei saw her evening. It stopped feeling like a delay and began to feel like part of a larger shared story.
