# Essays, Research, Argumentative Writing, Grammar & Usage

## Learning Goal
- I can write a focused claim that answers a real question.
- I can choose credible evidence and explain how it supports my claim.
- I can revise sentences so the argument sounds precise, formal, and easy to follow.

## Introduction
Argument writing in Grade 7 asks you to do more than state an opinion. You need to investigate a question, select trustworthy evidence, and guide the reader through your reasoning step by step.

In this lesson, you will work with the question:

Should the city convert an empty lot into a community park?

As you read, pay attention to three parts of a strong argument:

1. the claim
2. the evidence
3. the reasoning that connects them

## Key Vocabulary
**Claim**: A clear position the writer wants the reader to accept.

**Credible**: Believable and trustworthy.

**Evidence**: Facts, examples, quotations, or data that support a claim.

**Reasoning**: The explanation that shows how the evidence supports the claim.

**Formal tone**: A clear, respectful style that avoids slang and vague wording.

## What Makes a Grade 7 Argument Strong?
A strong argument does not simply sound confident. It makes a specific point and supports that point with evidence that fits the question.

Compare these claims:

- Weak: "Parks are good."
- Stronger: "The city should convert the empty lot into a community park because the space could improve neighborhood safety and give families a shared place to gather."

The stronger claim works better because it:

- takes a clear position
- gives reasons that can be supported
- sets up what kind of evidence will matter

The claim should guide the entire paragraph or essay. If the claim is blurry, the rest of the writing will also feel blurry.

## Choosing Credible Evidence
Suppose a writer is building the claim that a community park would benefit the neighborhood.

Which detail is stronger?

1. "A city report found that well-maintained public spaces can reduce litter and increase neighborhood use."
2. "My cousin said parks make places feel nicer."

The first detail is stronger because it comes from a more credible source and connects directly to community impact.

When you choose evidence, ask:

- Is this source trustworthy?
- Does this detail directly support my claim?
- Will the reader understand why this evidence matters?

If the evidence is only loosely connected, it will not carry much weight.

## Model Paragraph: Claim, Evidence, Reasoning
Read this argument paragraph:

"The city should convert the empty lot into a community park because the space could make the neighborhood safer and more active. A city report found that clean, well-used public spaces often reduce litter and attract more positive community activity. This evidence supports the claim because it shows that the lot would not just look better. It could change how people use the area and help the neighborhood feel more cared for."

Notice the structure:

- **Claim**: The city should convert the empty lot into a community park.
- **Evidence**: A city report links well-used public spaces to stronger neighborhood conditions.
- **Reasoning**: The writer explains how that evidence supports the benefit of the park.

The paragraph is effective because the writer does not stop after adding a fact. The writer interprets the fact for the reader.

## From Research Notes to a Draft
Writers often begin with notes, not polished sentences.

Imagine these research notes:

- empty lot currently unused
- residents say the area feels neglected
- city report connects maintained public spaces to stronger community use
- nearby schools and families need outdoor gathering space

Those notes are useful, but they are not yet an argument.

A stronger draft turns them into connected sentences:

"The city should turn the empty lot into a community park because the space is currently wasted and could serve neighborhood families. Residents already describe the area as neglected, and a city report explains that maintained public spaces can increase positive community use. Together, these details suggest that a park could improve both the appearance and function of the neighborhood."

The draft does not copy the notes. It organizes them around one controlling idea.

## Grammar Move: Make Relationships Clear
Grammar matters because argument writing depends on relationships between ideas.

Weak version:

"The lot is empty. The neighborhood needs a gathering place. A report talks about public spaces. The park is a good idea."

This version sounds choppy because the connections are missing.

Stronger version:

"Because the lot is currently unused and the neighborhood needs a gathering place, the city has a strong reason to create a park. A city report about public spaces strengthens that argument by showing that maintained community areas can encourage safer, more positive use."

The second version improves the writing by:

- combining related ideas
- showing cause and effect
- making the reasoning easier to follow

Useful sentence patterns include:

- "This evidence suggests that..."
- "Because ___, the reader can conclude that..."
- "This detail strengthens the claim by showing..."

## Addressing a Counterclaim
Strong argument writing also recognizes that another side exists.

A counterclaim might be:

"The city should not build the park because the money could be spent on road repairs instead."

A thoughtful response could be:

"Although road repairs also matter, the park proposal addresses a different community need by improving an unused space and creating a place for families to gather."

This makes the writing sound more balanced. It shows the writer understands the issue instead of pretending there is no disagreement.

## Revising for Precision and Formal Tone
Vague wording weakens an argument.

Weak sentence:

"The park would be super good and help a lot of stuff."

Stronger sentence:

"The park could improve neighborhood use, provide shared outdoor space, and make the area feel better maintained."

The revision is stronger because it replaces weak words like "super" and "stuff" with precise language the reader can evaluate.

When revising, look for words such as:

- good
- bad
- nice
- things
- a lot

Then replace them with specific nouns and verbs.

## Summary
Grade 7 argument writing works best when the writer builds a clear chain:

- claim
- credible evidence
- reasoning

Grammar and usage matter because they help the reader follow that chain without confusion.

Before you finish a paragraph or essay, ask:

1. Is my claim specific and arguable?
2. Is my evidence credible and relevant?
3. Have I explained how the evidence supports the claim?
4. Do my sentences sound precise and formal enough for academic writing?

If one of those parts is weak, the whole argument becomes less convincing.
