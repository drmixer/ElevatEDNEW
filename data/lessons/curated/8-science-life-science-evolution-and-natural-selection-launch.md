# Evolution & Natural Selection

## Learning Goal
- I can explain how variation and selection can change populations over time.
- I can use evidence from traits and environments to support a claim about natural selection.
- I can distinguish between changes in individuals and changes in populations.

## Introduction
Evolution does not mean that one organism decides to change because it wants to survive. Evolution describes how populations change over many generations when some inherited traits help organisms survive and reproduce more successfully in a particular environment.

To understand natural selection, scientists track variation, environment, and reproductive success together. In this lesson, ask:

1. What variation exists in the population?
2. How does the environment affect which traits are more successful?
3. How can that pattern shift the population over time?

## Key Vocabulary
**Variation**: Differences in traits among individuals in a population.

**Natural selection**: The process in which individuals with advantageous inherited traits are more likely to survive and reproduce.

**Population**: A group of the same species living in the same area.

**Adaptation**: An inherited trait that improves survival or reproduction in a specific environment.

**Trait**: A characteristic of an organism.

**Environment**: The surrounding conditions in which organisms live.

## Phenomenon: Darker Insects Become More Common
In one forest region, students compare insect populations before and after a wildfire darkens large sections of tree bark. They observe:

- the insect population includes both lighter and darker individuals
- birds prey on insects resting on the bark
- several generations after the fire, darker insects make up a larger share of the population

The question is:

How can a change in the environment influence which traits become more common?

## How Natural Selection Works
Natural selection depends on several linked ideas:

- individuals in a population show variation
- some of that variation is inherited
- the environment creates different survival or reproduction pressures
- individuals with helpful inherited traits may leave more offspring
- over time, the population's trait distribution can shift

This is a population-level process. Individual insects do not choose to become darker. Instead, darker insects may become more common if they are more likely to survive and reproduce.

## Evidence Matters in Evolution
Scientists support evolutionary claims with several kinds of evidence, such as:

- trait patterns in populations
- fossil sequences showing change over time
- anatomical similarities
- distribution of organisms in different environments

For this lesson, the key evidence pattern is population change linked to environmental conditions.

## Worked Example 1: Explaining the Bark Camouflage Pattern
What is a strong explanation for darker insects becoming more common after the wildfire?

"Darker insects may have been harder for birds to see on the darker bark, so they were more likely to survive and reproduce, causing the darker trait to become more common over generations."

This explanation is strong because it includes:

- the variation in the population
- the environmental change
- the survival advantage
- the population shift over time

## Worked Example 2: Individual Change or Population Change?
Compare these two claims:

- "An insect darkened its body because the bark became darker."
- "Darker insects already in the population became more common after the environment changed."

Which claim matches natural selection?

"The second claim matches natural selection."

Reasoning:

Natural selection changes the population over generations. It does not say that individual organisms intentionally change their inherited traits because they need to.

## Worked Example 3: Trait Advantage Depends on Environment
Suppose a population of small mammals includes both thicker-furred and thinner-furred individuals. A long period of colder winters begins.

What is a reasonable prediction?

"If thicker fur improves survival in colder conditions and the trait is inherited, thicker-furred individuals may become more common over generations."

This example matters because an adaptation is not universally "better." It is advantageous in a specific environment.

## Common Mistake: Thinking Organisms Evolve Because They Try
Students often say:

"The population evolved thicker fur because it needed to stay warm."

That wording hides the mechanism.

A stronger method is:

1. identify the existing variation
2. describe the environmental pressure
3. explain how differential survival or reproduction changes the population over generations

## Summary
Evolution by natural selection explains how populations change when inherited variation interacts with environmental pressures.

Key ideas from this lesson:

1. natural selection acts on inherited variation in a population
2. environmental conditions can make some traits more advantageous than others
3. the result is a shift in the population over many generations, not a need-driven change in one individual

When you analyze an evolution question, ask what variation exists, what the environment favors, and how that could change the population over time.
