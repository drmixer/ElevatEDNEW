# Cells & Organelles

## Learning Goal
- I can explain how organelles support cell function.
- I can connect a cell's structure to the work it performs.
- I can use evidence about organelles to support a claim about a cell.

## Introduction
Cells are living systems made of specialized parts. Each organelle has a job, and the cell functions because those parts work together.

In this lesson, ask:

1. What jobs do organelles perform?
2. How does structure connect to function?
3. How do organelles support the whole cell system?

## Key Vocabulary
**Cell**: The basic unit of life.

**Organelle**: A specialized structure inside a cell.

**Nucleus**: The organelle that stores genetic information in many cells.

**Cell membrane**: The boundary that controls what enters and leaves the cell.

**Mitochondrion**: The organelle that helps release usable energy.

**Chloroplast**: The organelle in plant cells that captures light energy.

## Phenomenon: Energy Needs in Different Cells
Scientists compare different cells and find that muscle cells contain many mitochondria. They also observe:

- muscle cells need large amounts of energy
- plant cells contain chloroplasts
- all cells need boundaries that regulate transport

The question is:

How does a cell's structure relate to the work it must do?

## Organelles Have Specific Jobs
Important organelles include:

- the **nucleus**, which stores instructions
- the **cell membrane**, which regulates movement of materials
- the **mitochondria**, which support energy release
- the **chloroplasts**, which support photosynthesis in plant cells

Cells work well because these parts perform specialized tasks inside one larger system.

## Structure Relates to Function
If a cell has a particular job, its structure often helps support that job.

Examples:

- muscle cells need many mitochondria because movement requires energy
- plant cells need chloroplasts because they capture light energy
- the cell membrane matters because cells must control what enters and leaves

## Worked Example 1: Why Muscle Cells Need Mitochondria
What is a strong explanation?

"Muscle cells contain many mitochondria because they need a lot of usable energy for repeated movement."

## Worked Example 2: Plant or Animal Cell?
Suppose a cell contains chloroplasts and a cell wall.

What is a reasonable claim?

"It is most likely a plant cell."

Reasoning:

- chloroplasts support photosynthesis
- plant cells commonly have a cell wall

## Worked Example 3: Thinking of the Cell as a System
Suppose the cell membrane is badly damaged.

Why would that affect the whole cell?

"The membrane controls what enters and leaves the cell, so damage can disrupt the entire system."

## Common Mistake: Memorizing Names Without Explaining Jobs
Students sometimes stop at:

"Mitochondria equals energy."

A stronger method is:

1. identify the organelle
2. explain its job
3. connect that job to the needs of the whole cell

## Summary
Cells function because organelles perform specialized tasks and work together.

Key ideas from this lesson:

1. organelles have different jobs
2. cell structure connects to cell function
3. strong biology explanations connect cell parts to the work of the whole system

When you study a cell, ask what each structure does and how it supports the cell's life.
