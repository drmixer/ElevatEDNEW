# Kinematics & Forces

## Learning Goal
- I can use changes in motion to infer whether forces are balanced or unbalanced.
- I can explain how net force affects an object's speed or direction.
- I can use evidence from a motion situation or graph to support a force explanation.

## Introduction
Objects do not change motion without a reason. If a skateboard speeds up, slows down, or turns, the forces acting on it are not balancing in the same way they were before.

Kinematics describes motion. Force explanations tell why the motion changes. Those ideas work best together when you use evidence from speed, direction, and graphs instead of relying on guesswork.

In this lesson, ask:

1. What does the object's motion tell me?
2. Are the forces balanced or unbalanced?
3. What evidence supports my explanation?

## Key Vocabulary
**Motion**: A change in an object's position over time.

**Force**: A push or pull on an object.

**Net force**: The overall force on an object after combining all forces acting on it.

**Balanced forces**: Forces that combine to make a net force of zero.

**Unbalanced forces**: Forces that create a nonzero net force.

**Acceleration**: A change in speed, direction, or both.

## Phenomenon: A Cart on a Track
During a class investigation, a cart moves along a straight track. Scientists observe three trials:

- Trial 1: the cart rolls at a steady speed
- Trial 2: the cart starts steady, then speeds up
- Trial 3: the cart starts steady, then slows down after a rough patch on the track

The question is:

What do these motion changes reveal about the net force in each trial?

## Motion Evidence and Net Force
A force explanation should start with the motion evidence.

If an object:

- keeps the same speed and direction, the net force is zero
- speeds up, slows down, or changes direction, the net force is not zero

This does not mean no forces are present when motion stays steady. It means the forces balance overall.

That distinction matters:

- balanced forces can still act on an object
- unbalanced forces are what change the motion

## Worked Example 1: Steady Motion
A hockey puck glides across smooth ice at a constant speed in a straight line.

What does this suggest about net force?

A strong answer is:

"The puck's net force is approximately zero because its motion is not changing."

Reasoning:

- the puck is still moving
- but its speed and direction stay the same
- that means the forces are balanced overall

Students sometimes think motion itself proves a force must be pushing in the direction of travel. The stronger explanation focuses on change in motion, not motion alone.

## Worked Example 2: Speeding Up
A cart moves to the right and then begins to speed up to the right.

What can you infer?

A strong claim is:

"There is an unbalanced net force acting to the right."

Evidence:

- the cart's speed increases
- the change happens in the same direction as the motion

Reasoning:

Because the cart is accelerating, the forces cannot be balanced. A net force to the right best explains why the cart speeds up in that direction.

## Worked Example 3: Reading a Motion Graph
Suppose a distance-time graph for a runner looks like this:

- from `0` to `4` seconds, the line is straight and steep
- from `4` to `8` seconds, the line is still straight but less steep

What does the graph show?

A strong explanation is:

"The runner is still moving forward during both intervals, but the runner is moving more slowly during the second interval."

How does that connect to forces?

If the runner slowed down during that change, some unbalanced force acted opposite the motion during the transition. The graph provides evidence of a motion change, and the motion change supports a net-force explanation.

## Worked Example 4: Balanced or Unbalanced?
Imagine two students pull on a box in opposite directions with equal strength, and the box does not change its motion.

What is the best explanation?

"The forces are balanced, so the net force is zero."

This is true whether the box stays at rest or keeps moving at a constant velocity. What matters is that its motion does not change.

## Common Mistake: Thinking Motion Requires an Unbalanced Force
Students often say:

"If an object is moving, there must be an unbalanced force in the direction of motion."

That is incorrect.

A stronger rule is:

1. if motion stays constant, net force is zero
2. if motion changes, net force is not zero
3. use the direction of the change to infer the direction of the net force

The science explanation should always match the motion evidence.

## Summary
Kinematics and force work together because motion evidence reveals how forces are acting.

Key ideas from this lesson:

1. balanced forces do not change an object's motion
2. unbalanced forces cause acceleration
3. speed and direction changes are the evidence to examine first

When you explain a force situation, do not start by guessing the force. Start with the motion pattern, then infer the net force from the evidence.
