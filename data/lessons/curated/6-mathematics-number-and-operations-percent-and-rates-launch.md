# Percent & Rates

## Learning Goal
- I can explain percent as a rate out of `100`.
- I can find a percent of a quantity using fractions or decimals.
- I can use unit rates to compare prices and speeds.

## Introduction
Percent and rate problems compare two quantities.

A percent compares a number to `100`. A rate compares quantities with different units, such as miles per hour or dollars per notebook.

These ideas show up often in real life:

- store discounts
- test scores
- unit prices
- speed
- recipes

In Grade 6, the goal is not just to compute. It is to understand what the comparison means.

In this lesson, focus on three questions:

1. What does a percent tell me?
2. How do I find a part of a whole using percent?
3. How can a unit rate help me compare choices?

## Key Vocabulary
**Percent**: A rate out of `100`.

**Rate**: A comparison of two quantities with different units.

**Unit rate**: A rate for `1` unit of the second quantity.

**Whole**: The full amount being compared.

**Part**: A portion of the whole.

**Equivalent ratio**: A ratio that names the same comparison in a different form.

## Percent Means "Out of 100"
The symbol `%` means "out of `100`."

Examples:

- `25%` means `25/100`
- `60%` means `60/100`
- `8%` means `8/100`

That means a percent can be written as:

- a fraction
- a decimal
- a rate per `100`

For example:

`25% = 25/100 = 0.25`

Once you understand that equivalence, many percent problems become easier.

## Worked Example 1: Finding a Percent of a Quantity
Find `25%` of `60`.

Step 1: Rewrite the percent.

`25% = 25/100 = 1/4`

Step 2: Find one-fourth of `60`.

`60 / 4 = 15`

So:

`25% of 60 = 15`

You could also use the decimal form:

`0.25 x 60 = 15`

Both methods show the same result.

## Worked Example 2: Finding the Whole From a Percent
A student answered `18` questions correctly, which was `90%` of the quiz. How many questions were on the quiz?

Step 1: Think about what `90%` means.

It means `90` out of `100`, or `0.9` of the whole.

Step 2: Write the equation.

`0.9x = 18`

Step 3: Solve for the whole.

`x = 18 / 0.9 = 20`

So the quiz had `20` questions.

Check:

`90% of 20 = 18`

## What a Rate Tells You
A rate compares quantities with different units.

Examples:

- `150` miles in `3` hours
- `$12` for `4` notebooks
- `45` heartbeats in `30` seconds

A unit rate tells how much goes with `1` unit.

That makes comparison easier because every choice is measured the same way.

## Worked Example 3: Finding a Unit Price
A pack of `6` markers costs `$9`.

Find the cost per marker.

Step 1: Write the rate.

`$9 for 6 markers`

Step 2: Divide both quantities by `6`.

`9 / 6 = 1.5`

So the unit rate is:

`$1.50 per marker`

This means each marker costs `$1.50` if the cost is shared evenly.

## Worked Example 4: Comparing Speeds
Which speed is greater:

- `180` miles in `4` hours
- `140` miles in `3` hours

Find each unit rate.

First speed:

`180 / 4 = 45`

So the rate is `45 miles per hour`.

Second speed:

`140 / 3 = 46 2/3`

So the rate is about `46.7 miles per hour`.

Compare:

`46.7 > 45`

So `140` miles in `3` hours is the greater speed.

## Connecting Percent and Rate
Percent is a special kind of rate because it compares a number to `100`.

Example:

If `30` out of `50` students prefer basketball, the fraction is:

`30 / 50 = 3 / 5`

Convert to percent:

`3 / 5 = 60 / 100 = 60%`

That means `60` students out of every `100` would represent the same comparison.

Seeing percent as a rate helps you move between fractions, decimals, and percents more confidently.

## Common Mistake: Forgetting the Whole
Students sometimes see:

"Find `20%` of `80`"

and compute:

`80 - 20 = 60`

That is not what the question asked.

The percent tells what fraction of the whole to take, not what number to subtract unless the problem says it is a discount or decrease.

To avoid mistakes:

1. identify the whole
2. rewrite the percent as a fraction or decimal
3. multiply to find the part
4. only subtract afterward if the problem asks for a remaining amount

## Summary
Percent and rate both compare quantities.

To solve these problems well:

1. remember that percent means "out of `100`"
2. rewrite percents as fractions or decimals when helpful
3. use multiplication to find a percent of a whole
4. find unit rates to compare prices, speeds, and other measurements fairly

When you know what is being compared and what the whole is, percent and rate problems become much easier to interpret and solve.
