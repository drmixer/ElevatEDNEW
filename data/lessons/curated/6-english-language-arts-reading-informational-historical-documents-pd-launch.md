# Historical Documents (PD)

## Learning Goal
- I can identify the main message of a historical document.
- I can use details from the text to explain the author's purpose.
- I can explain how the document's historical situation affects its meaning.

## Introduction
Historical documents were written for real people during important moments in the past. Strong readers do more than understand the words. They also ask why the document was written and what action or idea the author wanted readers to accept.

In this lesson, you will read an adapted community notice from a time of shortage. As you read, focus on three questions:

1. What is the document asking people to do?
2. Which details show the author's purpose?
3. How does the situation in the town help explain the message?

## Key Vocabulary
**Historical document**: A text created during a real time in history.

**Purpose**: The reason the author wrote the document.

**Audience**: The people the author wants to influence.

**Proclamation**: A public announcement by a leader or authority.

**Conserve**: To use something carefully so it lasts longer.

## Text Focus: Adapted Town Notice
Read the document below. The paragraph numbers will help you point to the strongest evidence.

### Adapted Historical Document: "Notice to River Street, 1918"
**Paragraph 1.** To the families and shopkeepers of River Street: Our town's water tank is lower than it has been at any time this summer. If we continue using water in the usual way, the town may not have enough for the school, clinic, and fire pump before the end of the month.

**Paragraph 2.** Beginning tomorrow, each household must limit outdoor water use to one evening every two days. Shopkeepers are asked to close public taps after sunset. These rules apply to every block and every household.

**Paragraph 3.** These changes are not meant to punish anyone. They are meant to protect the services every resident depends on. A careful town can endure a dry season. A careless town may place everyone at risk.

## Finding the Main Message
The document is not only reporting a problem. It is trying to guide the town's actions.

A strong statement of the main message is:

"The town must conserve water now so important services can continue during the dry period."

That answer works because it includes:

- the action the town should take
- the reason the action matters

A weaker answer would say only, "This document is about water." That gives the topic, but not the message.

## Model Analysis: Purpose and Evidence
Suppose the question is:

"Which detail best shows why the author wants residents to conserve water?"

A strong choice is this line from Paragraph 3:

"They are meant to protect the services every resident depends on."

Why is this strong evidence?

- It explains the reason for the new rules.
- It shows the author is thinking about the whole community.
- It makes the purpose clearer than a general warning alone.

A weaker choice would be "the water tank is lower." That shows the problem is real, but it does not explain the purpose as directly.

## Context Helps the Reader
The time and situation matter. If the town is facing a dry season and limited water, people may worry about daily needs such as school, health, and safety.

That context helps explain why the document uses:

- clear instructions
- a serious tone
- community-based language like "every resident depends on"

The author is trying to persuade readers that the rules are necessary, not random.

## Common Mistake: Summary Without Purpose
A weak response might say:

"The town has less water, so people should use less."

That summary is partly correct, but it leaves out the author's purpose.

A stronger response sounds like this:

"The document tells residents to conserve water so the town can protect important shared services. The author supports this purpose by explaining both the shortage and the risk to the whole community."

## Summary
When you read a historical document:

1. identify the main message
2. ask who the document addresses
3. choose details that reveal the author's purpose
4. use the historical situation to explain the message

In this lesson, the strongest reading move was noticing that the document was not only describing a problem. It was trying to persuade people to act together for the good of the town.
