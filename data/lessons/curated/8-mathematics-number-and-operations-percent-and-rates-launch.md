# Percent & Rates

## Learning Goal
- I can solve multi-step percent and rate problems in real-world contexts.
- I can interpret unit rate and rate of change from tables, graphs, equations, and situations.
- I can compare percent change and linear rate of change without confusing them.

## Introduction
In Grade 8, percent and rate problems connect strongly to algebra. You still work with tax, discount, and percent change, but you also analyze rates of change in tables, graphs, and equations.

That means you need to distinguish between two ideas:

- percent change, which compares a change to the original amount
- rate of change, which compares how one variable changes relative to another

Both ideas describe change, but they answer different questions.

In this lesson, focus on three questions:

1. Is the problem asking for percent change, a final amount, or a unit rate?
2. What quantities are being compared?
3. How can I use equations or tables to interpret the rate correctly?

## Key Vocabulary
**Percent change**: The amount of increase or decrease compared to the original value.

**Rate of change**: The ratio between the change in output and the change in input.

**Unit rate**: A rate for `1` unit.

**Markup**: An increase added to an original amount, often described as a percent.

**Discount**: A decrease subtracted from an original amount.

**Slope**: The constant rate of change in a linear relationship.

## Percent Change and Rate of Change Are Related but Different
Suppose a laptop price rises from `$500` to `$560`.

The change is:

`560 - 500 = 60`

The percent change is:

`60 / 500 = 0.12 = 12%`

That compares the change to the original price.

Now suppose a graph shows that a runner travels `6` miles every `1` hour.

That is a rate of change:

`6 miles per hour`

It compares two different quantities, not a change to an original whole.

## Worked Example 1: Multi-Step Percent Increase
A bicycle costs `$240`. The store adds an `8%` markup.

Find the new price.

Step 1: Find the markup amount.

`8% = 0.08`

`0.08 x 240 = 19.2`

So the markup is `$19.20`.

Step 2: Add the markup to the original price.

`240 + 19.2 = 259.2`

So the new price is:

`$259.20`

## Worked Example 2: Percent Decrease
A town used `18,500` gallons of water one month and `16,650` gallons the next month.

Find the percent decrease.

Step 1: Find the amount of decrease.

`18,500 - 16,650 = 1,850`

Step 2: Compare the decrease to the original amount.

`1,850 / 18,500 = 0.10`

Step 3: Convert to percent.

`0.10 = 10%`

So the water use decreased by `10%`.

## Worked Example 3: Finding Rate of Change From a Table
Study the table:

| Hours | Distance (miles) |
| --- | --- |
| 1 | 42 |
| 3 | 126 |
| 5 | 210 |

Step 1: Compare the changes.

From `1` to `3` hours:

- change in hours = `2`
- change in distance = `84`

Rate of change:

`84 / 2 = 42`

From `3` to `5` hours:

- change in hours = `2`
- change in distance = `84`

Again the rate is `42`.

So the constant rate of change is:

`42 miles per hour`

## Worked Example 4: Interpreting Rate of Change From an Equation
Consider the equation:

`y = 1.75x + 12`

Suppose `x` is the number of pounds of trail mix and `y` is the total cost in dollars.

Interpret the numbers.

`1.75` is the rate of change.

It means the cost increases by `$1.75` for each additional pound.

`12` is the initial value.

It means the total cost starts at `$12` before the per-pound amount is added.

If the problem asks for the unit rate, the answer is:

`$1.75 per pound`

## Worked Example 5: Comparing Two Situations
Plan A charges `$18` plus `$4` per visit.

Plan B charges `$6` per visit and no starting fee.

Which plan is cheaper for `5` visits?

Plan A:

`18 + 4(5) = 38`

Plan B:

`6(5) = 30`

So for `5` visits, Plan B is cheaper.

What if the question changes?

- Plan A has a lower rate of change
- Plan B has a lower initial value

This is why analyzing both numbers matters when comparing rates in linear situations.

## Choosing the Right Tool
When a problem includes percent or rate, ask:

1. Am I comparing a part to a whole?
2. Am I comparing two different units?
3. Am I comparing a change to the original amount?
4. Am I reading a constant rate from a table, graph, or equation?

Those questions help you choose the right calculation:

- multiply for finding a percent amount
- divide for a unit rate
- subtract first for percent change
- use slope for linear rate of change

## Common Mistake: Using Percent Change When the Problem Asks for Unit Rate
Students sometimes see a table of values and try to find percent change between rows even when the problem is about a constant rate.

Example:

If distance increases from `42` to `84` miles, that is a `100%` increase, but that does not tell you the speed unless you compare distance to time.

For speed, you need:

`distance / time`

That gives the unit rate.

Percent change and unit rate can both describe change, but they answer different questions.

## Summary
Grade 8 percent and rate work connects arithmetic to linear relationships.

To solve well:

1. decide whether the problem is about percent amount, percent change, or unit rate
2. compare the correct quantities
3. interpret slope as rate of change in linear situations
4. separate initial value from ongoing rate when comparing plans or equations

When you know what is being compared, the calculation becomes clearer and the interpretation becomes stronger.
