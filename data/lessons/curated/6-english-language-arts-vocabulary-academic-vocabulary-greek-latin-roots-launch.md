# Academic Vocabulary, Greek/Latin Roots

## Learning Goal
- I can identify common roots, prefixes, and suffixes in academic words.
- I can use word parts to make a reasonable meaning prediction.
- I can explain how word-part evidence supports my understanding of a new word.

## Introduction
Academic words can look difficult at first, but many of them are built from smaller parts that carry meaning. When readers know common Greek and Latin roots, prefixes, and suffixes, they can make stronger guesses about unfamiliar words.

In this lesson, you will study how word parts help unlock meaning. As you read, focus on three questions:

1. Which parts of the word already have meaning?
2. How do those parts work together?
3. What is the best reasonable meaning of the whole word in context?

## Key Vocabulary
**Root**: The main part of a word that carries core meaning.

**Prefix**: A word part added to the beginning of a word.

**Suffix**: A word part added to the end of a word.

**Academic vocabulary**: Words commonly used in school, reading, and formal writing.

**Context**: The words and ideas around a word that help explain its meaning.

## Word Focus: Breaking Apart Academic Words
Look at the words below and the word parts they contain.

- **transport** = **trans** ("across") + **port** ("carry")
- **inspection** = **in** ("into") + **spect** ("look") + **ion** ("act or process")
- **predictable** = **pre** ("before") + **dict** ("say") + **able** ("able to")
- **graphical** = **graph** ("write or draw") + **ic/al** ("related to")

These examples show that long words often become clearer when you break them into meaningful parts.

## How Word Parts Help Readers
When you meet a new academic word, you do not have to guess randomly.

A stronger method is:

1. find the root
2. notice the prefix and suffix
3. combine the meanings
4. check whether the result fits the sentence or passage

This method does not guarantee a perfect first guess every time, but it gives readers evidence to work from.

## Model Analysis: Using Roots to Predict Meaning
Suppose you see the word **inspection** in this sentence:

"Before the bridge opened, engineers completed a final inspection."

Start with the parts:

- **spect** = look
- **ion** = act or process

A strong meaning prediction is:

"Inspection means the act of looking closely or examining."

Why is this strong?

- It uses the root meaning.
- It fits the context of engineers checking a bridge.
- It is more precise than a vague guess like "something important."

## Worked Example 1: Predicting a Meaning
Suppose a student sees the word **transportable**.

What is a reasonable prediction?

"It probably means able to be carried or moved from one place to another."

Reasoning:

- **trans** means across
- **port** means carry
- **able** means able to

## Worked Example 2: Checking the Context
Suppose a reader predicts that **predictable** means "said before."

That is close, but can it be improved?

Yes.

A stronger explanation is:

"Predictable means something can be expected ahead of time."

That version uses the word parts and fits how the word is commonly used in context.

## Worked Example 3: Avoiding a Bad Guess
A student says:

"Graphical must mean something about math because graphs are in math."

That guess might work sometimes, but it is too narrow.

A stronger explanation is:

"Graphical means related to drawing, writing, or visual display, depending on the context."

This is better because it uses the word root rather than only one school subject connection.

## Common Mistake: Ignoring the Context
Word parts help, but context matters too.

If a reader uses parts only, they may make a rough guess that does not fully match the sentence.

A stronger method is:

1. use the word parts
2. test the guess in the sentence
3. revise if needed

## Summary
Greek and Latin roots help readers approach unfamiliar academic vocabulary with evidence instead of random guessing.

Key ideas from this lesson:

1. roots, prefixes, and suffixes each contribute meaning
2. word parts can help readers make strong predictions
3. context helps confirm or revise the prediction

When you meet a new academic word, break it apart, build a meaning from the parts, and then check whether that meaning fits the context.
