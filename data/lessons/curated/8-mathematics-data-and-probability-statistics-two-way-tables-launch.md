# Two-way Tables

## Learning Goal
- I can calculate relative frequencies from a two-way table.
- I can compare row and column relative frequencies to look for association between two variables.
- I can explain whether the data suggests a relationship between categories without overstating the conclusion.

## Introduction
By Grade 8, two-way tables become tools for analysis, not just organization. The main question is often no longer "What is the count?" but "Does one category appear to be related to another?"

To answer that well, you usually need relative frequencies.

If two groups are different sizes, counts alone can mislead you. Relative frequencies let you compare how common an outcome is within each group.

In this lesson, focus on three questions:

1. What are the row and column relative frequencies?
2. Do those relative frequencies look similar or different across groups?
3. Does the table suggest an association between the two variables?

## Key Vocabulary
**Two-way table**: A table that organizes data for two categorical variables.

**Relative frequency**: A count divided by a relevant total.

**Association**: A pattern showing that one variable may be related to another.

**Row relative frequency**: A relative frequency found by dividing by a row total.

**Column relative frequency**: A relative frequency found by dividing by a column total.

## From Counts to Analysis
Suppose a school surveyed students about whether they prefer digital notes or handwritten notes.

| Grade | Digital notes | Handwritten notes | Total |
| --- | ---: | ---: | ---: |
| 7 | 12 | 18 | 30 |
| 8 | 20 | 10 | 30 |
| Total | 32 | 28 | 60 |

At first glance, the counts already suggest a difference.

But the real question is:

Is note preference associated with grade level?

To answer that, compare relative frequencies within each grade.

## Worked Example 1: Row Relative Frequencies
Use the table above.

For Grade 7:

- digital notes: `12 / 30 = 0.4`
- handwritten notes: `18 / 30 = 0.6`

For Grade 8:

- digital notes: `20 / 30 = 2 / 3`
- handwritten notes: `10 / 30 = 1 / 3`

Interpretation:

- Grade 7 students are more likely to prefer handwritten notes.
- Grade 8 students are more likely to prefer digital notes.

Because the relative frequencies are noticeably different, the table suggests an association between grade level and note preference.

## Worked Example 2: Why Counts Alone Can Mislead
Suppose another survey gives this table.

| Group | Likes school lunch | Does not like school lunch | Total |
| --- | ---: | ---: | ---: |
| Group A | 24 | 16 | 40 |
| Group B | 18 | 12 | 30 |
| Total | 42 | 28 | 70 |

Which group is more likely to like school lunch?

If you look only at counts:

- Group A has `24`
- Group B has `18`

But compare relative frequencies:

- Group A: `24 / 40 = 0.6`
- Group B: `18 / 30 = 0.6`

The groups are equally likely to like school lunch.

This is why relative frequency is essential for fair comparison.

## Worked Example 3: Interpreting Association Carefully
Suppose a table shows whether students attend tutoring and whether they pass a quiz.

| Tutoring | Passed | Did not pass | Total |
| --- | ---: | ---: | ---: |
| Attended | 27 | 9 | 36 |
| Did not attend | 16 | 18 | 34 |
| Total | 43 | 27 | 70 |

Find the row relative frequency of passing:

- attended tutoring: `27 / 36 = 0.75`
- did not attend: `16 / 34` which is about `0.47`

The difference between `0.75` and `0.47` is substantial.

So the data suggests an association between attending tutoring and passing the quiz.

But be careful with the wording.

A strong conclusion is:

"Students who attended tutoring were more likely to pass in this data set."

A weak conclusion would be:

"Tutoring caused the passing scores."

The table shows association, not automatic proof of cause.

## Row vs Column Relative Frequencies
Sometimes row relative frequencies are more useful. Sometimes column relative frequencies are.

If the question is:

"Within each grade, which note style is more common?"

Use row relative frequencies.

If the question is:

"Of all students who prefer digital notes, what fraction are in Grade 8?"

Use column relative frequencies.

Strong data analysis starts by deciding which comparison the question actually asks for.

## Common Mistake: Saying There Is an Association from Small Count Differences Alone
Students sometimes compare only raw counts:

- Group A: `15`
- Group B: `18`

and decide there must be an association.

That conclusion may be weak if:

- the group sizes are different
- the relative frequencies are actually close

Always compare proportions before making a claim about association.

## Worked Example 4: A Table with No Clear Association
Suppose a survey asks whether students prefer working alone or in a group.

| Grade | Alone | Group | Total |
| --- | ---: | ---: | ---: |
| 7 | 14 | 16 | 30 |
| 8 | 15 | 15 | 30 |
| Total | 29 | 31 | 60 |

Row relative frequencies:

- Grade 7 alone: `14 / 30`
- Grade 8 alone: `15 / 30`

These are very close.

That means the table does not suggest a strong association between grade level and this preference.

Small differences can happen without indicating a meaningful pattern.

## Summary
Grade 8 two-way-table analysis depends on relative frequencies and careful interpretation.

To analyze a table well:

1. decide whether row or column relative frequencies fit the question
2. compare the proportions across groups
3. look for meaningful differences, not just different counts
4. describe association carefully without claiming automatic cause

Two-way tables become powerful when you stop treating them as simple count charts and start using them to evaluate whether categories appear related.
