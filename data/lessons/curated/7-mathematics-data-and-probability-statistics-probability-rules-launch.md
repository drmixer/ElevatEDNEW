# Probability Rules

## Learning Goal
- I can find the theoretical probability of a simple event from a sample space.
- I can compare theoretical probability to experimental probability.
- I can use probability values to predict long-run frequencies.

## Introduction
By Grade 7, probability becomes more formal. You are not only describing events as likely or unlikely. You are calculating exact probabilities, organizing sample spaces, and connecting probabilities to repeated trials.

A strong probability thinker moves between three ideas:

- the list of possible outcomes
- the numerical probability of an event
- the long-run frequency the probability predicts

In this lesson, focus on three questions:

1. How do I find theoretical probability from a sample space?
2. How is experimental probability different from theoretical probability?
3. What does a probability predict over many trials?

## Key Vocabulary
**Sample space**: The complete list of all possible outcomes.

**Theoretical probability**: Probability found by reasoning about equally likely outcomes.

**Experimental probability**: Probability based on collected data from trials.

**Long-run frequency**: The approximate number of times an event should occur over many trials.

**Favorable outcomes**: The outcomes that match the event you want.

## Building Probability from a Sample Space
Suppose a fair number cube is rolled once.

The sample space is:

`{1, 2, 3, 4, 5, 6}`

If the event is "rolling a prime number," the favorable outcomes are:

`{2, 3, 5}`

So the probability is:

`P(prime) = 3 / 6 = 1 / 2`

This works because all six outcomes are equally likely.

The general rule is:

`P(event) = favorable outcomes / total outcomes`

## Worked Example 1: Finding Theoretical Probability
A fair spinner has `8` equal sections:

- `3` red
- `2` blue
- `2` green
- `1` yellow

What is the theoretical probability of landing on green?

Step 1: Count the favorable outcomes.

There are `2` green sections.

Step 2: Count the total outcomes.

There are `8` sections.

Step 3: Write and simplify the probability.

`P(green) = 2 / 8 = 1 / 4`

Interpretation:

Green should occur about one-fourth of the time in many spins.

## Worked Example 2: Predicting Long-Run Frequency
If the probability of landing on green is `1 / 4`, about how many times should green occur in `60` spins?

Use the probability as a rate:

`1 / 4 x 60 = 15`

So green should occur about `15` times.

This is not a guarantee for exactly `60` spins. It is a prediction based on the theoretical probability.

That word "about" matters. Probability predicts patterns over many trials, not exact short-term results.

## Worked Example 3: Comparing Theoretical and Experimental Probability
A student flips a fair coin `40` times and gets `26` heads.

The theoretical probability of heads is:

`1 / 2`

The experimental probability is:

`26 / 40 = 13 / 20 = 0.65`

Compare:

- theoretical: `0.5`
- experimental: `0.65`

The experimental probability is higher than expected, but that does not prove the coin is unfair. It means this experiment produced more heads than average.

With many more trials, the experimental probability would usually move closer to `0.5`.

## Using Probability to Compare Events
Suppose you roll a fair number cube.

Compare these events:

- rolling a `1`: `1 / 6`
- rolling a number less than `5`: `4 / 6 = 2 / 3`
- rolling an odd number: `3 / 6 = 1 / 2`

Which is most likely?

The event with probability `2 / 3`.

Which is least likely?

The event with probability `1 / 6`.

Probability is useful because it lets us compare events quantitatively, not just by intuition.

## Common Mistake: Using Past Results to Change Theoretical Probability
Students sometimes say:

"A coin landed tails three times in a row, so heads is now more likely."

For a fair coin, that is not true.

The theoretical probability of heads on the next flip is still:

`1 / 2`

Past outcomes do not change the probability of the next independent trial.

This is an important difference between:

- noticing a short pattern
- understanding the actual structure of the experiment

## Worked Example 4: Two-Step Sample Space
Suppose a fair coin is flipped and then a fair number cube is rolled.

The sample space can be written as ordered outcomes:

- `H1, H2, H3, H4, H5, H6`
- `T1, T2, T3, T4, T5, T6`

There are `12` total outcomes.

What is the probability of getting tails and an even number?

Favorable outcomes:

- `T2`
- `T4`
- `T6`

So:

`P(tails and even) = 3 / 12 = 1 / 4`

Listing the sample space helps prevent missed outcomes.

## Summary
Grade 7 probability connects sample spaces, numerical probabilities, and long-run predictions.

To solve well:

1. identify the full sample space
2. count the favorable outcomes
3. calculate the probability
4. interpret what that probability means over many trials

Theoretical probability comes from the structure of the experiment. Experimental probability comes from collected results. Strong probability reasoning uses both without confusing one for the other.
