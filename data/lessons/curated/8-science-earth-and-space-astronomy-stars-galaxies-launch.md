# Astronomy: Stars & Galaxies

## Learning Goal
- I can explain how patterns in starlight help scientists infer star motion and composition.
- I can describe how gravity organizes stars into galaxies and galaxies into larger structures.
- I can use evidence from observations to support a scientific claim about stars or galaxies.

## Introduction
The night sky can look calm and fixed, but astronomy shows a universe full of motion, energy, and structure. Stars form, shine, and change over time. Galaxies rotate, cluster, and move relative to one another.

Because scientists cannot travel to most of these objects directly, astronomy depends on evidence gathered from light. In this lesson, ask:

1. What can starlight tell us about stars?
2. How does gravity shape galaxies?
3. How do astronomers use observations to make claims about objects far away?

## Key Vocabulary
**Spectrum**: The pattern of light separated by wavelength or color.

**Redshift**: A shift of light toward longer wavelengths, showing that an object is moving away.

**Blueshift**: A shift of light toward shorter wavelengths, showing that an object is moving closer.

**Galaxy**: A huge system of stars, gas, dust, and dark matter held together by gravity.

**Light-year**: The distance light travels in one year.

**Gravity**: The force that attracts masses to one another.

## Phenomenon: A Galaxy's Light Looks Stretched
Astronomers compare light from a distant galaxy with light patterns measured in a lab. They notice that several known spectral lines appear shifted toward the red end of the spectrum.

They also observe:

- the galaxy has a rotating spiral shape
- millions of stars are visible within it
- similar redshifts appear in many other distant galaxies

The question is:

What can these observations tell scientists about how galaxies move and how the universe is structured?

## What Light Reveals About Stars
Astronomers learn about stars by studying their light.

Important patterns include:

- color, which can suggest relative temperature
- brightness, which can help estimate size or distance when paired with other data
- absorption or emission lines, which reveal chemical elements
- wavelength shifts, which show motion toward or away from Earth

This matters because stars are too distant to sample directly. Light is the evidence scientists use to infer what the star is made of, how hot it is, and whether it is moving relative to us.

## How Gravity Organizes Galaxies
Galaxies are not random collections of stars. Gravity pulls matter together and helps keep the system organized.

Within a galaxy:

- stars orbit a common center
- gas and dust can collect into new star-forming regions
- the galaxy's overall shape depends on how matter is distributed and moving

On larger scales:

- galaxies can gather into clusters
- clusters can form even larger structures
- gravity shapes motion across those systems too

Astronomy is not only about individual stars. It is also about using evidence to explain how matter behaves across enormous scales.

## Worked Example 1: Interpreting a Spectrum
Suppose astronomers observe a star and identify hydrogen absorption lines in its spectrum. The lines match the hydrogen pattern from a lab, but they are shifted slightly toward the blue end of the spectrum.

What does the observation suggest?

A strong claim is:

"The star likely contains hydrogen and is moving toward Earth."

Evidence:

- the line pattern matches hydrogen
- the shift is toward shorter wavelengths

Reasoning:

The matching pattern identifies the element, while the blueshift shows motion toward the observer. This example matters because one set of light data can provide more than one kind of evidence.

## Worked Example 2: Why a Galaxy Stays Together
Students sometimes imagine that stars in a galaxy should simply fly apart. But observations show organized rotation and long-lasting structure.

A strong explanation is:

"Gravity holds the galaxy together by pulling stars and other matter toward the system's center and by shaping orbital motion."

This is stronger than saying only, "The stars stay close because they are in space together." The scientific explanation identifies the force and the mechanism.

## Worked Example 3: Using Redshift as Evidence
Astronomers compare many galaxies and find that most distant galaxies show redshifted spectral lines.

What is a reasonable claim?

"The galaxies are moving away from us."

Why?

- redshift means the observed wavelengths are stretched
- that shift is evidence of motion away from the observer

This does not mean every galaxy behaves exactly the same way, but it does show how astronomers use light patterns as evidence about large-scale motion in the universe.

## Common Mistake: Treating Pictures as the Only Evidence
Students often rely only on how an image looks:

"This galaxy looks like a spiral, so that tells us everything."

Images matter, but astronomy depends on more than pictures.

A stronger method is:

1. identify the observation, such as spectral lines, brightness, or shape
2. decide what each observation can actually support
3. connect the evidence to a claim about motion, composition, or structure

Scientific claims become stronger when they are tied to measured evidence, not just visual appearance.

## Summary
Astronomy uses light as evidence to explain objects and motion across the universe.

Key ideas from this lesson:

1. spectra can reveal a star's composition and relative motion
2. gravity organizes stars into galaxies and galaxies into larger systems
3. astronomers make claims by connecting observations to physical explanations

When you study stars and galaxies, ask not only what they look like, but also what their light and structure reveal.
