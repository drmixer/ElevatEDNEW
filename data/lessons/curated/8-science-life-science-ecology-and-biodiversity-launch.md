# Ecology & Biodiversity

## Learning Goal
- I can explain how changes in one part of an ecosystem can affect the whole system.
- I can use evidence to describe how biodiversity supports ecosystem resilience.
- I can make a claim about human impact on an ecosystem and support it with reasoning.

## Introduction
Ecosystems do not respond to disturbance in only one way. A drought, pollution event, or invasive species can alter populations, food webs, and the flow of matter through a system.

Grade 8 ecology asks you to think beyond a single chain reaction. You need to explain how evidence from the whole ecosystem supports a claim about stability, biodiversity, and human impact.

In this lesson, ask:

1. What pattern shows that the ecosystem changed?
2. How does that change spread through the system?
3. Why might biodiversity help the ecosystem recover or resist collapse?

## Key Vocabulary
**Ecosystem**: A community of organisms and their physical environment interacting as a system.

**Biodiversity**: The variety of living things in an ecosystem.

**Resilience**: The ability of a system to keep functioning or recover after disturbance.

**Invasive species**: A nonnative species that spreads and disrupts an ecosystem.

**Food web**: A model of interconnected feeding relationships in an ecosystem.

**Limiting factor**: A condition or resource that restricts the growth of a population.

## Phenomenon: A Forest After an Invasive Insect Outbreak
Scientists study a forest over several years after an invasive insect begins killing one common tree species. They observe:

- the number of that tree species drops sharply
- some birds that nested in those trees become less common
- sunlight reaches the forest floor more easily
- a few other plant species spread into the new open spaces
- soil dries out faster in some areas during summer

The forest does not disappear, but its structure and populations change.

The question is:

How does one disturbance create several linked changes across the ecosystem?

## How Ecosystem Change Spreads
Ecosystems are connected through habitat, food, water, competition, and cycles of matter.

If one key species declines, other changes can follow:

- some organisms lose food or shelter
- different organisms may expand into the open niche
- the flow of energy through the food web may shift
- physical conditions such as shade, moisture, or soil temperature can change

This is why scientists treat ecosystems as systems. A disturbance is not just one isolated event. It can change relationships across many parts of the environment.

## Worked Example 1: Tracing an Ecosystem Response
Suppose a wetland food web includes:

- aquatic plants
- snails
- small fish
- herons

If pollution causes the aquatic plants to decrease, what is one likely chain of effects?

A strong explanation is:

"Snail populations may decrease because they lose a food source, which can then reduce the number of small fish and make food less available for herons."

Reasoning:

- aquatic plants help support organisms at higher levels of the web
- if plant biomass drops, less energy is available to consumers
- the effect can spread upward through the ecosystem

This explanation is stronger than saying only, "The wetland becomes unhealthy." It names the mechanism.

## Worked Example 2: Why Biodiversity Matters
Compare two grasslands after a long dry period.

Grassland A has many plant species with different root depths and water needs.

Grassland B depends on only one dominant grass species.

Which grassland is more likely to stay productive during the drought?

A strong claim is:

"Grassland A is more likely to remain resilient because its biodiversity provides multiple ways for the ecosystem to keep functioning."

Evidence and reasoning:

- if one plant species struggles, others may still survive
- different species may use water differently or tolerate stress better
- the ecosystem is less dependent on one single species

Biodiversity does not guarantee no damage, but it can reduce the chance that one stress wipes out a major system function.

## Worked Example 3: Human Impact and Ecosystem Services
A city drains part of a marsh to build new parking lots. After several seasons, nearby neighborhoods experience more flooding after heavy rain.

How can ecology explain that pattern?

A strong answer is:

"The marsh likely helped absorb and slow water, so removing part of it reduced an ecosystem service and increased flooding risk."

This explanation matters because ecology is not only about wild organisms. It also helps explain how ecosystems benefit human communities through services such as water storage, pollination, and erosion reduction.

## Common Mistake: Treating Biodiversity Like Decoration
Students sometimes think biodiversity only means "more types of organisms are interesting to look at."

That misses the science.

A stronger method is:

1. identify how species contribute to food webs or habitat
2. ask what happens if one species is removed
3. explain how multiple species can help the system continue functioning

Biodiversity matters because it affects how stable and flexible the ecosystem can be under stress.

## Summary
Ecology at this level is about explaining systems, not just naming organisms.

Key ideas from this lesson:

1. disturbances can spread through ecosystems by changing food webs, habitat, and physical conditions
2. biodiversity can support resilience by providing multiple pathways for a system to function
3. human actions can weaken or strengthen ecosystem stability and services

When you analyze an ecosystem, look for the full pattern of change and explain how the parts of the system connect.
