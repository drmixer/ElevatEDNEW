# Volume & Surface Area

## Learning Goal
- I can distinguish between surface area and volume in three-dimensional figures.
- I can find the surface area of a prism using its faces or a net.
- I can find the volume of a prism and explain why the two measurements are different.

## Introduction
In Grade 7, solids often require two different types of measurement.

- **Surface area** tells how much material is needed to cover the outside.
- **Volume** tells how much space is inside.

These quantities sound related, but they answer different questions. A cereal box problem about cardboard uses surface area. A water tank problem about capacity uses volume.

In this lesson, focus on three questions:

1. When should I use surface area and when should I use volume?
2. How do I calculate each one correctly?
3. How can a net help me organize surface-area work?

## Key Vocabulary
**Surface area**: The total area of all the outside faces of a solid.

**Volume**: The amount of space inside a solid.

**Net**: A two-dimensional layout of the faces of a solid.

**Prism**: A solid with two matching parallel bases and rectangular side faces.

**Base area**: The area of one base of a solid figure.

## Surface Area and Volume Answer Different Questions
Suppose you have a rectangular prism.

If the question asks:

- How much wrapping paper is needed?

you need surface area.

If the question asks:

- How much sand can the box hold?

you need volume.

The formulas are different because the measurements represent different things.

## Worked Example 1: Surface Area of a Rectangular Prism
A rectangular prism has:

- length `5`
- width `3`
- height `2`

Find the area of each pair of matching faces.

Top and bottom:

`5 x 3 = 15`

There are `2` of these faces:

`2 x 15 = 30`

Front and back:

`5 x 2 = 10`

There are `2` of these:

`2 x 10 = 20`

Left and right:

`3 x 2 = 6`

There are `2` of these:

`2 x 6 = 12`

Add them:

`30 + 20 + 12 = 62`

So the surface area is:

`62 square units`

## Worked Example 2: Using a Net
You can also imagine the prism unfolded into a net.

The net shows all six faces as rectangles. Surface area is just the total area of that unfolded figure.

For the prism above, the net contains:

- two `5 x 3` rectangles
- two `5 x 2` rectangles
- two `3 x 2` rectangles

That gives the same calculation:

`2(15) + 2(10) + 2(6) = 62`

The net is helpful because it keeps you from forgetting a face.

## Worked Example 3: Volume of the Same Prism
Now find the volume of the prism with dimensions `5 x 3 x 2`.

Use:

`V = l x w x h`

`V = 5 x 3 x 2`

`V = 30`

So the volume is:

`30 cubic units`

Compare:

- surface area: `62 square units`
- volume: `30 cubic units`

The numbers and units are different because the measurements describe different things.

## Worked Example 4: Choosing the Correct Measure
A company is making a cardboard box with no lid. The box has:

- length `8`
- width `4`
- height `3`

Should you use surface area or volume to find how much cardboard is needed?

Use surface area, because cardboard covers the outside faces.

Because the box has no lid, you would include:

- the bottom
- four side faces

You would not include a top face.

This example shows why context matters. Geometry formulas should match the real situation.

## Common Mistake: Using Volume When the Problem Asks for Covering
Students often see a solid figure and immediately use `l x w x h`.

That only works when the problem asks for inside space.

If the question asks about:

- paint
- wrapping
- cardboard
- labels

then surface area is more likely the correct measure.

Read the context before choosing the formula.

## Summary
Grade 7 geometry asks you to work carefully with both surface area and volume.

To solve well:

1. decide whether the problem is about inside space or outside covering
2. use a net or the faces to organize surface area
3. use `length x width x height` for prism volume
4. label area in square units and volume in cubic units

Surface area and volume are both important, but they should never be confused. The context tells you which measure belongs in the problem.
