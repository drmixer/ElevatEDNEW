# Sampling & Inference

## Learning Goal
- I can evaluate whether a sample is likely to support a reasonable inference about a population.
- I can compare sample results and explain why different samples may lead to different conclusions.
- I can write an inference that reflects the evidence without claiming more certainty than the data supports.

## Introduction
By Grade 8, sampling and inference should feel more analytical. The question is not only whether a sample exists, but whether that sample gives trustworthy evidence about a population.

When students compare samples, they need to think about:

- how the sample was chosen
- how large it is
- whether the result is similar to other likely samples

Different samples from the same population can give different results. That does not mean sampling failed. It means inference always includes some uncertainty.

In this lesson, focus on three questions:

1. Does this sample support a reasonable inference?
2. Why might two samples from the same population give different results?
3. How can I describe uncertainty without making the inference useless?

## Key Vocabulary
**Population**: The entire group being studied.

**Sample**: A subset of the population used to collect data.

**Representative sample**: A sample that reflects the population reasonably well.

**Inference**: A conclusion about a population based on sample data.

**Variability**: Natural differences that can occur from one sample to another.

## Why Different Samples Can Give Different Results
Suppose a city wants to estimate how many residents use the public library each month.

One random sample of residents may show `42%` use the library.

Another random sample may show `47%`.

These results are not identical, but they can both be reasonable because samples vary.

This variability is a normal part of sampling. It does not automatically mean one sample is wrong.

The key question is whether the sample was chosen in a way that makes the inference believable.

## Worked Example 1: Evaluating a Sample
A school wants to estimate the percentage of students who support adding more clubs.

Which sample is better?

### Sample A
Ask `80` randomly selected students from the full student list.

### Sample B
Ask `80` students already attending club fair sign-ups.

Sample A is better for making an inference about the whole school.

Why?

- it is random
- it is less likely to overrepresent students already interested in clubs
- it is more representative of the full population

Sample B may be biased because club-interested students are more likely to support adding more clubs.

## Worked Example 2: Writing a Reasonable Inference
A random sample of `120` students is asked whether they would use a later bus route after school.

Results:

- yes: `78`
- no: `42`

The sample proportion supporting the route is:

`78 / 120 = 0.65`

A reasonable inference is:

"About `65%` of students at the school may support a later bus route."

This is stronger than saying:

"Exactly `65%` of students support the route."

The first statement respects sampling uncertainty. The second ignores it.

## Worked Example 3: Comparing Two Random Samples
Two random samples are taken from the same population of students.

### Sample 1
`56%` prefer digital textbooks.

### Sample 2
`61%` prefer digital textbooks.

Do these different percentages mean one sample must be wrong?

No.

Because of sample variability, two reasonable random samples can produce somewhat different results.

A strong interpretation is:

"Both samples suggest that more than half of students may prefer digital textbooks, even though the exact sample percentages differ."

This interpretation pays attention to the common pattern instead of demanding identical results.

## Sample Size Still Matters
Compare these two random samples:

### Sample A
`12` students surveyed

### Sample B
`200` students surveyed

Even if both are random, Sample B usually gives stronger evidence because it includes more information from the population.

That does not make Sample A useless, but it usually makes its inference less stable.

Larger random samples tend to produce more reliable estimates than very small random samples.

## Common Mistake: Treating an Inference Like Proof
Students sometimes move from sample data to a statement that sounds certain.

Example:

"Seventy percent of the sample liked the new menu, so the new menu is definitely popular with everyone."

That claim is too strong.

A better statement is:

"The sample provides evidence that the new menu may be popular with the larger group."

Inference should sound evidence-based, not absolute.

## Worked Example 4: Weak Inference from a Weak Sample
A community center wants to know whether families want more evening events.

The survey is posted only at a Saturday morning event for families already attending a program.

Why is this a weak basis for inference?

- it misses families who never attend that event
- it may overrepresent families already highly involved
- the sample is unlikely to reflect the full community

A weak sample can produce a weak inference even if the numbers look clear.

## Summary
Grade 8 sampling and inference depends on judging evidence carefully.

To evaluate an inference:

1. consider how the sample was selected
2. decide whether the sample is likely to represent the population
3. remember that different samples can produce somewhat different results
4. write conclusions that match the evidence without overstating certainty

Strong inference is not about pretending sample data is exact. It is about making the most reasonable conclusion the data can support.
