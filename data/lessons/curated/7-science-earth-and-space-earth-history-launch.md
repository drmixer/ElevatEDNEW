# Earth History

## Learning Goal
- I can explain how rocks and fossils provide evidence about Earth's past.
- I can place geologic events in relative order.
- I can use evidence from layers and fossils to support a claim about a past environment.

## Introduction
Earth's history is recorded in layers of rock, preserved fossils, and changes in the land. Scientists reconstruct that history by studying clues and placing events in order.

In this lesson, ask:

1. What clues record Earth's past?
2. How do scientists decide which events happened first?
3. What can fossils and layers reveal about ancient environments?

## Key Vocabulary
**Geologic record**: The preserved evidence of Earth's history found in rocks, fossils, and landforms.

**Fossil**: Preserved remains or traces of ancient life.

**Relative age**: Which event is older or younger without using exact dates.

**Deposition**: The building up of sediment in layers.

**Erosion**: The wearing away and movement of rock or sediment.

**Fault**: A break in Earth's crust where movement has occurred.

## Phenomenon: Fish Fossils in a Rock Layer
Scientists examine an area with several rock layers and find:

- fish fossils in one lower layer
- plant fossils in a higher layer
- a fault cutting across both layers

The question is:

How can scientists use this evidence to reconstruct the order of events and the area's environmental history?

## How Earth Records Change
The geologic record includes:

- rock layers formed over time
- fossils trapped in those layers
- cracks, tilting, and faults that happen later
- signs that erosion removed material before new layers formed

These clues matter because Earth changes over long periods, and the record helps scientists reconstruct those changes.

## Putting Events in Order
Scientists often begin with relative age.

Useful rules include:

- lower layers are usually older than higher layers
- a fault is younger than the layers it cuts through
- fossils can help show what kind of environment existed when a layer formed

This reasoning helps scientists build a sequence instead of guessing.

## Worked Example 1: Ordering Events
Suppose students observe:

- Layer A at the bottom
- Layer B above it
- a fault cutting both

What is the likely order?

"Layer A formed first, then Layer B, and then the fault happened."

## Worked Example 2: Using Fossils
Suppose a rock layer contains shell fossils, but the area is dry land today.

What is a reasonable claim?

"The area likely had a water-based environment when that layer formed."

Reasoning:

- shell fossils provide evidence about where the organisms lived
- the environment must have changed over time

## Worked Example 3: Reconstructing Change
Suppose a region shows river sediment at the bottom, shell-rich rock above it, and tilted layers today.

What story does the evidence support?

"The area changed from one environment to another over time, and later geologic forces tilted the layers."

## Common Mistake: Assuming the Land Always Looked the Same
Students sometimes think:

"If the place is dry now, it must always have been dry."

That ignores the geologic record.

A stronger method is:

1. identify the evidence in the rocks
2. place the events in order
3. explain what changed over time

## Summary
Earth history is reconstructed from rock layers, fossils, and geologic structures.

Key ideas from this lesson:

1. Earth's past is recorded in the geologic record
2. relative order helps scientists reconstruct events
3. fossils and layers reveal changing environments over time

When you study Earth history, ask what the clues show and what sequence of events best fits the evidence.
