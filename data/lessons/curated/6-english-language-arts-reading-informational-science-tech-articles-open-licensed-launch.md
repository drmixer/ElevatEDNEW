# Science/Tech Articles (open-licensed)

## Learning Goal
- I can identify the central idea of a science or technology article.
- I can choose details that best support the central idea.
- I can explain why an article seems careful or credible.

## Introduction
Science and technology articles often present a problem, describe evidence, and explain what the results mean. Strong readers do more than collect facts. They look for the main point, test which details really support it, and notice whether the article sounds trustworthy.

In this lesson, you will read a short article about a school testing a cooling roof paint. As you read, focus on three questions:

1. What is the article mainly trying to show?
2. Which evidence best supports that idea?
3. What makes the article sound careful and credible?

## Key Vocabulary
**Central idea**: The main point the author wants the reader to understand.

**Supporting detail**: A fact, example, or result that helps prove the central idea.

**Evidence**: Specific information that supports a claim.

**Credible**: Believable because it is supported by careful evidence or trustworthy sources.

**Claim**: A statement the author wants the reader to accept.

## Text Focus: Article Excerpt
Read the article below. The paragraph numbers will help you point to evidence.

### Article: "A Cooler Roof for Maple School"
**Paragraph 1.** Last fall, several classrooms at Maple School became uncomfortably hot during the afternoon. To test one possible solution, the district painted part of the roof with a bright reflective coating designed to bounce more sunlight away.

**Paragraph 2.** For one month, the school compared temperatures in rooms under the painted roof section and rooms under the untreated section. The painted section stayed about 3 degrees cooler on average during the warmest part of the day.

**Paragraph 3.** The district engineer said the early results are promising, but she also warned that one month of data is not enough to prove the roof paint will work the same way in every season.

## Finding the Central Idea
The article is not just about a school roof. It is making a more specific point.

A strong central idea is:

"The reflective roof paint may help keep classrooms cooler, but the school still needs more evidence."

That answer works because it includes both:

- the result of the test
- the caution about limited evidence

## Model Analysis: Best Supporting Detail
Suppose the question is:

"Which detail best supports the article's central idea?"

A strong choice is this line from Paragraph 2:

"The painted section stayed about 3 degrees cooler on average during the warmest part of the day."

Why is this strong evidence?

- It gives a measured result.
- It connects directly to the problem in Paragraph 1.
- It supports the claim better than a general opinion would.

A weaker detail would be "the district painted part of the roof." That tells what happened, but not whether the test worked.

## Evaluating Credibility
The article sounds more credible because it:

- includes a specific measurement
- compares two roof sections
- gives a warning that the evidence is still limited

Paragraph 3 is especially important. A weaker article might claim the problem is completely solved. This article sounds more trustworthy because it does not overclaim.

## Common Mistake: Topic Instead of Central Idea
A weak response might say:

"The article is about school buildings."

That is too broad.

A stronger response explains the article's actual point:

"The article explains that roof paint may help cool classrooms, but more evidence is still needed."

## Summary
When you read a science or technology article:

1. identify the central idea
2. choose the strongest supporting detail
3. notice what makes the article sound careful and credible

In this lesson, the strongest detail was the measured temperature change, and the article sounded credible because it included both evidence and caution.
