# Earth History

## Learning Goal
- I can explain how rock layers and fossils help scientists study Earth's past.
- I can identify evidence that shows which events happened earlier or later.
- I can use geologic evidence to support a claim about an ancient environment.

## Introduction
Earth has changed for a very long time. Mountains rise, seas cover land, volcanoes erupt, and living things appear, change, and disappear. Scientists reconstruct this long history by studying clues in rocks, fossils, and landforms.

In this lesson, ask:

1. What clues record Earth's past?
2. How do scientists place events in order?
3. What can fossils and rock layers reveal about earlier environments?

## Key Vocabulary
**Rock layer**: A band of rock or sediment formed during a period of deposition.

**Fossil**: Preserved remains or traces of ancient life.

**Deposition**: The dropping and building up of sediment.

**Erosion**: The wearing away and movement of rock or sediment.

**Relative age**: Which event is older or younger without using an exact date.

**Geologic record**: The evidence of Earth's past preserved in rocks, fossils, and landforms.

## Phenomenon: Sea Fossils Found on Dry Land
Scientists examine a dry hillside far from the ocean and find:

- shell fossils inside a rock layer
- several stacked layers above and below it
- a crack that cuts through some layers

The question is:

How can this place contain sea fossils even though it is dry land today?

## How Earth's Past Is Recorded
The geologic record stores clues over long periods of time.

Important clues include:

- layers of sediment
- fossils trapped in those layers
- cracks, folds, and faults that change older rock
- signs that erosion removed material before new layers formed

Scientists use these clues together because one rock layer alone cannot tell the whole story.

## Putting Events in Order
Earth history often begins with relative order.

Scientists commonly reason that:

- lower layers are usually older than layers above them
- a crack or fault that cuts through rock is younger than the rock it cuts
- fossils can help identify what kind of environment existed when a layer formed

This helps scientists build a sequence instead of guessing.

## Worked Example 1: Reading Layer Order
Suppose students observe:

- Layer A at the bottom
- Layer B above it
- a fault cutting through both layers

What likely happened first?

"Layer A formed first, then Layer B, and the fault happened later."

Reasoning:

- lower layers are usually older
- the fault must be younger because it cuts through the layers after they formed

## Worked Example 2: Inferring an Ancient Environment
What is a strong claim about shell fossils found in a rock layer?

"That layer likely formed in or near a marine environment."

Why?

- shells come from organisms that lived in water
- if the fossils are preserved in the layer, that layer likely formed where those organisms lived

This does not mean the area is marine now. It means the environment changed over time.

## Worked Example 3: Explaining Change Over Time
Suppose a region shows river sediment at the bottom, a shell-rich layer above it, and tilted layers today.

What story does the evidence support?

"The area changed from one environment to another over time, and later forces tilted the layers."

This is stronger than naming the layers alone because it explains change across time.

## Common Mistake: Assuming a Place Always Looked the Way It Looks Now
Students sometimes think:

"It is dry land now, so it was always dry land."

That ignores geologic evidence.

A stronger method is:

1. identify the clues in the rock
2. place the events in order
3. explain how the environment changed

## Summary
Earth history is reconstructed from clues in rocks, fossils, and structures.

Key ideas from this lesson:

1. rock layers and fossils record Earth's past
2. scientists use relative order to place events in sequence
3. geologic evidence shows that environments change over time

When you study Earth history, ask what the rocks record and what story the evidence tells.
