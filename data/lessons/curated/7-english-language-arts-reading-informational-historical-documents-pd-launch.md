# Historical Documents (PD)

## Learning Goal
- I can identify the main message of a historical document.
- I can cite details that reveal the author's purpose and audience.
- I can explain how the document's time period helps me understand its meaning.

## Introduction
Historical documents were written for real people during real moments of conflict, change, or decision. That means good readers ask more than, "What does the document say?" They also ask why it was written and who it was trying to influence.

In this lesson, you will read an adapted town notice from a drought year. As you read, focus on three questions:

1. What action does the writer want people to take?
2. Which details show the writer's purpose?
3. How does the situation in the town shape the message?

## Key Vocabulary
**Audience**: The person or group the writer wants to influence.

**Purpose**: The reason a document was written.

**Proclamation**: A public announcement made by a leader or authority.

**Ration**: To limit how much people can use of something.

**Context**: The situation or time period around a text.

## Text Focus: Adapted Town Proclamation
Read the document below. The paragraph numbers will help you locate the strongest evidence.

### Adapted Historical Document: "Town Water Notice, 1936"
**Paragraph 1.** To all households of Mesa Junction: The wells on the eastern ridge have fallen lower than any summer in living memory. If we continue drawing water at the current rate, the town will not have enough stored for late August.

**Paragraph 2.** Beginning Monday, each family must collect water for washing and gardens only on alternate days. Shop owners are asked to close outdoor taps after sunset, and ranchers must report weekly use to the town office.

**Paragraph 3.** These rules are not made to burden one street more than another. They are made so the school kitchen, clinic, and public pump can continue serving everyone through the hottest part of the season.

**Paragraph 4.** We ask every resident to treat conservation as a shared duty. A town that wastes together will suffer together. A town that plans carefully can endure until the rains return.

## Finding the Main Message
The document is not only giving information about dry wells. It is also trying to guide the town's behavior.

A strong statement of the main message is:

"The town must limit water use now so essential services can continue during the drought."

That works because it includes:

- the action the writer wants
- the reason the action matters

A weaker answer would be "This document is about water." That is only the topic, not the full message.

## Author's Purpose and Audience
The audience is the residents of Mesa Junction:

- households
- shop owners
- ranchers

The purpose is to persuade people to accept and follow water restrictions.

The strongest details for that purpose appear in Paragraphs 2 and 3:

- the document gives clear rules
- it explains that the rules protect the school kitchen, clinic, and public pump

Those details matter because the writer is not just warning people. The writer is trying to make the rules seem necessary and fair.

## Model Analysis: Best Evidence
Suppose the question is:

"Which detail best shows why the town believes rationing is necessary?"

A strong choice is this line from Paragraph 3:

"They are made so the school kitchen, clinic, and public pump can continue serving everyone through the hottest part of the season."

Why is this strong evidence?

- It explains the reason for the rules.
- It shows the goal is protecting important shared services.
- It helps readers understand that the limits are connected to community survival, not punishment.

A weaker choice would be the phrase "fallen lower than any summer in living memory." That detail shows the drought is serious, but it does not explain the exact reason behind the rationing plan as directly.

## Context Helps Interpretation
The time period matters here. In a drought year, people may already feel worried, tired, or frustrated. That helps explain why the writer uses both direct instructions and community language.

The document says:

- "shared duty"
- "A town that wastes together will suffer together"

These lines show that the writer wants residents to think beyond individual convenience. The context of shortage makes cooperation more important.

## Common Mistake: Summarizing Without Explaining Purpose
A weak response might say:

"The town is in a drought, so people need to use less water."

That summary is partly true, but it leaves out the author's purpose.

A stronger response sounds like this:

"The document tells residents to ration water so the town can protect important services during the drought. The writer supports this purpose by giving specific rules and by explaining that the limits help the whole community."

## Summary
When you read a historical document:

1. identify the main action or message
2. figure out who the document addresses
3. choose the details that reveal the writer's purpose
4. use context to explain why the document is written that way

In this lesson, the town notice became clearer once you saw that it was not just reporting a drought. It was trying to persuade people to accept shared sacrifice so the town could endure the season.
