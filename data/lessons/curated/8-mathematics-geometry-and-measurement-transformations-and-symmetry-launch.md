# Transformations & Symmetry

## Learning Goal
- I can describe translations, reflections, and rotations as rigid motions.
- I can use coordinate rules to predict the image of a figure after a transformation.
- I can explain why rigid motions preserve congruence and how symmetry fits into that idea.

## Introduction
In Grade 8 geometry, transformations are part of a more formal idea: **rigid motion**.

A rigid motion moves a figure without changing its size or shape. That is why transformations such as translations, reflections, and rotations are central to congruence.

The coordinate plane makes these ideas precise because you can track exactly how each vertex changes.

As you read, focus on three questions:

1. What coordinate change does each rigid motion produce?
2. Why do rigid motions preserve lengths and angles?
3. How does symmetry show that a figure maps onto itself under a reflection or rotation?

## Key Vocabulary
**Rigid motion**: A transformation that preserves size and shape.

**Translation**: A slide that moves every point the same distance and direction.

**Reflection**: A flip across a line.

**Rotation**: A turn around a point.

**Symmetry**: A property in which a figure maps onto itself under a reflection or rotation.

## Rigid Motions Preserve Congruence
Translations, reflections, and rotations are rigid motions because they keep:

- side lengths
- angle measures
- overall shape

This means the image of a figure under any rigid motion is congruent to the original figure.

That is one of the most important Grade 8 geometry ideas:

congruence can be explained through rigid motion.

## Worked Example 1: Translation on the Coordinate Plane
Triangle `A` has vertices:

- `(1, 1)`
- `(4, 1)`
- `(2, 3)`

Translate the triangle right `3` units and up `2` units.

Apply the rule:

`(x, y) -> (x + 3, y + 2)`

Find the image of each point:

- `(1, 1) -> (4, 3)`
- `(4, 1) -> (7, 3)`
- `(2, 3) -> (5, 5)`

The image triangle has the same side lengths and angles as the original because a translation is a rigid motion.

## Worked Example 2: Reflection Across the y-axis
Point `P(3, -2)` is reflected across the `y`-axis.

Use the rule:

`(x, y) -> (-x, y)`

So:

`P(3, -2) -> P'(-3, -2)`

Only the horizontal position changes sign because the point flips to the opposite side of the `y`-axis.

The distance from the axis stays the same.

## Worked Example 3: Rotation 90 Degrees About the Origin
Point `Q(2, 5)` is rotated `90` degrees counterclockwise about the origin.

Use the rule:

`(x, y) -> (-y, x)`

So:

`Q(2, 5) -> Q'(-5, 2)`

This rotation changes orientation, but it still preserves the figure's size and angle measures.

That is why the image remains congruent to the original point configuration.

## Worked Example 4: Congruence Through Rigid Motion
Two triangles on a grid have vertices:

Triangle `M`:

- `(1, 1)`
- `(3, 1)`
- `(2, 4)`

Triangle `N`:

- `(-1, 1)`
- `(-3, 1)`
- `(-2, 4)`

How can we explain the relationship?

Each point in Triangle `M` changes by reflecting across the `y`-axis:

- `(1, 1) -> (-1, 1)`
- `(3, 1) -> (-3, 1)`
- `(2, 4) -> (-2, 4)`

So Triangle `N` is the reflection image of Triangle `M`.

Because reflection is a rigid motion, the triangles are congruent.

## Symmetry as a Special Transformation
Symmetry means a figure maps onto itself under a transformation.

Examples:

- a reflection across a line of symmetry
- a rotation around a center of rotational symmetry

For instance, a square has:

- reflection symmetry across several lines
- rotational symmetry when turned about its center

This means symmetry is not a separate topic from transformations. It is a case where a transformation sends a figure onto itself.

## Common Mistake: Mixing Up Rigid Motions and Dilations
Students sometimes group dilation with translations, reflections, and rotations and assume all four preserve congruence.

That is incorrect.

A dilation changes size, so it does not preserve congruence unless the scale factor is `1`.

Use this check:

- translation, reflection, rotation -> rigid motions
- dilation -> size-changing transformation

This distinction matters because it separates congruence from similarity.

## Summary
Grade 8 transformations are best understood through rigid motion.

To solve these problems well:

1. identify the type of transformation
2. apply the correct coordinate rule
3. use rigid-motion reasoning to justify congruence
4. connect symmetry to transformations that map a figure onto itself

Rigid motions are powerful because they give a precise, coordinate-based explanation for why two figures are congruent.
