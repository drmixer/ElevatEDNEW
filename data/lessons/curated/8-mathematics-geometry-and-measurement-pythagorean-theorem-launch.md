# Pythagorean Theorem & Distance

## Learning Goal
- I can identify when the Pythagorean Theorem applies.
- I can solve for a missing side length in a right triangle.
- I can use the Pythagorean Theorem to find distance on a coordinate plane and explain my steps.

## Introduction
Some geometry problems look very different on the surface, but they share the same structure.

- a ladder leaning against a wall
- the diagonal of a rectangle
- the straight-line distance between two points on a grid

Each of these can create a right triangle. When that happens, the Pythagorean Theorem gives a reliable way to connect the side lengths.

The theorem is not just a formula to memorize. It is a way to reason about right triangles. Your job is to first recognize the triangle, then decide which side is missing, then solve carefully.

## Key Vocabulary
**Right triangle**: A triangle with one `90` degree angle.

**Legs**: The two sides that form the right angle.

**Hypotenuse**: The side opposite the right angle; it is always the longest side in a right triangle.

**Pythagorean Theorem**: For a right triangle with legs `a` and `b` and hypotenuse `c`, `a^2 + b^2 = c^2`.

**Distance formula idea**: On a coordinate plane, horizontal and vertical changes can form the legs of a right triangle.

## When the Theorem Applies
The Pythagorean Theorem applies only when:

- the figure contains a right triangle
- you know two side lengths and need the third

It does **not** apply to every triangle.

Before writing the equation, ask:

1. Where is the right angle?
2. Which side is opposite the right angle?
3. Are the known side lengths legs or is one of them the hypotenuse?

Those questions prevent many common mistakes.

## Worked Example 1: Finding the Hypotenuse
A right triangle has legs `6` and `8`. Find the hypotenuse.

Because `6` and `8` are the legs, they are `a` and `b`. The unknown longest side is `c`.

Write the theorem:

`a^2 + b^2 = c^2`

Substitute:

`6^2 + 8^2 = c^2`

`36 + 64 = c^2`

`100 = c^2`

Take the square root:

`c = 10`

So the hypotenuse is `10`.

Why this makes sense:

- the hypotenuse should be longer than `8`
- `10` is longer than both legs
- the result is reasonable before you even finish checking

## Worked Example 2: Finding a Missing Leg
A right triangle has hypotenuse `13` and one leg `5`. Find the other leg.

This time the hypotenuse is known, so be careful not to put `13` in a leg position.

Let the missing leg be `b`.

`a^2 + b^2 = c^2`

`5^2 + b^2 = 13^2`

`25 + b^2 = 169`

Subtract `25` from both sides:

`b^2 = 144`

Take the square root:

`b = 12`

So the missing leg is `12`.

This is a good place to notice a common number pattern: `5, 12, 13` is a Pythagorean triple. Even if you do not recognize that pattern, the algebra still works.

## Common Mistake: Putting the Hypotenuse in the Wrong Place
Students often write:

`13^2 + 5^2 = b^2`

That is incorrect because the hypotenuse must be `c`, and `c^2` is alone on one side of the equation.

A quick check helps:

- the hypotenuse is the longest side
- if you square the longest side, it should be larger than either leg square
- the two smaller squares add to the largest square

If your setup makes a shorter side the hypotenuse, stop and relabel.

## Distance on a Coordinate Plane
Suppose you want the straight-line distance between `(2, 3)` and `(8, 11)`.

First find the horizontal and vertical changes:

- horizontal change: `8 - 2 = 6`
- vertical change: `11 - 3 = 8`

Those changes form the legs of a right triangle.

Now apply the theorem:

`6^2 + 8^2 = c^2`

`36 + 64 = c^2`

`100 = c^2`

`c = 10`

So the distance between the two points is `10` units.

The key idea is this: the coordinate plane problem is really a right triangle problem in disguise.

## Worked Example 3: The Diagonal of a Square
A square has side length `7`. What is the diagonal?

Draw one diagonal. It splits the square into two right triangles.

Each triangle has:

- one leg of `7`
- another leg of `7`
- a diagonal as the hypotenuse

Set up the equation:

`7^2 + 7^2 = c^2`

`49 + 49 = c^2`

`98 = c^2`

`c = square root of 98`

Simplify:

`98 = 49 x 2`

`c = 7 square root of 2`

So the diagonal is `7 square root of 2`.

This example matters because not every answer is a whole number. A correct answer may be an irrational number in simplest radical form.

## How To Check Your Answer
After solving, ask:

- Is the hypotenuse the longest side?
- Did I square the correct numbers?
- Did I undo the square with a square root?
- Does the answer fit the geometry of the problem?

For a distance problem, also ask whether the result is greater than either single horizontal or vertical change when it should be.

## Summary
The Pythagorean Theorem connects the side lengths of a right triangle:

`a^2 + b^2 = c^2`

To use it well:

- identify the right triangle first
- label the hypotenuse correctly
- substitute carefully
- solve and then check whether the result makes geometric sense

When a geometry or coordinate problem can be turned into a right triangle, the theorem gives you a dependable path to the answer.
