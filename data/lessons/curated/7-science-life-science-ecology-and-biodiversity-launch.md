# Ecology & Biodiversity

## Learning Goal
- I can explain how energy moves through an ecosystem using producers, consumers, and decomposers.
- I can use evidence from a food web to predict how a change in one population affects other populations.
- I can explain why biodiversity can make an ecosystem more stable.

## Introduction
An ecosystem can look healthy on the surface and still be changing underneath. If one population drops sharply, the effects can spread through the whole system.

Ecology helps explain those chain reactions. Organisms depend on one another for energy and for the cycling of matter. Biodiversity matters because ecosystems with more kinds of organisms often have more than one pathway for energy and matter to move.

In this lesson, ask:

1. How does energy move through a food web?
2. What happens when one part of the web changes?
3. Why can biodiversity make an ecosystem more resilient?

## Key Vocabulary
**Producer**: An organism, such as a plant or algae, that makes its own food using sunlight.

**Consumer**: An organism that gets energy by eating other organisms.

**Decomposer**: An organism that breaks down dead matter and returns materials to the ecosystem.

**Food web**: A model showing how energy moves among organisms in an ecosystem.

**Biodiversity**: The variety of living things in an ecosystem.

**Ecosystem stability**: The ability of an ecosystem to keep functioning even when conditions change.

## Phenomenon: A Pond Changes After Runoff
Scientists study a pond after heavy rain washes extra fertilizer into the water. Over the next two weeks, they observe:

- algae grow rapidly near the surface
- the water becomes cloudier
- insect populations fall
- small fish become less common near the shoreline
- dead plant and animal material builds up on the bottom

The pond still contains living things, but the pattern has changed. The scientific question is not only, "What died?" It is, "How did the change spread through the system?"

## How Energy and Matter Move in an Ecosystem
Energy enters most ecosystems through producers. In a pond, algae and aquatic plants use sunlight to make food. Consumers then get energy by eating producers or by eating other consumers.

Matter also cycles through the system. When organisms die or release waste, decomposers break that material down. The nutrients return to the environment and can be used again by producers.

This means an ecosystem is not just a list of organisms. It is a system of connected pathways:

- energy moves from one organism to another
- matter is reused through decomposition
- changes in one population can affect many others

## Worked Example 1: Explaining a Food-Web Change
Suppose a pond food web includes:

- algae
- insect larvae
- small fish
- herons

If insect larvae decrease sharply, what changes would you expect next?

A strong explanation is:

"Small fish may decrease because they lose a major food source, and herons may then have fewer small fish to eat."

Evidence and reasoning:

- insect larvae transfer energy from producers to higher consumers
- if that link weakens, less energy reaches small fish
- if fewer small fish survive, predators above them are affected too

This is food-web reasoning. You are tracing energy movement, not guessing randomly about which organism matters.

## Worked Example 2: The Role of Decomposers
After organisms die during the pond change, decomposers become more active.

Why does that matter?

A strong answer is:

"Decomposers break down dead material and return nutrients to the pond, which helps cycle matter back into the ecosystem."

This does not automatically mean the ecosystem is healthy again. It means decomposers are doing one essential job in the system:

- recycling matter
- making nutrients available again
- connecting dead material back to future growth

Students often focus only on producers and consumers, but decomposers are necessary for the full cycle.

## Worked Example 3: Why Biodiversity Supports Stability
Compare two grassland ecosystems.

Ecosystem A has many plant species, several insect species, and multiple small herbivores.

Ecosystem B has only one main grass species and one major herbivore.

A drought damages one plant species in both ecosystems.

Which ecosystem is more likely to remain stable?

A strong claim is:

"Ecosystem A is more likely to remain stable because its biodiversity provides more alternative pathways for energy flow."

Reasoning:

- if one producer declines, other producers may still support herbivores
- if one consumer changes, other organisms may still fill similar roles
- a more diverse system is often less likely to collapse from one disturbance

Biodiversity does not prevent every problem, but it can reduce how far one disruption spreads.

## Common Mistake: Treating a Food Web Like a Simple Chain
Students sometimes treat a food web as if every organism has only one connection.

That leads to weak explanations such as:

"If insect larvae decrease, only the fish are affected."

In a real food web, several organisms may depend on the same population. Strong ecosystem reasoning asks:

1. which organisms depend on this one directly?
2. which organisms may be affected indirectly?
3. how does the change affect both energy flow and matter cycling?

The wider pattern matters more than one single arrow.

## Summary
Ecology explains how organisms interact through energy flow, matter cycling, and system relationships.

Key ideas from this lesson:

1. producers, consumers, and decomposers all play connected roles
2. changes in one population can spread through a food web
3. biodiversity can help ecosystems stay more stable when conditions change

When you explain an ecosystem change, do not list organisms separately. Ask how the evidence shows energy and matter moving through the whole system.
