# Percent & Rates

## Learning Goal
- I can solve percent increase, percent decrease, tax, discount, and tip problems.
- I can find and compare unit rates in real-world contexts.
- I can choose whether to multiply, divide, or subtract based on what the question asks.

## Introduction
In Grade 7, percent and rate problems become more realistic and more multi-step.

You may need to:

- find a discount and then a sale price
- calculate tax or tip
- compare unit prices
- analyze constant speed

These problems are connected because percent is a rate per `100`, and many rate situations depend on proportional reasoning.

In this lesson, focus on three questions:

1. What quantity does the percent apply to?
2. Do I need the part, the whole, or the remaining amount?
3. How can a unit rate help me compare options fairly?

## Key Vocabulary
**Percent**: A rate out of `100`.

**Percent increase**: The amount a quantity grows compared to its original value.

**Percent decrease**: The amount a quantity shrinks compared to its original value.

**Discount**: The amount subtracted from the original price.

**Unit rate**: A comparison to `1` unit.

**Markup**: An amount added to the original price, often described as a percent.

## The Original Amount Matters
In percent problems, the most important question is:

What is the whole?

If the whole changes, the answer changes.

For example, a `20%` discount on `$50` is not the same as a `20%` discount on `$80`.

That sounds obvious, but many percent mistakes happen because students apply the percent to the wrong amount.

## Worked Example 1: Finding a Discount and Sale Price
A backpack costs `$68` and is marked down `25%`.

Find the discount and the sale price.

Step 1: Find the discount.

`25% = 0.25`

`0.25 x 68 = 17`

So the discount is `$17`.

Step 2: Subtract from the original price.

`68 - 17 = 51`

So the sale price is:

`$51`

The part found by the percent was the discount, not the final price. That is why the subtraction step matters.

## Worked Example 2: Finding Tax
A skateboard costs `$84`. Sales tax is `6%`.

What is the total cost?

Step 1: Find the tax.

`6% = 0.06`

`0.06 x 84 = 5.04`

So the tax is `$5.04`.

Step 2: Add the tax to the original price.

`84 + 5.04 = 89.04`

So the total cost is:

`$89.04`

Percent decrease problems usually end with subtraction. Tax and markup problems usually end with addition.

## Worked Example 3: Percent Increase
A concert ticket used to cost `$40`. Now it costs `$46`.

What is the percent increase?

Step 1: Find the amount of increase.

`46 - 40 = 6`

Step 2: Compare the increase to the original value.

`6 / 40 = 0.15`

Step 3: Convert to percent.

`0.15 = 15%`

So the price increased by `15%`.

The comparison must be made with the original amount, not the new amount.

## Worked Example 4: Comparing Unit Prices
Which is the better buy?

- `12` granola bars for `$7.20`
- `18` granola bars for `$10.26`

Find each unit price.

First package:

`7.20 / 12 = 0.60`

So the cost is `$0.60` per bar.

Second package:

`10.26 / 18 = 0.57`

So the cost is `$0.57` per bar.

Compare:

`0.57 < 0.60`

So the second package is the better buy.

## Worked Example 5: Constant Speed as a Unit Rate
A runner travels `9.6` miles in `1.5` hours.

Find the unit rate in miles per hour.

`9.6 / 1.5 = 6.4`

So the runner's speed is:

`6.4 miles per hour`

Once the rate is found, you can predict other distances.

For example, in `3` hours the runner would travel:

`6.4 x 3 = 19.2`

miles

## One-Step and Two-Step Percent Thinking
Some percent problems can be done in one multiplication step.

Example:

`30%` of `90`

`0.30 x 90 = 27`

Other problems need two steps.

Example:

Find the final price after a `30%` discount.

Step 1: Find the discount:

`0.30 x 90 = 27`

Step 2: Subtract:

`90 - 27 = 63`

The numbers are the same, but the question is different. That is why reading the problem carefully matters.

## Common Mistake: Using the New Value Instead of the Original Value
To find percent change, students sometimes divide by the new amount.

For the ticket problem:

- increase = `6`
- original value = `40`
- new value = `46`

The correct calculation is:

`6 / 40`

not

`6 / 46`

Percent change is always based on the original amount.

## Summary
Grade 7 percent and rate work often requires more than one step.

To solve well:

1. identify the original amount
2. decide whether the percent gives a part, an increase, or a decrease
3. add or subtract only after finding the percent amount when needed
4. use unit rates to compare prices, speeds, and other choices fairly

When you keep the whole, the percent amount, and the final question separate, percent and rate problems become much more accurate.
