# Integers & Rational Numbers

## Learning Goal
- I can classify integers and rational numbers within the real number system.
- I can explain why rational numbers have terminating or repeating decimal forms.
- I can compare and use rational numbers accurately in algebraic and real-world contexts.

## Introduction
By Grade 8, rational numbers are no longer just numbers to compute with. They also become numbers to classify, represent, and interpret across several forms.

A rational number can be written as a fraction of integers. Because of that, rational numbers have predictable decimal behavior:

- they terminate
- or they repeat

Integers are part of the rational number system because any integer can be written as a fraction with denominator `1`.

In this lesson, focus on three questions:

1. How are integers and rational numbers related?
2. How can decimal form help me identify a rational number?
3. How can I compare and use rational numbers precisely?

## Key Vocabulary
**Integer**: A whole number, its opposite, or `0`.

**Rational number**: A number that can be written in the form `a / b`, where `a` and `b` are integers and `b` is not `0`.

**Terminating decimal**: A decimal that ends.

**Repeating decimal**: A decimal in which one digit or a group of digits repeats forever.

**Real number**: Any number that can be placed on the number line.

**Equivalent form**: Another representation of the same value.

## Integers Are Rational Numbers
Every integer is also a rational number.

Examples:

- `5 = 5/1`
- `-12 = -12/1`
- `0 = 0/1`

This means the set of integers is inside the set of rational numbers.

That relationship matters because it helps organize the number system:

- some rational numbers are integers
- some rational numbers are fractions or decimals that are not integers

## Worked Example 1: Classifying Numbers
Classify each number as an integer, a rational number, or both:

- `-7`
- `3/4`
- `1.25`
- `0`

`-7`:

- integer
- rational number because `-7 = -7/1`

`3/4`:

- rational number
- not an integer

`1.25`:

- rational number because `1.25 = 125/100 = 5/4`
- not an integer

`0`:

- integer
- rational number because `0 = 0/1`

So two of the numbers are both integers and rational numbers, while the others are rational but not integers.

## Worked Example 2: Converting a Fraction to a Decimal
Write `7/8` as a decimal.

Step 1: Divide numerator by denominator.

`7 / 8 = 0.875`

Step 2: Interpret the result.

The decimal terminates, so it is rational.

This fits the rule that rational numbers have decimal forms that terminate or repeat.

## Worked Example 3: Recognizing a Repeating Decimal as Rational
Consider the decimal:

`0.4444...`

This decimal repeats.

A repeating decimal is rational because it can be written as a fraction.

In this case:

`0.4444... = 4/9`

You do not always need to find the exact fraction to know the number is rational. The repeating pattern itself is enough evidence.

## Worked Example 4: Comparing Rational Numbers in Different Forms
Order these numbers from least to greatest:

`-1.2`, `-5/4`, `0.2`, `1/8`

Step 1: Rewrite in comparable forms.

`-5/4 = -1.25`

`1/8 = 0.125`

Step 2: Order the values.

Among the negatives:

`-1.25 < -1.2`

Among the positives:

`0.125 < 0.2`

So:

`-5/4 < -1.2 < 1/8 < 0.2`

## Worked Example 5: Using Rational Numbers in Context
A hiker begins at an elevation of `125.5` meters. The trail drops `18.75` meters and then rises `6.5` meters.

Find the final elevation.

Step 1: Subtract the drop.

`125.5 - 18.75 = 106.75`

Step 2: Add the rise.

`106.75 + 6.5 = 113.25`

So the final elevation is:

`113.25` meters

This example uses rational numbers in decimal form, but the reasoning is the same as with fractions or integers: interpret the sign, combine the changes, and keep track of the units.

## Decimal Form Reveals Structure
Rational numbers can look different, but their forms are connected:

- fraction: `2/5`
- decimal: `0.4`
- percent: `40%`

They all represent the same value.

This flexibility matters in algebra because some forms are easier for comparing, some are better for calculating, and some are clearer in context.

## Common Mistake: Thinking Every Decimal That Goes On Forever Is Irrational
Students sometimes assume that if a decimal does not stop, it must be irrational.

That is not correct.

A decimal can go on forever and still be rational if it repeats.

Examples:

- `0.3333...`
- `1.272727...`

These are rational because they have repeating patterns.

The key distinction is:

- terminating or repeating decimal: rational
- non-terminating and non-repeating decimal: not rational

## Summary
Grade 8 work with integers and rational numbers includes classifying numbers and understanding their decimal structure.

To reason well:

1. remember that every integer is also a rational number
2. use fraction, decimal, and percent forms as equivalent representations
3. recognize that rational decimals terminate or repeat
4. rewrite numbers in useful forms when comparing or solving problems

When you see how the forms connect, the number system becomes more organized and easier to use.
