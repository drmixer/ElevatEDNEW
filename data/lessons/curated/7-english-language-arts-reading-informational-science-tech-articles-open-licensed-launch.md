# Science/Tech Articles (open-licensed)

## Learning Goal
- I can identify the central idea of a science or technology article.
- I can choose details from the text that best support the central idea.
- I can explain why a source seems credible using evidence from the article.

## Introduction
Science and technology articles often explain a problem, describe a test, and report what the results show. Strong readers do more than collect facts. They track the article's main claim, notice which details actually support that claim, and ask whether the source sounds trustworthy.

In this lesson, you will read a short informational article about a middle school testing a reflective roof coating to keep classrooms cooler. As you read, pay attention to three questions:

1. What problem is the school trying to solve?
2. Which details show whether the coating helped?
3. Which details make the report sound credible?

## Key Vocabulary
**Central idea**: The main point the author wants the reader to understand after reading the whole text.

**Supporting detail**: A fact, example, or quotation that helps prove or explain the central idea.

**Claim**: A statement the author wants the reader to accept as true.

**Credible**: Believable because it is supported by reliable evidence, careful wording, or trustworthy sources.

**Evidence**: Specific information from the text that supports a claim or idea.

## Text Focus: Article Excerpt
Read the excerpt below. The paragraph numbers will help you point to the strongest evidence.

### Article: "A Cooler Roof for Carter Middle School"
**Paragraph 1.** Last August, classrooms at Carter Middle School reached 87 degrees by early afternoon. Teachers closed the blinds, and the school spent extra money on fans during the hottest weeks. To test a possible solution, the district covered one section of the roof with a pale reflective coating often called a "cool roof."

**Paragraph 2.** For six weeks, the school compared two similar seventh-grade hallways. According to temperature logs collected at 1:00 p.m. and 3:00 p.m., rooms under the coated section averaged 4 degrees cooler than rooms under the untreated roof. Electricity use in the cooler hallway also dropped by about 8 percent.

**Paragraph 3.** Dr. Lena Ortiz, an engineer at the state university, reviewed the district's measurements. She said the early results look promising, but she warned that one short test cannot prove the coating solves every heat problem. She recommended collecting data in another season before making a final decision.

## Finding the Central Idea
The topic of the article is a school roof experiment. The central idea is more specific than the topic.

A strong central idea for this article is:

"Carter Middle School tested a reflective roof coating, and the early data suggests it may lower classroom temperature and energy use, but the school still needs more evidence before making a final claim."

That sentence works because it includes both the result and the caution. A weaker answer would say only "The article is about roofs" because that is just the topic, not the full point.

## Model Analysis: Which Detail Best Supports the Central Idea?
When you need the strongest supporting detail, ask yourself which sentence gives direct proof for the author's main point.

Here is one strong choice from Paragraph 2:

"Rooms under the coated section averaged 4 degrees cooler than rooms under the untreated roof."

Why is that detail strong?
- It gives a measured result, not just an opinion.
- It directly connects to the problem from Paragraph 1: classrooms were too hot.
- It helps prove that the coating may have made a real difference.

A weaker detail would be "the district covered one section of the roof with a pale reflective coating." That sentence explains the setup, but it does not show the result of the test.

## Evaluating Credibility
Good readers also check whether the article sounds credible. This article includes several credibility clues:

- It gives specific measurements instead of vague words like "better" or "cooler."
- It names when the data was collected.
- It includes a named expert, Dr. Lena Ortiz.
- The expert uses careful language and does not exaggerate the results.

Notice that Paragraph 3 does not say, "The problem is completely solved." Instead, Dr. Ortiz says the results are promising but still limited. That caution makes the article sound more trustworthy, not less.

## Response Frame: Claim, Evidence, Explanation
When you answer a reading-analysis question, keep the steps clear:

- **Claim**: State the idea you want to prove.
- **Evidence**: Quote or paraphrase a detail from the text.
- **Explanation**: Tell what the detail shows.

Here is a model response:

"The article's central idea is that the cool-roof test may help the school. Paragraph 2 supports this idea by reporting that rooms under the coated section averaged 4 degrees cooler. This evidence matters because it shows a measured result from the experiment, not just a guess."

You can also use this sentence frame:

"The author shows ___ by stating ___. This detail matters because ___."

## Common Mistake: Topic Versus Central Idea
One common mistake is to give a broad topic instead of the central idea.

Example:
- Topic only: "The article is about school buildings."
- Better central idea: "The article explains that a reflective roof coating may reduce heat and energy use, but more testing is still needed."

If your answer could fit almost any science article, it is probably too broad.

## Summary
When you read a science or technology article, do three things:

- identify the central idea
- choose the detail that gives the strongest proof
- check whether the source sounds credible and careful

In this lesson, the strongest evidence came from measured temperature data, and the article sounded credible because it included specific numbers, a named expert, and a warning not to overclaim. That is the reading move to reuse in the next article you analyze.
