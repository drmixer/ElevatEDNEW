# Pythagorean Theorem

## Learning Goal
- I can identify when the Pythagorean Theorem applies.
- I can find a missing side length in a right triangle.
- I can check whether my answer makes sense by comparing the side lengths.

## Introduction
Some geometry problems look different at first, but they share the same structure.

- the diagonal of a rectangle
- a ladder leaning against a wall
- a ramp rising to a platform

Each of these can create a right triangle. When that happens, the Pythagorean Theorem connects the side lengths.

This lesson is about recognizing that structure and using it carefully. Before solving, always ask:

1. Is this a right triangle?
2. Which side is the hypotenuse?
3. Which two side lengths are known?

## Key Vocabulary
**Right triangle**: A triangle with one `90` degree angle.

**Legs**: The two sides that form the right angle.

**Hypotenuse**: The side opposite the right angle; it is always the longest side.

**Pythagorean Theorem**: For a right triangle with legs `a` and `b` and hypotenuse `c`, `a^2 + b^2 = c^2`.

**Square number**: A number made by multiplying a number by itself, such as `5^2 = 25`.

## When the Theorem Applies
The Pythagorean Theorem applies only to right triangles.

If the triangle does not have a right angle, the theorem does not fit.

In a right triangle:

- the legs are the shorter sides that meet at the right angle
- the hypotenuse is across from the right angle
- the hypotenuse is the longest side

That last fact is useful for checking your work.

## Worked Example 1: Finding the Hypotenuse
A right triangle has legs `3` and `4`. Find the hypotenuse.

Because the unknown side is opposite the right angle, it is the hypotenuse.

Write the theorem:

`a^2 + b^2 = c^2`

Substitute the known side lengths:

`3^2 + 4^2 = c^2`

Square the legs:

`9 + 16 = c^2`

Add:

`25 = c^2`

Take the square root:

`c = 5`

So the hypotenuse is `5`.

Check:

- `5` is longer than `3`
- `5` is longer than `4`

The answer fits the geometry.

## Worked Example 2: Finding a Missing Leg
A right triangle has hypotenuse `10` and one leg `6`. Find the other leg.

This time the hypotenuse is known, so be careful to put `10` in the `c` position.

Write the theorem:

`a^2 + b^2 = c^2`

Substitute:

`6^2 + b^2 = 10^2`

Square the known values:

`36 + b^2 = 100`

Subtract `36` from both sides:

`b^2 = 64`

Take the square root:

`b = 8`

So the missing leg is `8`.

## Using the Theorem in a Rectangle
A rectangle is `5` units long and `12` units wide. What is the diagonal?

The diagonal creates a right triangle inside the rectangle.

The side lengths `5` and `12` are the legs, and the diagonal is the hypotenuse.

Set up the equation:

`5^2 + 12^2 = c^2`

`25 + 144 = c^2`

`169 = c^2`

`c = 13`

So the diagonal is `13` units.

This is a useful example because the theorem can appear inside other shapes, not only in a triangle picture.

## Worked Example 3: A Real-World Ramp
A ramp rises `2` feet to a stage and extends `6` feet across the ground. How long is the ramp?

The rise and the horizontal run form the legs of a right triangle.

Use the theorem:

`2^2 + 6^2 = c^2`

`4 + 36 = c^2`

`40 = c^2`

Take the square root:

`c = square root of 40`

Simplify:

`40 = 4 x 10`

`c = 2 square root of 10`

So the ramp is `2 square root of 10` feet long.

An answer does not always have to be a whole number. The important part is that the setup is correct and the result is longer than each leg.

## Common Mistake: Mislabeling the Hypotenuse
Students sometimes put the longest side in the wrong place.

For example, if a triangle has sides `8`, `15`, and `17`, the correct setup is:

`8^2 + 15^2 = 17^2`

not

`17^2 + 8^2 = 15^2`

Why?

The hypotenuse must be alone on one side as `c^2`.

A quick safety check is to ask:

- Which side is opposite the right angle?
- Is that side also the longest side?

If not, relabel before solving.

## Summary
The Pythagorean Theorem connects the side lengths of a right triangle:

`a^2 + b^2 = c^2`

To use it well:

1. identify the right triangle
2. label the hypotenuse correctly
3. substitute the known side lengths
4. solve and check whether the answer is reasonable

The theorem works best when you treat it as a geometry relationship, not just a formula to memorize.
