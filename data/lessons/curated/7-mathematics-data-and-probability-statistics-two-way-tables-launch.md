# Two-way Tables

## Learning Goal
- I can read counts from a two-way table and compute row and column totals.
- I can find relative frequencies from a two-way table.
- I can compare groups fairly by using proportions instead of only raw counts.

## Introduction
In Grade 7, two-way tables become more useful because you do more than read counts. You also compare how common something is within a group.

That matters because raw counts can be misleading. A larger group may have a larger count even when the event is less common.

Two-way tables help you organize:

- categorical data in rows and columns
- totals for each group
- relative frequencies that show proportions

In this lesson, focus on three questions:

1. What do the counts in the table represent?
2. How do I compute row and column relative frequencies?
3. When is a proportion more useful than a raw count?

## Key Vocabulary
**Two-way table**: A table that organizes data by two categories.

**Frequency**: The number of times a category occurs.

**Relative frequency**: A frequency divided by a total to show part of a whole.

**Row total**: The total across one row.

**Column total**: The total down one column.

## Starting with Counts
Suppose a school surveyed students about whether they participated in a sport.

| Grade | Plays a sport | Does not play a sport | Total |
| --- | ---: | ---: | ---: |
| 7 | 18 | 12 | 30 |
| 8 | 14 | 6 | 20 |
| Total | 32 | 18 | 50 |

What does the `18` mean?

It means:

- Grade 7 students
- who play a sport

What does the `32` mean?

It is the column total for students who play a sport across both grades.

The counts are the starting point, but not always the best comparison tool.

## Worked Example 1: Finding a Row Relative Frequency
Use the table above.

What fraction of Grade 7 students play a sport?

Use the Grade 7 row total as the whole because the question asks about Grade 7 students only.

`18 / 30 = 3 / 5 = 0.6`

So the row relative frequency is:

`0.6` or `60%`

Interpretation:

Among Grade 7 students, `60%` play a sport.

## Worked Example 2: Finding a Column Relative Frequency
Use the same table.

What fraction of students who play a sport are in Grade 8?

Now the whole is the "Plays a sport" column total:

`14 / 32 = 7 / 16`

As a decimal:

`7 / 16 = 0.4375`

So about `43.75%` of the students who play a sport are in Grade 8.

This example shows why the choice of denominator matters. Different questions can use the same table but require different totals.

## Worked Example 3: Why Relative Frequency Matters
Which grade is more likely to play a sport?

If you compare raw counts only:

- Grade 7: `18`
- Grade 8: `14`

It might look like Grade 7 is more involved in sports.

But the grades are not the same size.

Compare row relative frequencies instead:

- Grade 7: `18 / 30 = 0.6`
- Grade 8: `14 / 20 = 0.7`

Now the comparison is fair.

Grade 8 is more likely to play a sport because `70%` is greater than `60%`.

## Worked Example 4: Completing a Table and Interpreting It
Suppose a table tracks whether students prefer reading fiction or nonfiction.

| Preference | Grade 7 | Grade 8 | Total |
| --- | ---: | ---: | ---: |
| Fiction | 16 | 9 | 25 |
| Nonfiction | 8 | ? | 15 |
| Total | 24 | 16 | 40 |

Find the missing Grade 8 nonfiction value.

Use the Nonfiction row:

`8 + ? = 15`

`? = 7`

Check with the Grade 8 column:

`9 + 7 = 16`

Now ask:

What fraction of Grade 8 students prefer nonfiction?

`7 / 16`

That is the correct comparison because the question is about Grade 8 students as the whole group.

## Common Mistake: Using the Grand Total for Every Relative Frequency
Students often divide by the total number of all students even when the question is about one row or one column.

Example:

Question: "What fraction of Grade 8 students prefer nonfiction?"

Wrong calculation:

`7 / 40`

Why is this wrong?

`40` is the grand total, but the question asks about Grade 8 students only.

Correct calculation:

`7 / 16`

Always decide what the whole group is before dividing.

## Summary
Two-way tables help organize categorical data, but Grade 7 work goes beyond reading counts.

To use a two-way table well:

1. read the matching row and column carefully
2. find the correct total for the question
3. compute a relative frequency when comparing groups
4. use proportions when group sizes are different

Raw counts tell how many. Relative frequencies tell how common. Strong data reasoning knows when each one matters.
