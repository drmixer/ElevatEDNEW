# Proportional Relationships

## Learning Goal
- I can recognize whether a relationship is proportional.
- I can find and interpret the constant of proportionality.
- I can solve real-world problems using tables, equations, graphs, and unit rates.

## Introduction
In Grade 7, ratio reasoning becomes more formal. You are no longer only scaling simple comparisons. You are studying relationships that stay consistent across many values.

A proportional relationship has a stable multiplicative pattern. If one quantity doubles, the other quantity doubles. If one quantity is multiplied by `3`, the other is also multiplied by `3`.

That consistency can appear in several forms:

- a table with equivalent ratios
- a graph that passes through the origin
- an equation like `y = kx`
- a unit rate that stays constant

In this lesson, focus on three ideas:

1. How can I tell if the relationship is proportional?
2. What does the constant of proportionality mean in context?
3. Which representation helps me solve the problem most efficiently?

## Key Vocabulary
**Proportional relationship**: A relationship in which two quantities keep the same multiplicative comparison.

**Constant of proportionality**: The value `k` in the equation `y = kx`; it tells how much `y` goes with `1` unit of `x`.

**Unit rate**: A rate compared to `1` unit, such as `$3 per notebook`.

**Equivalent ratios**: Ratios that represent the same comparison because both parts were scaled by the same factor.

**Origin**: The point `(0, 0)` on a graph.

## What Makes a Relationship Proportional?
Suppose a table shows the cost of notebooks:

| Notebooks | Cost |
| --- | --- |
| 2 | 6 |
| 4 | 12 |
| 7 | 21 |
| 10 | 30 |

To test proportionality, compare the cost per notebook:

- `6 / 2 = 3`
- `12 / 4 = 3`
- `21 / 7 = 3`
- `30 / 10 = 3`

The unit rate stays constant at `$3 per notebook`, so the relationship is proportional.

That means:

- the ratios are equivalent
- the graph would be a straight line through the origin
- the equation is `y = 3x`

Now compare a non-proportional table:

| Notebooks | Cost |
| --- | --- |
| 2 | 6 |
| 4 | 13 |
| 7 | 21 |

The unit rates are not constant:

- `6 / 2 = 3`
- `13 / 4 = 3.25`
- `21 / 7 = 3`

Because the comparison changes, the relationship is not proportional.

## Worked Example 1: Finding the Constant of Proportionality
A car travels `180` miles in `3` hours at a constant speed. What is the constant of proportionality if `x` is hours and `y` is miles?

Use the equation form `y = kx`.

Substitute the known values:

`180 = k(3)`

Solve for `k`:

`k = 180 / 3 = 60`

So the constant of proportionality is `60`.

Interpretation:

- the car travels `60 miles per hour`
- for each increase of `1` hour, the distance increases by `60` miles

The constant is not just a number. It has meaning in the context.

## Worked Example 2: Connecting a Table to an Equation
Suppose a babysitter earns `$24` for `3` hours of work.

Find the constant of proportionality:

`24 / 3 = 8`

So the unit rate is `$8 per hour`.

Write the equation:

`y = 8x`

where:

- `x` = hours
- `y` = dollars earned

Now use the equation to predict the pay for `6` hours:

`y = 8(6) = 48`

So the babysitter earns `$48`.

This is the power of proportional relationships: once you know the constant, you can generate any matching value.

## Graphing a Proportional Relationship
If a relationship is proportional, its graph:

- is a straight line
- passes through `(0, 0)`

Why does it pass through the origin?

If you buy `0` notebooks, the cost is `$0`. If you travel for `0` hours, the distance is `0` miles. The relationship starts from nothing because the output depends directly on the input.

Example:

If `y = 4x`, points on the graph include:

- `(0, 0)`
- `(1, 4)`
- `(2, 8)`
- `(5, 20)`

The slope and the constant of proportionality match in this situation because the line goes through the origin.

## Worked Example 3: Solving a Multi-Step Percent Problem
A store marks down a jacket by `25%`. The original price is `$80`. What is the sale price?

This is still ratio reasoning because percent compares a number to `100`.

Step 1: Find the amount of the discount.

`25% of 80 = 0.25 x 80 = 20`

Step 2: Subtract the discount from the original price.

`80 - 20 = 60`

So the sale price is `$60`.

You can also reason proportionally:

- if `100%` of the price is `$80`
- then `75%` of the price remains after the discount
- `0.75 x 80 = 60`

Both methods rely on proportional thinking.

## Common Mistake: Looking for Equal Differences Instead of Equal Ratios
Students often test proportionality by checking whether the numbers increase by the same amount.

That is not enough.

Look at this table:

| x | y |
| --- | --- |
| 1 | 4 |
| 2 | 7 |
| 3 | 10 |

The `y` values increase by `3` each time, but the ratios are:

- `4 / 1 = 4`
- `7 / 2 = 3.5`
- `10 / 3` is not `4`

This relationship is linear, but it is not proportional.

The key difference:

- proportional relationships have equal ratios and pass through the origin
- some non-proportional linear relationships have equal differences but not equal ratios

## Compare the Representations
When solving a proportional relationship, ask what form helps most.

- Use a **table** when you want to see several equivalent pairs.
- Use a **unit rate** when the question asks for "per `1`."
- Use an **equation** when you want to calculate quickly.
- Use a **graph** when you need to visualize the pattern or compare relationships.

Strong proportional thinkers can move between these forms without losing the meaning of the constant.

## Summary
Proportional relationships are built on a constant multiplicative comparison.

To check whether a relationship is proportional:

1. test whether the unit rate stays constant
2. see whether the equation can be written as `y = kx`
3. check whether the graph is a straight line through the origin

The constant of proportionality tells how much output goes with `1` unit of input. Once you understand that constant in context, the whole relationship becomes easier to solve and explain.
