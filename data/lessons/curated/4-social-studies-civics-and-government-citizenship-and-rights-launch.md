# Citizenship & Rights Launch Lesson

## Learning Goal
- I can identify who created a civic source, when it was written, and why it matters.
- I can use details from the First Amendment to explain what rights it protects.
- I can separate what the source directly says from what I can reasonably infer.

## Introduction
Imagine that a principal announced, "Students may not share opinions about school rules, gather signatures for a change, or meet peacefully to ask for a safer crosswalk." Would that feel fair? How would students explain why those actions should still be allowed?

In this lesson, you will study a real primary source from the Bill of Rights: the First Amendment to the United States Constitution. You will look closely at the words of the source, figure out what rights it protects, and explain what the source suggests about the kind of government the writers wanted to build.

## Key Vocabulary
**Amendment**: A change or addition made to an important document or law.

**Primary source**: An original document or firsthand piece of evidence from the time being studied.

**Petition**: A formal request in which people ask a government or leader to fix a problem or make a change.

**Assemble**: To gather together in a group for a peaceful reason.

**Inference**: A conclusion based on evidence and reasoning, not just a guess.

## Source Focus: The First Amendment
The First Amendment was added to the Bill of Rights in 1791, only a few years after the United States became a new country. The writers wanted to limit what the federal government could do to citizens.

This source matters because it shows a promise the new government made. It says that the government may not take away certain freedoms.

Here is the key part of the source:

> "Congress shall make no law ... abridging the freedom of speech, or of the press; or the right of the people peaceably to assemble, and to petition the Government for a redress of grievances."

Because this is the actual wording of the amendment, it is a primary source. We are not reading someone else's summary. We are looking at the source itself.

## What The Source Directly Says
When historians and civics students read a source, the first step is to notice what the source clearly states.

This amendment directly names several rights:

- freedom of speech
- freedom of the press
- the right to assemble peacefully
- the right to petition the government

Those are observations because they come straight from the words on the page.

A strong observation sounds like this:

"The source says people have the right to speak, gather peacefully, and petition the government."

A weak observation would be:

"The source proves every problem in the country was solved."

That claim is too broad because the source does not say that.

## Observation Versus Inference
After we identify what the source says, we can make a careful inference about why it was written.

One reasonable inference is that the writers worried a powerful government might try to silence criticism or stop citizens from gathering to ask for change. The evidence for that inference is the list of protections in the amendment. If the writers chose to protect speech, assembly, and petitions, those rights must have seemed important and worth defending.

Notice the difference:

- **Observation**: The amendment protects speech, assembly, and petitions.
- **Inference**: The writers believed citizens needed ways to criticize the government and ask for change.

The inference is reasonable because it grows from the words in the source. It is not random. It stays close to the evidence.

## Perspective And Purpose
This source was written by lawmakers in the early United States. Their purpose was not to tell a story about one event. Their purpose was to set rules for government power.

That perspective matters. The amendment shows what freedoms the government promised not to block. It also reminds us that one source has limits. The amendment tells us what the law said, but we would need other sources to learn whether everyone in 1791 was treated fairly or whether those rights were always protected equally in real life.

That is another important source-analysis move:

- use this source to learn what rule was written
- use other sources to learn how people experienced that rule

## Worked Example: Claim, Evidence, And Reasoning
Suppose students in a town gather signatures asking the mayor to add a crosswalk near their school. Which right from the First Amendment best fits that action?

A strong answer would use claim, evidence, and reasoning:

- **Claim**: The students are using the right to petition the government.
- **Evidence**: The source says people have "the right ... to petition the Government for a redress of grievances."
- **Reasoning**: Asking leaders to fix a safety problem is a formal request for change, so it matches the meaning of a petition.

We could also extend the example:

- if the students hold a peaceful rally, that connects to assembly
- if they write an opinion article for the local paper, that connects to freedom of the press

The key is to match the action to the exact words of the source.

## Why This Source Still Matters
The First Amendment is more than an old document. It still shapes civic life today. People use these rights when they speak at meetings, write editorials, gather peacefully, or ask leaders to solve a problem.

When you analyze a civic source, ask:

1. Who created it and when?
2. What does it directly say?
3. What can I infer from the evidence in the source?
4. How does the source connect to the actions people take in civic life?

## Summary
The First Amendment is a primary source from 1791 that protects important civic rights, including speech, assembly, and petitions.

In this lesson, you used one real source to practice three important moves:

- identify what the source directly says
- make a careful inference about why the source was written
- connect a civic action to evidence from the source

That is the history and civics habit to reuse in the next source you study: stay close to the words, separate observation from inference, and support every claim with evidence.
