# Volume & Surface Area

## Learning Goal
- I can explain volume as the number of cubic units that fill a solid figure.
- I can find the volume of a rectangular prism using `length x width x height`.
- I can distinguish between the space inside a prism and the area on its outside faces.

## Introduction
When you measure a solid figure, there are two different ideas you might be measuring.

- how much space is inside the figure
- how much area covers the outside of the figure

In Grade 6, the main focus is volume of rectangular prisms. But it is important to understand that volume and surface area are not the same measurement.

Volume measures inside space in cubic units. Surface area measures the outside covering in square units.

In this lesson, focus on three questions:

1. What does volume represent?
2. Why does `length x width x height` work for a rectangular prism?
3. How is volume different from surface area?

## Key Vocabulary
**Volume**: The amount of space inside a three-dimensional figure.

**Rectangular prism**: A solid with rectangular faces.

**Cubic unit**: A unit used to measure volume, such as `cm^3` or `in^3`.

**Face**: A flat surface of a solid figure.

**Surface area**: The total area of all the outside faces of a solid figure.

## Why Volume Uses Cubic Units
Imagine filling a rectangular box with unit cubes.

If each cube is `1` unit by `1` unit by `1` unit, then each cube has volume `1 cubic unit`.

The volume of the box is the total number of unit cubes needed to fill it with no gaps and no overlaps.

This is why volume is measured in cubic units instead of square units.

## Worked Example 1: Counting Layers
A rectangular prism has:

- length `4`
- width `3`
- height `2`

Think of the base first.

One layer has:

`4 x 3 = 12`

So each layer contains `12` unit cubes.

Now count the layers.

The prism has height `2`, so there are `2` layers.

Total volume:

`12 x 2 = 24`

So the volume is:

`24 cubic units`

## Worked Example 2: Using the Formula
The volume formula for a rectangular prism is:

`V = l x w x h`

A prism has:

- length `6`
- width `5`
- height `4`

Substitute:

`V = 6 x 5 x 4`

`V = 30 x 4`

`V = 120`

So the volume is:

`120 cubic units`

This matches the idea of counting cubes in layers, but the formula is faster.

## Worked Example 3: Missing Dimension
A rectangular prism has volume `72 cubic units`.

Its length is `6` and width is `3`.

Find the height.

Start with the formula:

`72 = 6 x 3 x h`

Multiply the known dimensions:

`72 = 18h`

Divide by `18`:

`h = 4`

So the height is `4 units`.

This works because the volume formula can also help you find an unknown dimension.

## Volume Versus Surface Area
Suppose a prism has dimensions:

- length `4`
- width `2`
- height `3`

Its volume tells how much space is inside.

Its surface area tells how much area covers the outside faces.

These are different measurements because:

- volume uses cubic units
- surface area uses square units

A box could have a large surface area but still a smaller volume than another box, depending on its dimensions.

## Common Mistake: Mixing Square Units and Cubic Units
Students sometimes find a volume correctly but label it with square units.

Example:

If the volume is `24`, writing `24 square units` is incorrect.

Volume must be written in cubic units because the figure has three dimensions.

Use this check:

- area of a flat face -> square units
- volume of a solid -> cubic units

## Worked Example 4: Comparing Two Prisms
Prism `A` has dimensions `5 x 2 x 3`.

Prism `B` has dimensions `4 x 4 x 2`.

Find both volumes.

Prism `A`:

`V = 5 x 2 x 3 = 30`

Prism `B`:

`V = 4 x 4 x 2 = 32`

So Prism `B` has the greater volume, even though the dimensions look different.

This shows why the formula is useful. You cannot rely on one dimension alone.

## Summary
Grade 6 volume work focuses on rectangular prisms and cubic units.

To solve well:

1. identify the prism's length, width, and height
2. multiply the three dimensions
3. label the answer in cubic units
4. keep volume separate from surface area

Volume measures the space inside a solid. Surface area measures the outside covering. Strong geometry reasoning keeps those two ideas distinct.
