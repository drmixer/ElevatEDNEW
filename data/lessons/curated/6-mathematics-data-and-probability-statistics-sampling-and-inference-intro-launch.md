# Sampling & Inference

## Learning Goal
- I can explain the difference between a population and a sample.
- I can describe whether a sample seems useful for learning about a larger group.
- I can use sample data to make a careful statement about a population.

## Introduction
Sometimes it is easy to collect data from every member of a group. But often the group is too large.

For example, it would take too long to ask every student in a large district what lunch they prefer. Instead, you might ask a smaller group and study that data.

That smaller group is called a sample.

In Grade 6, the key idea is that a sample can help us learn about a population, but only if we are careful about what the sample actually represents.

In this lesson, focus on three questions:

1. What is the population?
2. What is the sample?
3. When is it reasonable to use the sample to say something about the larger group?

## Key Vocabulary
**Population**: The entire group you want information about.

**Sample**: A smaller group chosen from the population.

**Survey**: A way of collecting information by asking questions.

**Data**: The information collected in a survey or study.

**Inference**: A conclusion about a population based on sample data.

## Population vs Sample
Suppose a school wants to know whether students prefer recess before lunch or after lunch.

If the question is about every student in the school, then:

- the population is all students in the school

If the principal asks `30` students from different grades, then:

- the sample is those `30` students

The sample is part of the population, not a separate group.

This distinction matters because the goal of sampling is to use part of the group to learn about the whole group.

## Worked Example 1: Identifying the Population and Sample
A pet store wants to know which type of pet food customers buy most often. The manager asks `25` customers during one afternoon.

What is the population?

The population is all customers of the pet store.

What is the sample?

The sample is the `25` customers who were asked.

Why is the sample smaller than the population?

Because it would be harder and slower to ask every customer.

## Worked Example 2: Using Sample Data
Suppose a sample of `40` students is surveyed about favorite school subject.

Results:

- math: `14`
- science: `10`
- ELA: `8`
- social studies: `8`

What can you say about the sample?

Math was the most common favorite subject in the sample.

Can you say that math is definitely the favorite subject of every student in the school?

No. The sample gives evidence, but not certainty.

A careful inference is:

"This sample suggests that math may be the most popular subject in the school."

That wording matches what the data can actually support.

## When a Sample Is More Useful
Some samples give better information than others.

Suppose one student asks only members of the basketball team which lunch is best.

Would that sample represent the whole school well?

Probably not.

Why?

Because the basketball team is only one small part of the school and may not reflect all students.

A stronger sample would include students from different grades, classes, and activities.

## Worked Example 3: Comparing Two Samples
Which sample is more useful for learning about all students in a school?

### Sample A
Ask `20` students from one art class.

### Sample B
Ask `20` students chosen from different grades during lunch.

Sample B is more useful because it includes a wider part of the school.

That does not make it perfect, but it is more likely to reflect the larger population.

## Common Mistake: Treating a Sample Result as a Guaranteed Fact
Students sometimes see a sample result and state it as if it must be true for everyone.

Example:

"Twelve out of twenty surveyed students like pizza best, so everyone at school likes pizza best."

That conclusion is too strong.

A better statement is:

"The sample suggests pizza may be the most popular choice."

An inference should sound supported, not absolute.

## Worked Example 4: Writing a Careful Inference
A library surveys `50` visitors about whether they prefer mystery books or fantasy books.

Results:

- mystery: `18`
- fantasy: `32`

What is a reasonable inference?

"Based on this sample, fantasy books may be more popular than mystery books among library visitors."

Why is this reasonable?

- it uses the sample data
- it does not claim certainty
- it stays connected to the population being studied

## Summary
Sampling helps us learn about large groups by studying smaller groups.

To reason carefully:

1. identify the population
2. identify the sample
3. decide whether the sample seems useful for the question
4. make an inference that matches the evidence without overstating it

In Grade 6, strong sampling work means understanding that a sample can give clues about a population, but those clues need careful interpretation.
