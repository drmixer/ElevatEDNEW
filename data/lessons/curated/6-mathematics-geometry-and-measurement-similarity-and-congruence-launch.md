# Similarity & Congruence

## Learning Goal
- I can decide whether two figures have the same shape and size.
- I can compare side lengths and angles to test whether figures are congruent.
- I can explain why figures that match exactly are different from figures that are only scaled copies.

## Introduction
Some pairs of figures match exactly. Other pairs look alike but are different sizes.

That difference matters in geometry.

If two figures have the same shape and the same size, they are **congruent**. If they have the same shape but one is larger or smaller, they are not congruent even if they look similar.

In Grade 6, the main job is to compare figures carefully instead of guessing from appearance.

As you read, focus on two questions:

1. Do the matching sides have the same lengths?
2. Do the matching angles have the same measures?

## Key Vocabulary
**Congruent**: Figures that have the same shape and the same size.

**Similar**: Figures that have the same shape but may have different sizes.

**Corresponding parts**: Sides or angles that match in the same relative position.

**Angle measure**: The size of an angle in degrees.

**Scale**: The size relationship between two similar figures.

## What It Means To Be Congruent
Two figures are congruent if every pair of corresponding sides has the same length and every pair of corresponding angles has the same measure.

That means one figure can fit exactly on top of the other.

For example, if two rectangles both have:

- length `5`
- width `3`

then they are congruent.

If one rectangle is `5 by 3` and another is `10 by 6`, they have the same shape but not the same size. They are not congruent.

## Worked Example 1: Checking for Congruence
Triangle `A` has side lengths:

- `4`
- `5`
- `6`

Triangle `B` has side lengths:

- `4`
- `5`
- `6`

We want to decide whether the triangles are congruent.

Step 1: Compare the corresponding side lengths.

- `4 = 4`
- `5 = 5`
- `6 = 6`

Step 2: Interpret the comparison.

All corresponding side lengths match.

If the corresponding angles also match, then the triangles are congruent.

In this case, the side data already shows an exact size match, so the triangles are congruent.

## Worked Example 2: Same Shape But Different Size
Rectangle `P` has side lengths:

- `3`
- `7`

Rectangle `Q` has side lengths:

- `6`
- `14`

Step 1: Compare the side lengths.

- `6 / 3 = 2`
- `14 / 7 = 2`

Step 2: Interpret the ratios.

Each side in Rectangle `Q` is twice the matching side in Rectangle `P`.

That means the rectangles have the same shape, but Rectangle `Q` is larger.

So the rectangles are not congruent.

They are similar because the side lengths change by one constant scale.

## How To Compare Corresponding Parts
When you compare figures, the order matters.

You must match:

- shortest side to shortest side
- longest side to longest side
- top-left angle to top-left angle, or another consistent position

If you compare the wrong parts, the conclusion can be wrong.

This is why geometry problems often label the figures with matching vertices.

## Worked Example 3: Congruent Or Similar?
Figure `M` is a square with side length `5`.

Figure `N` is a square with side length `5`.

Figure `R` is a square with side length `8`.

Compare `M` and `N`.

They have:

- the same shape
- the same side lengths

So `M` and `N` are congruent.

Compare `M` and `R`.

They have:

- the same shape
- different side lengths

So `M` and `R` are similar, not congruent.

## Common Mistake: Judging Only By Appearance
Students sometimes decide two figures are congruent because they look alike.

That is not enough.

A drawing can be misleading if one figure is simply a scaled copy of the other.

Use this check:

- same shape and same size -> congruent
- same shape but different size -> similar

Measure or compare the corresponding parts before deciding.

## Worked Example 4: Using Angles and Sides Together
Two quadrilaterals each have angle measures:

- `90`
- `90`
- `90`
- `90`

One has side lengths `4` and `9`.

The other has side lengths `8` and `18`.

The angles match, so the shapes are both rectangles.

But the side lengths are not equal.

Instead:

- `8 / 4 = 2`
- `18 / 9 = 2`

So the figures are similar, not congruent.

Matching angles alone do not guarantee congruence. The size must match too.

## Summary
Similarity and congruence both compare figures, but they do not mean the same thing.

To solve these problems well:

1. match the corresponding sides and angles carefully
2. check whether side lengths are exactly equal or only proportional
3. decide whether the figures are congruent, similar, or neither
4. explain the decision using size and shape

In Grade 6, strong geometry reasoning means comparing figures with evidence instead of relying on how they seem to look.
