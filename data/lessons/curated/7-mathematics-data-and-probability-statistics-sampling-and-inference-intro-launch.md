# Sampling & Inference

## Learning Goal
- I can explain why random sampling is useful when making inferences about a population.
- I can decide whether a sample is likely to be representative of a population.
- I can use sample results to make a cautious inference about a larger group.

## Introduction
In Grade 7, sampling becomes more formal. It is not enough to say that a smaller group can tell us something about a larger group. We also need to think about how that smaller group was chosen.

A sample is most useful when it represents the population fairly.

That is why random sampling matters. If people are chosen in a biased way, the sample can give a misleading picture of the population.

In this lesson, focus on three questions:

1. What makes a sample representative?
2. Why is random sampling important?
3. What kind of inference is reasonable from a sample?

## Key Vocabulary
**Population**: The full group you want information about.

**Sample**: A smaller group chosen from the population.

**Random sample**: A sample selected so that members of the population have a fair chance of being chosen.

**Representative sample**: A sample that reflects the population reasonably well.

**Inference**: A conclusion about a population based on sample data.

## Why Random Sampling Matters
Suppose a school wants to know whether students support a later start time.

If the survey asks only students in first-period study hall, the results may be biased because that group may not reflect the whole school.

A stronger method is to choose students randomly from different parts of the school population.

Random sampling does not guarantee a perfect sample, but it helps prevent one group from being overrepresented just because it was easier to reach.

## Worked Example 1: Choosing the Better Sample
Which sample is more likely to represent the whole school?

### Sample A
Survey `30` students leaving basketball practice.

### Sample B
Use a student list and randomly choose `30` students from the school.

Sample B is better.

Why?

- it gives students a fairer chance of being selected
- it is less tied to one activity group
- it is more likely to reflect the full school population

This is the main advantage of random sampling.

## Worked Example 2: Interpreting a Random Sample
A random sample of `100` students is asked whether they would join an after-school homework club.

Results:

- yes: `64`
- no: `36`

What is a reasonable inference about the school?

A strong inference is:

"About `64%` of the school's students may be willing to join the homework club."

Why is this more reasonable than saying "Exactly `64%` of all students will join"?

Because the sample gives evidence, not certainty.

## Worked Example 3: Why a Nonrandom Sample Can Mislead
A cafeteria wants to know which lunch option students prefer.

The survey is given only to students who buy lunch on Tuesdays.

Why might this sample be weak?

- it misses students who bring lunch from home
- it misses students absent on Tuesdays
- it may overrepresent one pattern of behavior

Even if the sample size is not tiny, the method of selection still matters.

A weak sample can produce weak inferences.

## Representative Does Not Mean Perfect
Students sometimes think a representative sample must match the population exactly in every detail.

That is not realistic.

A representative sample is useful because it is reasonably similar to the population, especially when selected randomly.

It is still possible for sample results to differ somewhat from the true population results.

The goal is not perfection. The goal is fairness and usefulness.

## Worked Example 4: Comparing Sample Conclusions
Two samples are used to estimate how many students read for fun each week.

### Sample A
Randomly select `50` students from the school.

### Sample B
Ask `50` students in the library after school.

Which sample is more likely to lead to a trustworthy inference?

Sample A.

Why?

Students already in the library may be more likely than average to read for fun, so Sample B may overestimate reading interest.

The context of how the sample is collected matters just as much as the numbers in the sample.

## Common Mistake: Thinking a Large Sample Is Always Fair
Students sometimes assume:

"If the sample is big, it must be representative."

That is not always true.

A large biased sample can still give misleading results.

For example, asking `300` members of one sports team about school policy still does not represent the whole student body well.

Sample size matters, but selection method matters too.

## Summary
Grade 7 sampling focuses on fairness in how the sample is chosen.

To judge a sample well:

1. identify the population
2. examine how the sample was selected
3. decide whether the sample is likely to be representative
4. make a cautious inference that matches the evidence

Random samples are useful because they improve the chance that the sample reflects the larger population. Strong inferences begin with strong sampling methods.
