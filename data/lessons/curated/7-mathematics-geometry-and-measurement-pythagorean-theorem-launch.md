# Pythagorean Theorem

## Learning Goal
- I can identify when the Pythagorean Theorem applies.
- I can solve for a missing side length in a right triangle.
- I can use the theorem in geometric and real-world problems and explain each step.

## Introduction
In Grade 7 geometry, many shapes can be broken into right triangles.

That matters because right triangles have a reliable side-length relationship:

`a^2 + b^2 = c^2`

This is the Pythagorean Theorem.

The theorem is useful when:

- a triangle is right
- two side lengths are known
- the third side length must be found

The hardest part is often not the arithmetic. It is identifying the triangle correctly and labeling the hypotenuse before solving.

## Key Vocabulary
**Right triangle**: A triangle with one `90` degree angle.

**Legs**: The two sides that meet at the right angle.

**Hypotenuse**: The side opposite the right angle; it is always the longest side.

**Pythagorean Theorem**: For a right triangle with legs `a` and `b` and hypotenuse `c`, `a^2 + b^2 = c^2`.

**Square root**: A value that, when multiplied by itself, gives the original number.

## How To Set Up the Theorem
Before substituting numbers, decide which side is the hypotenuse.

Use this checklist:

1. Find the right angle.
2. Look at the side across from it.
3. Label that side as `c`.

The other two sides are the legs, so they become `a` and `b`.

This is the part many students rush through, and that is where mistakes begin.

## Worked Example 1: Finding the Hypotenuse
A right triangle has legs `7` and `24`. Find the hypotenuse.

Because `7` and `24` are the legs, the unknown side is `c`.

Set up the theorem:

`7^2 + 24^2 = c^2`

Square the legs:

`49 + 576 = c^2`

Add:

`625 = c^2`

Take the square root:

`c = 25`

So the hypotenuse is `25`.

## Worked Example 2: Finding a Missing Leg
A right triangle has hypotenuse `15` and one leg `9`. Find the other leg.

Set up the equation:

`9^2 + b^2 = 15^2`

Square the known values:

`81 + b^2 = 225`

Subtract `81`:

`b^2 = 144`

Take the square root:

`b = 12`

So the missing leg is `12`.

Check:

- `12` is shorter than `15`
- that makes sense because `15` is the hypotenuse

## Worked Example 3: The Diagonal of a Rectangle
A computer screen is `8` inches tall and `15` inches wide. What is the diagonal length?

The height and width form the legs of a right triangle.

Set up the theorem:

`8^2 + 15^2 = c^2`

`64 + 225 = c^2`

`289 = c^2`

`c = 17`

So the diagonal is `17` inches.

This is a common use of the theorem: finding a straight-line distance across a rectangular shape.

## Worked Example 4: A Non-Whole-Number Answer
A right triangle has legs `5` and `11`. Find the hypotenuse.

Set up the theorem:

`5^2 + 11^2 = c^2`

`25 + 121 = c^2`

`146 = c^2`

Take the square root:

`c = square root of 146`

This does not simplify to a whole number.

That is okay.

The correct answer is:

`square root of 146`

or about `12.1`

depending on whether the problem wants an exact value or an approximation.

## Using the Theorem in a Coordinate Grid
Point `A(1, 2)` and point `B(6, 14)` lie on a grid. What is the straight-line distance between them?

Find the horizontal and vertical changes:

- horizontal change: `6 - 1 = 5`
- vertical change: `14 - 2 = 12`

These form the legs of a right triangle.

Apply the theorem:

`5^2 + 12^2 = c^2`

`25 + 144 = c^2`

`169 = c^2`

`c = 13`

So the distance is `13` units.

## Common Mistake: Squaring the Hypotenuse Incorrectly
Students sometimes see a triangle with sides `9`, `12`, and `15` and write:

`15 = 9^2 + 12^2`

That is not correct because the theorem compares squared lengths.

The correct relationship is:

`9^2 + 12^2 = 15^2`

Both sides of the equation are areas of squares built on the triangle's sides. That is why the exponents matter.

## Summary
The Pythagorean Theorem applies to right triangles:

`a^2 + b^2 = c^2`

To solve well:

1. identify the right triangle
2. label the hypotenuse correctly
3. square the side lengths carefully
4. solve for the unknown side and check whether the answer fits the geometry

When you slow down at the setup stage, the rest of the problem becomes much easier.
