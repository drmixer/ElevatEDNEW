# Scatterplots & Correlation

## Learning Goal
- I can read ordered pairs from a scatterplot.
- I can describe whether a scatterplot shows an upward trend, downward trend, or no clear trend.
- I can use points on a scatterplot to answer questions about a data set.

## Introduction
A scatterplot shows pairs of numerical data on a coordinate plane.

Each point represents one pair of values, such as:

- hours studied and quiz score
- shoe size and height
- number of books read and minutes spent reading

In Grade 6, the most important work is learning how to read the points accurately and notice whether the data seems to move in a pattern.

In this lesson, focus on three questions:

1. What does one point on a scatterplot represent?
2. Does the overall pattern seem to rise, fall, or stay mixed?
3. What can I say from the data without guessing beyond the graph?

## Key Vocabulary
**Scatterplot**: A graph that shows pairs of numerical data as points.

**Ordered pair**: Two numbers written as `(x, y)` that give a point's location.

**Trend**: The general direction the data points seem to move.

**Positive trend**: A pattern in which larger `x` values usually go with larger `y` values.

**Negative trend**: A pattern in which larger `x` values usually go with smaller `y` values.

## What One Point Means
Suppose a scatterplot shows the relationship between hours studied and quiz score.

If one point is `(3, 80)`, that means:

- `3` hours studied
- quiz score of `80`

The point does **not** mean:

- every student who studied `3` hours scored `80`
- one number is added to the other

It represents one data pair.

Reading scatterplots starts with interpreting each point correctly.

## Worked Example 1: Reading Points from a Scatterplot
Imagine a scatterplot with these points:

- `(1, 65)`
- `(2, 70)`
- `(3, 80)`
- `(4, 85)`
- `(5, 90)`

What does the point `(4, 85)` mean?

It means:

- the student studied `4` hours
- and scored `85`

What does the point `(1, 65)` mean?

It means:

- the student studied `1` hour
- and scored `65`

Each point is one complete data record.

## Worked Example 2: Describing the Overall Trend
Look at the same data:

- `(1, 65)`
- `(2, 70)`
- `(3, 80)`
- `(4, 85)`
- `(5, 90)`

As the number of study hours increases, the quiz scores also increase.

This suggests a positive trend.

A strong description is:

"The scatterplot shows an upward pattern. Students who studied more hours usually had higher quiz scores."

That statement describes the graph without claiming that studying more always guarantees a higher score.

## Worked Example 3: A Negative Trend
Suppose a scatterplot shows outside temperature and number of hot chocolates sold:

- `(40, 18)`
- `(50, 14)`
- `(60, 10)`
- `(70, 6)`

As temperature increases, hot chocolate sales decrease.

This suggests a negative trend.

A strong description is:

"The scatterplot shows a downward pattern. Warmer temperatures usually go with fewer hot chocolates sold."

## When There Is No Clear Trend
Not every scatterplot has an obvious pattern.

Suppose the points are:

- `(1, 7)`
- `(2, 3)`
- `(3, 9)`
- `(4, 4)`
- `(5, 8)`

These points jump around without a clear upward or downward direction.

That means there is no clear trend.

A strong reader does not force a pattern if the graph does not support it.

## Common Mistake: Focusing on Only One Point
Students sometimes look at one point and decide the whole graph's trend.

Example:

If most points rise but one point is lower, that does not automatically make the graph a negative trend.

Trend describes the overall pattern, not one single point.

Always look across the whole scatterplot before deciding.

## Worked Example 4: Answering Questions from the Data
Use this data set:

- `(2, 10)`
- `(3, 12)`
- `(4, 15)`
- `(5, 17)`

Question:

Which student read for `4` hours, and how many pages did that student read?

The matching point is `(4, 15)`.

So the student who read for `4` hours read `15` pages.

Question:

Does the graph suggest that reading longer is connected to reading more pages?

Yes. The points show a positive trend because larger `x` values usually go with larger `y` values.

## Summary
Scatterplots help show relationships between two numerical variables.

To read them well:

1. interpret each point as one ordered pair
2. look for the overall pattern of the points
3. describe whether the trend is positive, negative, or unclear
4. answer questions using the actual data shown

In Grade 6, good scatterplot reading means being accurate about what the points say and careful not to claim more than the graph supports.
