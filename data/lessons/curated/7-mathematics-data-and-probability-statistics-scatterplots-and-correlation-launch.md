# Scatterplots & Correlation

## Learning Goal
- I can interpret whether a scatterplot shows a positive association, negative association, or no association.
- I can describe the strength of a pattern by looking at how closely the points follow a trend.
- I can use a scatterplot to make cautious predictions about data values.

## Introduction
In Grade 7, scatterplots are not just about reading points. They are also about describing relationships between variables.

When two numerical variables seem connected, the scatterplot may show an association:

- positive
- negative
- or no clear association

The graph can also show whether the pattern is strong or weak.

In this lesson, focus on three questions:

1. What kind of association does the scatterplot show?
2. How strong does the pattern appear?
3. What predictions are reasonable, and what predictions go too far beyond the data?

## Key Vocabulary
**Scatterplot**: A graph showing paired numerical data as points.

**Association**: A relationship between two variables shown by a pattern in the data.

**Positive association**: A pattern in which larger `x` values tend to go with larger `y` values.

**Negative association**: A pattern in which larger `x` values tend to go with smaller `y` values.

**Prediction**: An estimate based on the pattern in the data.

## From Trend to Association
Suppose a scatterplot compares time spent practicing an instrument and performance score.

Points might include:

- `(1, 60)`
- `(2, 68)`
- `(3, 74)`
- `(4, 83)`
- `(5, 88)`

As practice time increases, the score tends to increase.

This suggests a positive association.

The word "association" matters because it reminds us that the graph shows a pattern in the data. It does not automatically prove cause in every possible situation.

## Worked Example 1: Identifying Positive, Negative, or No Association
Consider three data sets.

### Data Set A
- `(1, 55)`
- `(2, 63)`
- `(3, 71)`
- `(4, 79)`

This shows a positive association because the points rise from left to right.

### Data Set B
- `(1, 18)`
- `(2, 15)`
- `(3, 11)`
- `(4, 8)`

This shows a negative association because the points fall from left to right.

### Data Set C
- `(1, 7)`
- `(2, 12)`
- `(3, 8)`
- `(4, 13)`

This shows little or no clear association because the points do not follow one obvious direction.

## Worked Example 2: Describing Strength
Two scatterplots can both show positive association, but one can be stronger than the other.

Suppose Graph A has points close to an upward path:

- `(1, 52)`
- `(2, 58)`
- `(3, 64)`
- `(4, 69)`

Suppose Graph B also rises overall, but the points are more spread out:

- `(1, 50)`
- `(2, 67)`
- `(3, 55)`
- `(4, 73)`

Graph A shows a stronger association because the points stay closer to one pattern.

Graph B still suggests a positive association, but it is weaker.

Strength depends on how tightly the points cluster around the trend, not just whether they rise or fall.

## Worked Example 3: Making a Prediction
Suppose a scatterplot shows these pairs for hours practiced and performance score:

- `(1, 62)`
- `(2, 68)`
- `(3, 74)`
- `(4, 80)`

What score would you predict for a student who practiced `5` hours?

The pattern rises by about `6` points each hour, so a reasonable prediction is about:

`86`

This is a prediction, not an exact answer. It is reasonable because `5` hours is close to the data already shown.

## Interpolation vs Going Too Far
Predictions are safer when they stay near the existing data.

If the scatterplot only includes `1` to `5` study hours, predicting a score for `4.5` or `6` hours may be reasonable.

Predicting a score for `20` hours is much less reliable because that is far beyond the data shown.

Strong data readers know the difference between:

- using the visible pattern
- stretching the pattern too far

## Common Mistake: Treating Association as a Perfect Rule
Students sometimes say:

"If the graph shows positive association, then every larger `x` must have a larger `y`."

That is too strict.

Scatterplot association is about a general pattern, not a perfect rule for every single point.

A few points may be higher or lower than expected while the overall graph still shows positive association.

## Worked Example 4: Writing a Good Interpretation
Suppose a scatterplot compares absences and final grade. The points show a downward pattern.

A weak interpretation is:

"Absences ruin grades."

A stronger interpretation is:

"The scatterplot shows a negative association between absences and final grade. In this data set, students with more absences tended to have lower grades."

The stronger version:

- names the type of association
- stays connected to the data set
- avoids making a claim that is too absolute

## Summary
Grade 7 scatterplots help describe relationships between two numerical variables.

To interpret them well:

1. decide whether the association is positive, negative, or unclear
2. describe whether the pattern looks stronger or weaker
3. make predictions only when they stay close to the data
4. write conclusions that match the graph without overstating them

Scatterplots are useful because they show patterns, not because they produce perfect rules.
