# Plate Tectonics

## Learning Goal
- I can explain how evidence from seafloor spreading, magnetic stripes, earthquakes, volcanoes, and GPS supports plate tectonics.
- I can compare convergent, divergent, transform, and hotspot patterns using evidence and mechanism.
- I can make a scientific claim about a tectonic setting and support it with evidence and reasoning.

## Introduction
Earth's surface can feel solid and unchanging, but the evidence says otherwise. Continents move. Ocean crust forms and spreads. Earthquakes and volcanoes cluster in organized patterns instead of appearing randomly.

Plate tectonics explains these patterns by describing Earth's lithosphere as a set of moving plates. The theory is powerful because it does more than name features. It connects observations, measurements, and mechanisms into one model.

In this lesson, ask:

1. What evidence shows that plates move?
2. How do different plate interactions create different surface patterns?
3. How can I use evidence and reasoning to identify a tectonic setting?

## Key Vocabulary
**Lithosphere**: Earth's rigid outer layer, broken into tectonic plates.

**Asthenosphere**: The softer layer below the lithosphere that allows plates to move slowly.

**Seafloor spreading**: The process in which new ocean crust forms at a mid-ocean ridge and moves outward.

**Magnetic reversal**: A switch in Earth's magnetic field that can be recorded in cooling ocean crust.

**Subduction**: The process in which one tectonic plate sinks beneath another.

**Hotspot**: A place where magma rises from deep within the mantle and creates volcanoes even away from a plate boundary.

## Evidence That Plates Move
Plate tectonics is supported by several kinds of evidence that fit together.

Important patterns include:

- ocean-floor rocks are youngest near mid-ocean ridges and older farther away
- magnetic stripes on both sides of a ridge form a mirrored pattern
- earthquake and volcano zones trace narrow plate boundaries
- GPS stations show plates moving a few centimeters each year
- some volcanic island chains become older in one direction, showing a plate moving over a hotspot

Each clue matters, but the full theory is strongest because the clues agree with one another.

## How Plate Motion Creates Patterns
Plate motion is driven by energy from Earth's interior. Heat causes movement in the mantle, and that movement helps transfer plates across the surface. Scientists also describe forces such as ridge push and slab pull.

Different interactions create different evidence patterns:

- **Divergent boundaries** form ridges, new crust, and symmetrical spreading patterns.
- **Convergent boundaries** can form trenches, volcanic arcs, mountains, and deep earthquake zones.
- **Transform boundaries** often create fault lines and frequent earthquakes with little volcanic activity.
- **Hotspots** can create volcano chains that are not centered on a plate boundary.

The scientific task is not just naming the boundary. It is matching the evidence pattern to the mechanism.

## Worked Example 1: Reading Seafloor-Spreading Evidence
Scientists map a mid-ocean ridge and observe:

- the youngest basalt is nearest the ridge
- rock ages increase with distance from the ridge on both sides
- magnetic stripes appear in matching bands on the east and west sides

What does this evidence suggest?

A strong claim is:

"This pattern supports seafloor spreading at a divergent boundary."

Evidence:

- youngest rock near the ridge shows new crust is forming there
- older rock farther away shows crust is moving outward over time
- mirrored magnetic stripes show that both sides recorded the same sequence of magnetic reversals while spreading apart

Reasoning:

If magma rises at the ridge, cools into new crust, and moves outward in both directions, scientists would expect exactly this age and magnetic pattern.

## Worked Example 2: Identifying a Subduction Zone
At another location, scientists observe:

- a deep ocean trench
- a chain of volcanoes on a nearby continent
- earthquakes that become deeper farther inland

What tectonic setting best explains the pattern?

A strong answer is:

"This location is most likely a convergent boundary with subduction."

Reasoning:

- the trench forms where one plate bends downward
- earthquakes at increasing depths trace the sinking plate
- volcanoes form when material associated with the subducting plate causes melting and magma rises

This is stronger than saying only, "There are volcanoes, so plates must be colliding." The full evidence pattern explains the mechanism.

## Worked Example 3: Hotspot Track or Plate Boundary?
Suppose an island chain sits in the middle of an ocean plate. The youngest volcano is at one end, and the islands become older with distance from that point.

Is this most likely a plate boundary?

Not necessarily.

A stronger explanation is:

"The pattern suggests a hotspot track rather than a plate boundary."

Evidence:

- the volcanoes form a line through the middle of a plate
- island ages increase steadily in one direction

Reasoning:

If the hotspot stays mostly in one place while the plate moves over it, new volcanoes form above the hotspot and older islands are carried away. That produces an age-ordered chain.

This example matters because not every volcanic pattern comes from the same tectonic process.

## Common Mistake: Matching One Clue Instead of the Whole Pattern
Students often see one clue and jump to a conclusion:

"There are earthquakes, so it must be a convergent boundary."

That is too weak because earthquakes happen at divergent, convergent, and transform boundaries.

A stronger method is:

1. list the full set of observations
2. identify which boundary or process explains all of them together
3. connect the evidence to a mechanism, not just a vocabulary word

Scientific explanations improve when they combine claim, evidence, and reasoning instead of relying on one feature.

## Summary
Plate tectonics explains Earth's surface patterns by connecting evidence to moving plates and the mechanisms that drive them.

Key ideas from this lesson:

1. seafloor ages, magnetic stripes, earthquakes, volcanoes, GPS, and hotspot tracks all support plate motion
2. different tectonic settings create different evidence patterns
3. strong scientific explanations use claim, evidence, and reasoning together

When you study a tectonic setting, do not ask only, "What feature do I notice?" Ask, "What full pattern do I observe, and what plate process best explains it?"
