# Primary Sources (PD): Launch Lesson

## Learning Goal
- I can explain what makes a source primary and why its context matters.
- I can analyze a primary source for perspective, purpose, and key details.
- I can synthesize information from multiple primary sources to strengthen a claim.

## What You'll Learn
Primary sources are original materials created during the time being studied. They can include letters, speeches, photographs, diary entries, legal documents, interviews, recordings, or political cartoons. When readers work carefully with primary sources, they gain direct access to voices, decisions, and perspectives from the moment itself.

Imagine you are investigating a major event in history. A textbook may summarize it, but a speech, a newspaper page, or a personal letter can reveal how people understood that event while it was happening. Those firsthand details help readers ask stronger questions and build more precise interpretations.

## Key Vocabulary
- **Primary Source**: An original document, object, or firsthand account from the time being studied. For example, a letter written by a soldier during World War I is a primary source.
- **Context**: The historical, cultural, and political circumstances surrounding a source. For instance, the audience and time period of a speech can affect its tone and purpose.
- **Perspective**: The point of view shaped by a person's role, beliefs, or experiences.
- **Synthesis**: Combining ideas from multiple sources to form a fuller understanding of a topic.

## Understanding Primary Sources
Primary sources are powerful because they preserve language, priorities, and emotions from the original moment. At the same time, they do not speak for themselves. Readers need to ask who created the source, why it was created, who the intended audience was, and what limitations the source might have.

For example, a government poster encouraging wartime sacrifice and a private diary entry from the same period may describe the same historical moment very differently. One source may be persuasive and public, while the other may be personal and reflective.

## Worked Example: Analyzing a Source
Suppose students read a section of Martin Luther King Jr.'s "Letter from Birmingham Jail."

Questions a strong reader might ask:

- Who wrote this text, and under what circumstances?
- What claim is the author making?
- Which words reveal urgency, frustration, or hope?
- How does the intended audience shape the argument?

A strong interpretation might note that King is writing from jail to respond directly to criticism, so the source combines personal experience with persuasive argument.

## Strategy: Annotate for Claims and Context
When analyzing a primary source, readers can:

1. Mark the central claim or purpose.
2. Underline key details or phrases that reveal tone and perspective.
3. Note sourcing information such as author, date, audience, and situation.
4. Connect the source to other texts or historical knowledge.

For example, if students compare King's letter with a newspaper article from the same week, they can identify both shared facts and major differences in tone and emphasis.

## Synthesizing Across Sources
Synthesis means building understanding from more than one source.

For example, if students examine a speech, a photograph, and a newspaper report about the same protest, they can compare:

- what each source highlights
- what each source leaves out
- how perspective changes the story being told

That comparison helps students write stronger claims because they are not relying on a single piece of evidence.

## Common Mistake
Students sometimes assume that a primary source is automatically complete or unbiased just because it comes from the time period itself.

That is not true.

Primary sources are valuable, but they still reflect specific perspectives and purposes. A persuasive speech, a diary entry, and a government report may each be trustworthy in some ways while still remaining partial or limited in others.

## Summary
Primary sources help readers move beyond summary and into interpretation.

Strong analysis requires students to:

- identify the creator, audience, and purpose
- notice how language reveals perspective
- use specific details as evidence
- synthesize across multiple sources when building a claim

When approaching a new primary source, ask not only "What does it say?" but also "Why was it created, and how does that shape its meaning?"
