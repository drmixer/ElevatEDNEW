# Plan Your Week in 10 Minutes

## Learning Goal
- I can map my fixed commitments before I schedule study time.
- I can place one anchor block and two short review blocks in realistic spots.
- I can check whether a weekly study plan is balanced and doable.

## Introduction
Imagine it is Sunday night and you know you need time for math, reading, and homework review this week. If you only say, "I'll do it later," the week can fill up before you start. Practice, rides, dinner, and tired evenings can quietly take every open spot.

A short planning routine fixes that problem. In this lesson, you will learn how to build a simple weekly study plan in about 10 minutes. You will map your fixed commitments, choose one anchor block for your hardest subject, add two short review blocks, and check whether the plan is realistic.

## Key Vocabulary
**Fixed commitment**: A time you already have to use for something, such as school, practice, or a family event.

**Anchor block**: The main study block you protect first for your hardest or most important subject.

**Review block**: A short study block used to reread notes, practice vocabulary, or quiz yourself.

**Priority**: The task that matters most to place first.

**Realistic**: A plan that fits your actual week and is likely you can really do it.

## The Weekly Planning Routine
Use this four-step routine:

1. Map your fixed commitments.
2. Place one anchor block.
3. Add two short review blocks.
4. Check the plan.

This routine works because it starts with real life instead of wishful thinking. You do not plan in empty space. You plan around the week you actually have.

## Step 1: Map Fixed Commitments
Start by marking the times you cannot use.

Examples of fixed commitments include:

- school
- sports practice
- music lessons
- family dinner out
- rides home

When you mark those first, the open time becomes easier to see. This keeps you from promising yourself a study block that overlaps with something you already have to do.

## Step 2: Place One Anchor Block
Next, choose one anchor block for the subject that needs the most attention.

A good anchor block is:

- tied to a real open time
- long enough to get started, usually about 20 to 30 minutes
- placed on a day when you still have energy

For many students, math problem-solving or a writing task works well as the anchor block because those jobs take more focus than a quick review.

## Step 3: Add Two Short Review Blocks
After the anchor block, add two short review blocks somewhere else in the week.

Review blocks are useful for:

- rereading notes
- practicing vocabulary
- reviewing reading questions
- checking yesterday's mistakes

Short review blocks are easier to keep than one giant session. Fifteen or twenty minutes can be enough when the task is focused.

## Step 4: Check The Plan
Before you are done, ask:

1. Does any block overlap with a fixed commitment?
2. Did I put too much work on one day?
3. Do I have the materials I need when that block starts?
4. If I miss one block, where could I move it?

Checking the plan is what turns a rough idea into a routine you can repeat every week.

## Worked Example: Elena's Week
Elena has soccer on Monday and Wednesday, tutoring on Thursday, and family dinner out on Friday.

Her first plan says:

- Monday: 45 minutes of math after soccer
- Wednesday: 30 minutes of reading after soccer
- Friday: 30 minutes of vocabulary before dinner

This plan has a problem. Monday after soccer is too late for a long math block, and Friday before dinner may feel rushed.

A stronger plan is:

- Tuesday: 25-minute math anchor block right after snack
- Thursday: 15-minute reading review before tutoring
- Saturday morning: 15-minute vocabulary review block

This second plan spreads the work out, protects the hardest subject first, and uses shorter review blocks for lighter tasks.

## Guided Application: Fix Jamal's Plan
Jamal writes this plan:

- Tuesday night: 60 minutes of math
- Tuesday night: 30 minutes of reading
- Tuesday night: 20 minutes of vocabulary

He leaves the rest of the week empty.

Use the routine to improve his plan:

1. Keep one anchor block for the hardest subject.
2. Move the review blocks to different open times.
3. Check whether the new plan is more realistic.

One better answer would be:

- Tuesday: 25-minute math anchor block
- Thursday: 15-minute reading review block
- Saturday: 15-minute vocabulary review block

That version is easier to follow because it does not depend on one overloaded evening.

## Summary
A weekly study plan should be short, realistic, and easy to repeat.

This routine helps:

- map fixed commitments first
- protect one anchor block for the hardest subject
- add two short review blocks
- check the plan before the week begins

When your week feels crowded, do not wait for perfect free time. Use the routine, place the important blocks first, and build a plan you can actually keep.
