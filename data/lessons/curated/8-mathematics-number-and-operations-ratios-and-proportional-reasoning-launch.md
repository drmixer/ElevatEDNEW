# Ratios & Proportional Reasoning

## Learning Goal
- I can determine whether a relationship is proportional from a table, graph, equation, or context.
- I can solve proportions and interpret the constant of proportionality.
- I can use proportional reasoning in scale drawings, similar figures, and real-world rates.

## Introduction
By Grade 8, proportional reasoning becomes a bridge between arithmetic, geometry, and algebra.

A proportional relationship has a constant multiplicative comparison. That idea shows up in many settings:

- unit rates
- scale drawings
- similar figures
- equations of the form `y = kx`

The key is not only to solve for a missing number. It is to understand why the relationship stays balanced.

In this lesson, focus on three questions:

1. How can I tell whether a relationship is proportional?
2. What does the constant of proportionality mean?
3. How can proportional reasoning solve geometry and real-world problems?

## Key Vocabulary
**Ratio**: A comparison of two quantities.

**Proportion**: An equation stating that two ratios are equal.

**Proportional relationship**: A relationship with a constant multiplicative comparison that can be written as `y = kx`.

**Constant of proportionality**: The value `k` in `y = kx`; it tells how much output goes with `1` unit of input.

**Scale factor**: The number used to enlarge or reduce a figure.

**Similar figures**: Figures with the same shape and proportional side lengths.

## What Proportional Means
If a relationship is proportional, the ratio `y / x` stays constant for every nonzero input.

That means:

- the table shows equivalent ratios
- the graph is a straight line through the origin
- the equation has the form `y = kx`

These are not separate facts. They are different ways of describing the same structure.

## Worked Example 1: Testing a Table for Proportionality
Study the table:

| x | y |
| --- | --- |
| 2 | 5 |
| 4 | 10 |
| 6 | 15 |
| 10 | 25 |

Step 1: Find the ratio `y / x`.

- `5 / 2 = 2.5`
- `10 / 4 = 2.5`
- `15 / 6 = 2.5`
- `25 / 10 = 2.5`

The ratio stays constant.

So the relationship is proportional.

Step 2: Write the equation.

`y = 2.5x`

The constant of proportionality is `2.5`.

## Worked Example 2: Solving a Proportion
Solve:

`3 / 5 = x / 20`

Use the idea that equal ratios keep the same multiplicative structure.

Since `5` was multiplied by `4` to get `20`, multiply `3` by `4` as well:

`x = 12`

You can also use cross products:

`3 x 20 = 5x`

`60 = 5x`

`x = 12`

Both methods rely on the same balanced ratio relationship.

## Worked Example 3: Using a Scale Drawing
On a map, `1` centimeter represents `8` kilometers.

Two towns are `5.5` centimeters apart on the map.

What is the actual distance?

Step 1: Write the proportional relationship.

`actual distance = 8 x map distance`

Step 2: Substitute `5.5` for the map distance.

`actual distance = 8 x 5.5 = 44`

So the towns are:

`44 kilometers`

apart

Here, the constant of proportionality is `8 kilometers per centimeter`.

## Worked Example 4: Similar Figures
Two rectangles are similar.

Small rectangle:

- width `4`
- length `10`

Large rectangle:

- width `6`
- length `x`

Because the rectangles are similar, corresponding side lengths are proportional.

Write the proportion:

`4 / 10 = 6 / x`

Use cross products:

`4x = 60`

`x = 15`

So the large rectangle has length `15`.

You can also reason with scale factor:

`6 / 4 = 1.5`

Multiply the length by `1.5`:

`10 x 1.5 = 15`

## Worked Example 5: Comparing Proportional and Non-Proportional Equations
Decide which equation represents a proportional relationship:

1. `y = 7x`
2. `y = 7x + 4`

Equation `1` is proportional because it has the form `y = kx`.

Equation `2` is not proportional because of the added `4`.

Even though both equations are linear, only the first one passes through the origin.

This is an important Grade 8 idea:

- all proportional relationships are linear
- not all linear relationships are proportional

## Using the Constant of Proportionality
The constant of proportionality tells how much output goes with `1` unit of input.

If `y = 0.75x`, then:

- for every `1` unit of `x`, there are `0.75` units of `y`
- the unit rate is `0.75`

That number can represent:

- dollars per item
- miles per minute
- centimeters per inch
- cups of water per cup of rice

Its meaning depends on the context, not just the equation.

## Common Mistake: Confusing Additive and Multiplicative Patterns
Students sometimes decide a relationship is proportional because the outputs increase by a constant amount.

Example:

| x | y |
| --- | --- |
| 1 | 6 |
| 2 | 9 |
| 3 | 12 |

The outputs increase by `3`, but the ratios are:

- `6 / 1 = 6`
- `9 / 2 = 4.5`
- `12 / 3 = 4`

The ratios are not constant, so the relationship is not proportional.

Equal differences show a linear pattern. Equal ratios show a proportional pattern.

## Summary
Proportional reasoning in Grade 8 connects equations, graphs, geometry, and real-world rates.

To solve well:

1. test whether the ratio stays constant
2. write proportional equations in the form `y = kx`
3. solve proportions by preserving equivalent ratios
4. interpret the constant of proportionality in context

The main idea is balance. When two quantities stay in the same multiplicative relationship, proportional reasoning lets you predict unknown values with confidence.
