# Evolution & Natural Selection

## Learning Goal
- I can explain how variation and environment affect which traits become more common.
- I can describe how natural selection changes populations over generations.
- I can use evidence from organisms and environments to support a claim about selection.

## Introduction
Populations contain differences among individuals. Some of those differences can help certain organisms survive and reproduce better in a particular environment. Over many generations, this can change the population.

In this lesson, ask:

1. What variation exists in the population?
2. How does the environment affect survival?
3. How can the population change over time?

## Key Vocabulary
**Population**: A group of the same species living in one area.

**Variation**: Differences in traits among individuals.

**Natural selection**: The process in which individuals with helpful inherited traits are more likely to survive and reproduce.

**Trait**: A characteristic of an organism.

**Adaptation**: An inherited trait that helps survival or reproduction in a certain environment.

**Environment**: The conditions around an organism.

## Phenomenon: Insects Blend Into Dark Bark
Students study an insect population and observe:

- some insects are lighter
- some insects are darker
- birds more easily spot insects that stand out on the bark
- after the bark becomes darker over time, darker insects become more common in later generations

The question is:

How can environmental change affect which traits become more common in a population?

## How Natural Selection Works
Natural selection depends on several connected ideas:

- populations contain variation
- some traits are inherited
- the environment affects survival and reproduction
- individuals with helpful inherited traits may leave more offspring

Over time, the population can change.

## Populations Change Over Generations
It is important to explain this carefully:

- individual organisms do not decide to change a trait because they need it
- instead, traits already present in the population can become more common over generations

Natural selection is a population-level process.

## Worked Example 1: Explaining the Insect Pattern
What is a strong explanation?

"Darker insects were harder for birds to see on darker bark, so they survived and reproduced more often, making the darker trait more common over generations."

## Worked Example 2: Individual or Population Change?
Compare these claims:

- "An insect changed color because it needed camouflage."
- "Darker insects became more common in the population over generations."

Which one matches natural selection?

"The second claim matches natural selection."

## Worked Example 3: Advantage Depends on Environment
Suppose a population includes thick-furred and thin-furred animals, and the climate becomes colder.

What is a reasonable claim?

"If thicker fur helps survival in colder conditions and the trait is inherited, thick-furred animals may become more common over generations."

## Common Mistake: Saying Organisms Evolve Because They Try
Students often say:

"The animals evolved because they wanted to survive."

That hides the actual mechanism.

A stronger method is:

1. identify the variation
2. describe the environmental pressure
3. explain how the population changes over generations

## Summary
Natural selection explains how populations can change when inherited variation interacts with the environment.

Key ideas from this lesson:

1. populations contain variation
2. some traits become more helpful in certain environments
3. over generations, those traits can become more common

When you analyze natural selection, ask what variation exists, what the environment favors, and how the population changes through time.
