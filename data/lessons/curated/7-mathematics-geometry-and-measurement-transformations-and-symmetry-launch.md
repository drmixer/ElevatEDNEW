# Transformations & Symmetry

## Learning Goal
- I can describe a sequence of transformations that moves one figure onto another.
- I can explain why translations, reflections, and rotations preserve congruence.
- I can use symmetry to reason about matching parts of a figure.

## Introduction
In Grade 7, transformations become more useful because they help explain congruence.

If one figure can be moved onto another by a sequence of translations, reflections, and rotations, then the figures are congruent. The figure may change position or orientation, but it does not change size.

This makes transformations more than motion vocabulary. They become a method for justifying geometric relationships.

As you read, focus on three questions:

1. Which transformation, or sequence of transformations, maps one figure onto another?
2. Why do these transformations preserve side lengths and angles?
3. How can symmetry help reveal matching parts?

## Key Vocabulary
**Translation**: A slide that moves every point the same distance and direction.

**Reflection**: A flip across a line.

**Rotation**: A turn around a point.

**Sequence of transformations**: More than one transformation performed in order.

**Congruent**: Having the same shape and the same size.

## Why Transformations Matter for Congruence
Translations, reflections, and rotations are rigid transformations.

That means they preserve:

- side lengths
- angle measures
- overall shape

So if Figure A can be moved onto Figure B using only these transformations, the figures are congruent.

This is stronger than saying "they look alike." It gives a geometric reason.

## Worked Example 1: A Translation Followed by a Reflection
Triangle `A` is drawn on the left side of a vertical line. Triangle `B` is drawn on the right side of that line.

The triangles have the same size, but Triangle `B` is both shifted and mirrored.

What sequence could map Triangle `A` to Triangle `B`?

One possible sequence is:

1. translate Triangle `A` toward the line
2. reflect it across the vertical line

Why does this work?

- the translation moves the figure into position
- the reflection creates the mirror image

Because both transformations are rigid, the image remains congruent to the original.

## Worked Example 2: Rotation and Congruence
A rectangle is turned `90` degrees clockwise around its center.

Is the image congruent to the original rectangle?

Yes.

Why?

- a rotation changes orientation
- it does not change side lengths
- it does not change angle measures

So the rotated image is congruent to the original rectangle.

## Worked Example 3: Describing a Sequence Precisely
Point `A` moves to `A'` by sliding `3` units right. Then the image is reflected across the `x`-axis.

How should the sequence be described?

Say the transformations in order:

1. translate `3` units to the right
2. reflect across the `x`-axis

Order matters.

If you reflect first and then translate, the final image may be different.

In geometry, a sequence of transformations is not just a list of moves. It is an ordered process.

## How Symmetry Connects to Reflection
If a figure has line symmetry, then reflecting the figure across its line of symmetry maps the figure onto itself.

Example:

A regular hexagon has several lines of symmetry.

If you reflect it across one of those lines, the matching halves overlap.

This shows how symmetry is really a special reflection relationship built into the figure.

## Worked Example 4: Symmetry as Evidence
Suppose a shape has a vertical line through its center, and the left half is a mirror image of the right half.

What does that tell you?

It tells you the shape has vertical line symmetry.

It also tells you that corresponding points on opposite sides of the line are:

- equally far from the line
- matched by a reflection

This is useful because it helps identify equal or matching parts without measuring each one separately.

## Common Mistake: Assuming Any Moved Figure Is Congruent
Students sometimes say:

"The figure moved, so it must be congruent."

That is not always true.

A figure is congruent only if the movement is a rigid transformation.

If the figure is enlarged or reduced, then the size changed and congruence is lost.

Use this check:

- translation, reflection, rotation -> congruent image
- resizing -> not congruent

## Summary
Grade 7 transformations help explain why figures are congruent and how one figure maps onto another.

To solve these problems well:

1. identify the transformation or sequence of transformations
2. keep the order of the moves clear
3. use rigid-motion reasoning to justify congruence
4. connect symmetry to reflection when a figure maps onto itself

Transformations matter because they turn visual matching into a clear geometric argument.
