# Chemistry: Atoms, Bonding, & Reactions

## Learning Goal
- I can identify evidence that a chemical reaction has occurred.
- I can explain that chemical reactions rearrange atoms into new substances.
- I can distinguish between a physical change and a chemical change using evidence and reasoning.

## Introduction
Many changes in matter look dramatic, but not all of them are chemical reactions. Ice melting, sugar dissolving, iron rusting, and vinegar fizzing do not all represent the same kind of change.

Chemistry becomes clearer when you stop asking only, "What do I see?" and start asking, "What evidence suggests a new substance formed?"

In this lesson, focus on three questions:

1. Which observations count as evidence of a chemical reaction?
2. How do atoms behave during a reaction?
3. How can I separate chemical change from physical change?

## Key Vocabulary
**Atom**: The smallest unit of an element that still has that element's properties.

**Molecule**: A group of atoms bonded together.

**Chemical reaction**: A process in which atoms are rearranged to form new substances.

**Reactant**: A starting substance in a chemical reaction.

**Product**: A new substance formed by a chemical reaction.

**Physical change**: A change in form or state that does not create a new substance.

## Phenomenon: A Lab Cup Starts Fizzing
In a classroom investigation, a student combines a white powder with a clear liquid. Scientists observe:

- bubbles form quickly
- the cup becomes cooler to the touch
- the mixture looks different from either starting material
- after the bubbling stops, the original powder cannot be separated back out

The main question is:

Did the student observe only a physical change, or is there evidence of a chemical reaction?

## What Counts as Evidence of a Reaction?
One sign alone is not always enough, but several observations together can make a strong case.

Common evidence of a chemical reaction includes:

- gas production, such as bubbling
- temperature change without outside heating or cooling
- formation of a new solid
- color change linked to a new substance
- a new odor in some reactions

These clues matter because they suggest the starting substances are turning into something different, not just changing shape or state.

## Atoms Are Rearranged, Not Destroyed
In a chemical reaction, atoms do not disappear. They are rearranged into new combinations.

That means:

- the reactants contain the starting atoms
- the products contain those same atoms in new groupings
- the total number of each kind of atom stays conserved

This is why chemistry explanations should connect observations to particle behavior. Bubbles and temperature changes matter because they suggest atoms have been rearranged into new substances with different properties.

## Worked Example 1: Is It a Chemical Reaction?
A student mixes vinegar and baking soda and observes rapid bubbling and a cooler container.

What is the best explanation?

A strong claim is:

"The evidence supports that a chemical reaction occurred."

Evidence:

- gas formed, shown by bubbling
- the temperature changed during the process

Reasoning:

These observations suggest the starting substances did more than mix together. They produced at least one new substance, including a gas, which is evidence of a chemical reaction.

## Worked Example 2: Physical Change or Chemical Change?
Compare these two situations:

Situation A:

- ice melts into liquid water

Situation B:

- iron reacts with oxygen over time and forms rust

Which one is a chemical change?

A strong answer is:

"Rusting is a chemical change, but melting ice is a physical change."

Why?

- melting changes only the state of water; it remains the same substance
- rusting forms a new substance with different properties from the original iron

The key idea is not whether the change looks dramatic. The key question is whether a new substance formed.

## Worked Example 3: Connecting a Particle Model to Evidence
Suppose a particle diagram shows two different reactant molecules before a reaction. After the reaction, the same atoms appear, but they are grouped into new product molecules.

What does that model explain?

A strong explanation is:

"The model shows that atoms are conserved during the reaction but rearranged into new substances."

This matters because it connects the invisible particle model to the visible evidence:

- the products behave differently because the atoms are grouped differently
- the atoms were not created or destroyed
- the reaction changed the substances by changing their combinations

## Common Mistake: Assuming Any Visible Change Is Chemical
Students often think:

"If something changes color or shape, it must be a chemical reaction."

That is too quick.

A stronger method is:

1. describe the observations carefully
2. ask whether the evidence suggests a new substance formed
3. connect the evidence to atom rearrangement when possible

For example, breaking a solid into smaller pieces changes its shape, but it does not create a new substance.

## Summary
Chemical reactions are best understood through evidence and particle reasoning.

Key ideas from this lesson:

1. signs such as gas production or temperature change can support a reaction claim
2. chemical reactions rearrange atoms into new substances
3. physical changes do not create new substances, even when the material looks different

When you analyze a change in matter, do not stop at what you can see. Ask what the evidence suggests about the substances and the atoms inside them.
