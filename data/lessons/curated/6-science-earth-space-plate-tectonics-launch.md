# Plate Tectonics (Earth & Space Science, Grade 6)

## Learning Goal
- I can describe the pattern of earthquakes, volcanoes, and mountain ranges at plate boundaries.
- I can use evidence from fossils, rock layers, and the ocean floor to explain that Earth's plates move.
- I can predict what usually happens at convergent, divergent, and transform boundaries.

## Introduction
Earth's surface does not stay still. Scientists noticed that many earthquakes and volcanoes happen in long belts instead of being spread evenly across the planet. They also found fossils, rock layers, and ocean-floor patterns that seem to connect places now separated by oceans.

In this lesson, you will figure out the same question scientists asked:

1. What evidence shows that Earth's plates move?
2. Why do earthquakes and volcanoes happen most often near plate boundaries?
3. What different patterns do convergent, divergent, and transform boundaries create?

## Key Vocabulary
**Tectonic plate**: A large piece of Earth's outer layer that moves slowly over time.

**Plate boundary**: The place where two tectonic plates meet.

**Subduction**: The process in which one plate sinks beneath another plate.

**Seafloor spreading**: The process in which new ocean crust forms at a ridge and moves outward.

**Convection**: The slow movement of warmer and cooler material in Earth's mantle that helps drive plate motion.

## Phenomenon: Why Are Earthquakes and Volcanoes Clustered?
If you marked active volcanoes and major earthquakes on a world map, you would notice a pattern:

- many line up around the Pacific Ocean
- many happen along ocean ridges and deep trenches
- large quiet areas exist far from those narrow belts

That pattern matters because it suggests earthquakes and volcanoes are connected to the edges of moving plates, not random spots on Earth's surface.

When plates push together, pull apart, or grind past each other, stress builds and Earth's crust changes shape. That is why plate boundaries are the places where the strongest surface changes often happen.

## Evidence Set 1: Clues From Continents
Long before scientists could measure plate motion with satellites, they noticed several clues that continents may once have been connected:

- coastlines on opposite sides of the Atlantic seem to fit together
- identical fossils of ancient plants and animals appear on continents now separated by oceans
- matching mountain belts and rock layers line up across those same continents

Each clue matters on its own, but together they make a stronger case. If South America and Africa were once connected, then matching fossils and rock patterns make sense. If the continents had always been far apart, scientists would need a much weaker explanation, such as identical land organisms somehow crossing a wide ocean.

This is an important science move: one observation can be interesting, but several related observations can support a strong explanation.

## Evidence Set 2: Clues From the Ocean Floor
Scientists later gathered evidence from the seafloor:

- mid-ocean ridges form long underwater mountain chains
- rocks closest to a ridge are younger than rocks farther away
- the pattern of rock ages appears on both sides of the ridge

That pattern supports seafloor spreading. New crust forms near the ridge, then slowly moves away as more new crust rises behind it.

A useful worked example is the Mid-Atlantic Ridge. If new crust forms there, then the youngest ocean-floor rock should be near the ridge and older rock should appear farther east and west. That is exactly the pattern scientists found. The evidence matches the explanation.

## Boundary Types and Surface Changes
Plate tectonics explains three major boundary patterns:

- **Convergent boundary**: Plates move toward each other. One plate may subduct, causing trenches, earthquakes, volcanoes, or mountain building.
- **Divergent boundary**: Plates move apart. Magma rises, new crust forms, and ridges or rift valleys develop.
- **Transform boundary**: Plates slide past each other. This motion often causes earthquakes because stress builds and then releases suddenly.

Notice that each boundary type has a different pattern of evidence. Scientists do not just memorize the names. They connect the observed landforms and hazards to the type of plate movement that would cause them.

## Worked Example: Explaining the Andes
Consider the Andes Mountains along the west coast of South America. Scientists observe:

- a deep ocean trench just offshore
- frequent earthquakes
- a chain of volcanoes on land

A strong explanation is that an oceanic plate is subducting beneath the South American Plate at a convergent boundary.

Here is the reasoning step by step:

- **Claim**: The Andes are shaped by a convergent plate boundary.
- **Evidence**: There is a trench offshore, many earthquakes, and a line of volcanoes.
- **Reasoning**: When an oceanic plate sinks beneath a continental plate, the plates create stress that causes earthquakes, and melting above the sinking plate helps form volcanoes.

This is the same move scientists use in many Earth science questions: identify the pattern, match it to the boundary type, then explain why that boundary would create those features.

## Summary
Plate tectonics explains why many earthquakes, volcanoes, trenches, ridges, and mountain belts are found in connected patterns around Earth.

The strongest evidence in this lesson came from:

- matching fossils and rock layers on distant continents
- young ocean-floor rock near ridges and older rock farther away
- boundary patterns that connect plate motion to earthquakes, volcanoes, and landforms

When you study a new Earth science example, ask:

1. What pattern do I observe?
2. What evidence supports the explanation?
3. Which type of plate boundary best fits the pattern?
