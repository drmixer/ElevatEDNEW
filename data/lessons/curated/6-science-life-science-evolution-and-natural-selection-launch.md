# Evolution & Natural Selection

## Learning Goal
- I can explain how variation in a population can affect survival and reproduction.
- I can describe how natural selection changes populations over time.
- I can use evidence from organisms and environments to support a claim about selection.

## Introduction
Living things in a population are not all exactly the same. Some differences may help certain individuals survive better in a particular environment. Over many generations, those differences can make some traits more common.

In this lesson, ask:

1. What variation exists in the population?
2. How does the environment affect which traits are helpful?
3. How can a population change over time?

## Key Vocabulary
**Population**: A group of the same species living in one area.

**Variation**: Differences in traits among individuals.

**Trait**: A characteristic of an organism.

**Natural selection**: The process in which individuals with helpful inherited traits are more likely to survive and reproduce.

**Adaptation**: An inherited trait that helps an organism survive or reproduce in a certain environment.

**Environment**: The conditions in which organisms live.

## Phenomenon: Darker Beetles Become More Common
Students study a beetle population living on tree bark. They observe:

- some beetles are lighter
- some beetles are darker
- birds eat beetles they can see more easily
- after the bark darkens in the environment, darker beetles become more common over generations

The question is:

How can environmental change affect which traits become more common in a population?

## How Natural Selection Works
Natural selection depends on several connected ideas:

- individuals in a population show variation
- some of the variation is inherited
- the environment makes some traits more helpful than others
- individuals with those traits may survive and reproduce more successfully

Over time, the population can change.

## Populations Change, Not Individual Needs
This is an important idea:

- individual beetles do not decide to change color because they need to
- instead, beetles that already have a helpful trait may become more common over generations

Natural selection is about population change across time.

## Worked Example 1: Explaining the Beetle Pattern
What is a strong explanation?

"Darker beetles may have been harder for birds to see on darker bark, so they survived and reproduced more often, making the darker trait more common over generations."

This answer is strong because it connects variation, environment, and population change.

## Worked Example 2: Individual or Population?
Compare these two claims:

- "A beetle changed color because it needed to hide."
- "Beetles with darker coloring became more common over generations."

Which claim matches natural selection?

"The second claim matches natural selection."

## Worked Example 3: Trait Advantage Depends on Environment
Suppose a population of small animals includes thick-furred and thin-furred individuals, and the environment becomes much colder.

What is a reasonable claim?

"If thicker fur helps survival in colder conditions and the trait is inherited, thick-furred individuals may become more common over generations."

## Common Mistake: Saying Organisms Evolve Because They Try
Students often say:

"The animals evolved because they wanted to survive."

That hides the mechanism.

A stronger method is:

1. identify the variation in the population
2. describe the environmental pressure
3. explain how the population changes over generations

## Summary
Natural selection explains how populations can change when inherited variation interacts with the environment.

Key ideas from this lesson:

1. populations contain variation
2. some traits may help survival and reproduction more than others
3. over generations, those traits can become more common

When you analyze natural selection, ask what variation exists, what the environment favors, and how the population changes over time.
