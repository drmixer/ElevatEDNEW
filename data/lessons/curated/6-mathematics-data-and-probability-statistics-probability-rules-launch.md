# Probability Rules

## Learning Goal
- I can describe the probability of a simple event using words, fractions, and decimals.
- I can compare theoretical probability to experimental results.
- I can explain why experimental results may be close to, but not exactly equal to, the expected probability.

## Introduction
Probability helps us describe how likely an event is to happen.

In Grade 6, the most important idea is that probability is not a guarantee. A fair coin is likely to land heads about half the time, but that does not mean heads and tails must appear in a perfectly even pattern every short experiment.

In this lesson, focus on three questions:

1. How can I represent the probability of a simple event?
2. How do I find probability from equally likely outcomes?
3. Why can experimental results differ from the expected probability?

## Key Vocabulary
**Probability**: A number from `0` to `1` that describes how likely an event is.

**Outcome**: One possible result of an experiment.

**Event**: A specific outcome or group of outcomes you are interested in.

**Theoretical probability**: Probability found by reasoning about all equally likely outcomes.

**Experimental probability**: Probability found from actual results in an experiment.

## Probability on a Scale
Probability values fall between `0` and `1`.

- `0` means impossible
- `1` means certain
- values near `0` are unlikely
- values near `1` are likely
- `0.5` means equally likely to happen or not happen

You can also write probability as a fraction, decimal, or percent.

Example:

If a fair coin is flipped once, the probability of heads is:

`1 / 2 = 0.5 = 50%`

This does not predict one exact flip. It describes the long-run likelihood.

## Worked Example 1: Simple Probability from Equally Likely Outcomes
A fair number cube has `6` faces labeled `1` through `6`. What is the probability of rolling a number greater than `4`?

Step 1: List the outcomes greater than `4`.

- `5`
- `6`

There are `2` favorable outcomes.

Step 2: Count the total number of outcomes.

There are `6` possible outcomes.

Step 3: Write the probability.

`P(number greater than 4) = 2 / 6 = 1 / 3`

Interpretation:

The event is less than half likely because only `2` of the `6` outcomes work.

## Worked Example 2: Theoretical vs Experimental Probability
A student spins a spinner with `4` equal sections: red, blue, green, and yellow.

The theoretical probability of landing on blue is:

`1 / 4`

Now suppose the student spins `20` times and lands on blue `7` times.

The experimental probability is:

`7 / 20`

Compare the two probabilities:

- theoretical: `1 / 4 = 5 / 20`
- experimental: `7 / 20`

The experimental result is not exactly the same, but it is reasonably close.

This happens because short experiments often include random variation.

## Why Results Can Differ
Students sometimes expect experimental probability to match theoretical probability exactly every time.

That is not how probability works.

If you flip a fair coin `10` times, you might get:

- `6` heads and `4` tails
- `7` heads and `3` tails
- `5` heads and `5` tails

All of these are possible.

As the number of trials increases, experimental probability usually moves closer to theoretical probability. But in a small number of trials, variation is normal.

## Worked Example 3: Probability from an Experiment Table
A bag contains tiles. A student does not know the exact contents, so they draw one tile, record the color, replace it, and repeat this `30` times.

Results:

- red: `12`
- blue: `9`
- yellow: `6`
- green: `3`

What is the experimental probability of drawing yellow?

Step 1: Identify the favorable count.

Yellow occurred `6` times.

Step 2: Identify the total number of trials.

There were `30` draws.

Step 3: Write the probability.

`P(yellow) = 6 / 30 = 1 / 5`

As a decimal:

`1 / 5 = 0.2`

This means yellow occurred in about `20%` of the trials.

## Common Mistake: Mixing Up Outcomes and Favorable Outcomes
Students sometimes write:

`P(rolling an even number on a number cube) = 6 / 3`

That is backwards.

The correct setup is:

- favorable outcomes: `2, 4, 6` so `3`
- total outcomes: `6`

So:

`P(even number) = 3 / 6 = 1 / 2`

Always use:

`probability = favorable outcomes / total outcomes`

## Compare These Events
On a fair number cube, compare these probabilities:

- rolling a `2`: `1 / 6`
- rolling an even number: `3 / 6 = 1 / 2`
- rolling a number less than `7`: `6 / 6 = 1`

This comparison helps you interpret size:

- `1 / 6` is unlikely
- `1 / 2` is equally likely
- `1` is certain

The number tells more than just the answer. It describes the event's likelihood.

## Summary
Probability describes how likely an event is using a number from `0` to `1`.

To find or interpret probability:

1. identify the event
2. count the favorable outcomes or successful trials
3. divide by the total number of outcomes or trials
4. decide whether the result is unlikely, equally likely, likely, or certain

Theoretical probability comes from the structure of the situation. Experimental probability comes from actual results. They may differ in short experiments, but both help describe chance.
