# Integers & Rational Numbers

## Learning Goal
- I can identify and compare positive and negative integers on a number line.
- I can use opposites and absolute value to describe rational numbers.
- I can order integers, fractions, and decimals from least to greatest.

## Introduction
Grade 6 number sense expands the number line in both directions.

Before this, many problems used only positive numbers. Now you work with numbers below zero and with rational numbers such as fractions and decimals that can also be positive or negative.

These numbers help describe real situations:

- temperatures above and below `0`
- money earned and money owed
- elevations above and below sea level
- gains and losses in a game

In this lesson, focus on three questions:

1. What does the sign of a number tell me?
2. How do opposites and absolute value help describe location on a number line?
3. How can I compare and order rational numbers accurately?

## Key Vocabulary
**Integer**: A whole number, its opposite, or `0`.

**Rational number**: A number that can be written as a fraction of integers; rational numbers include integers, fractions, and terminating or repeating decimals.

**Opposites**: Numbers the same distance from `0` on a number line but on opposite sides.

**Absolute value**: The distance a number is from `0` on the number line.

**Negative number**: A number less than `0`.

**Positive number**: A number greater than `0`.

## Integers on the Number Line
The number line helps show order and distance.

Important ideas:

- numbers to the right are greater
- numbers to the left are smaller
- negative numbers are less than `0`
- positive numbers are greater than `0`

Example:

`-5 < -2 < 0 < 3 < 7`

Even though `5` is greater than `2`, the integer `-5` is less than `-2` because it is farther left on the number line.

That is a common place where students need to slow down and think about position, not just digits.

## Worked Example 1: Opposites and Absolute Value
Consider the integers `6` and `-6`.

Step 1: Identify the relationship.

They are opposites because they are the same distance from `0` but on opposite sides.

Step 2: Find their absolute values.

`|6| = 6`

`|-6| = 6`

Step 3: Interpret the meaning.

Absolute value describes distance, not direction.

So both numbers are `6` units from `0`, even though one is positive and the other is negative.

## Worked Example 2: Comparing Negative Numbers
Which number is greater: `-3` or `-8`?

Use the number line idea.

- `-3` is closer to `0`
- `-8` is farther left

So:

`-3 > -8`

This may feel backward at first, but greater means farther right, not "has a larger digit."

## Worked Example 3: Ordering Rational Numbers
Order these numbers from least to greatest:

`-1.5`, `2/3`, `-2`, `0.25`

Step 1: Rewrite in comparable forms if helpful.

`2/3` is about `0.67`

Step 2: Separate negative and positive numbers.

Negative numbers:

- `-2`
- `-1.5`

Positive numbers:

- `0.25`
- `2/3`

Step 3: Order within each group.

Among the negatives:

`-2 < -1.5`

Among the positives:

`0.25 < 2/3`

So the full order is:

`-2 < -1.5 < 0.25 < 2/3`

## Worked Example 4: Interpreting Rational Numbers in Context
A submarine is `12.5` meters below sea level. A helicopter is `45` meters above sea level.

Represent each situation with an integer or rational number.

Submarine:

`-12.5`

Helicopter:

`45`

Which is greater?

`45 > -12.5`

What is the distance between the two locations relative to sea level?

Add the distances from `0`:

`45 + 12.5 = 57.5`

So the helicopter is `57.5` meters above the submarine's level.

This example shows why sign matters. The numbers are not just amounts. They describe direction relative to a reference point.

## Fractions, Decimals, and Integers Are Connected
Rational numbers can appear in different forms:

- integer: `-4`
- fraction: `3/5`
- decimal: `-0.75`

They all belong to the same number system because they can be placed on the number line and compared by value.

To compare them well:

1. use the sign first
2. rewrite numbers in similar forms when needed
3. place them mentally or physically on a number line

That process is more reliable than guessing from the appearance of the numbers.

## Common Mistake: Thinking Absolute Value Can Be Negative
Students sometimes write:

`|-7| = -7`

That is incorrect.

Absolute value means distance from `0`, and distance cannot be negative.

The correct statement is:

`|-7| = 7`

Another common mistake is thinking `-12` is greater than `-4` because `12` is greater than `4`. On the number line, `-12` is farther left, so it is actually less.

## Summary
Integers and rational numbers extend the number line beyond positive whole numbers.

To reason well about them:

1. use the sign to show direction or position relative to `0`
2. use opposites to compare numbers on opposite sides of `0`
3. use absolute value for distance from `0`
4. compare and order fractions, decimals, and integers by their actual location on the number line

When you connect signs, distance, and order, rational numbers become much easier to interpret in math and in real situations.
