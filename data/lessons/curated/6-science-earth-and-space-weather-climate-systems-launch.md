# Weather & Climate Systems

## Learning Goal
- I can explain how water, air, and energy help create weather patterns.
- I can distinguish between weather and climate.
- I can use evidence from observations to support a claim about a weather or climate pattern.

## Introduction
Weather changes from day to day, but climate describes patterns over much longer times. Both depend on how the Sun's energy moves through air, water, land, and the atmosphere.

In this lesson, ask:

1. What causes a weather event?
2. How is climate different from one day's weather?
3. What evidence fits a short-term claim, and what evidence fits a long-term one?

## Key Vocabulary
**Weather**: The short-term condition of the atmosphere in a place.

**Climate**: The long-term pattern of weather in a region.

**Atmosphere**: The layer of gases around Earth.

**Humidity**: The amount of water vapor in the air.

**Precipitation**: Water that falls from clouds, such as rain or snow.

**Air mass**: A large body of air with similar temperature and moisture.

## Phenomenon: Storms Build After Hot Afternoons
Students observe a pattern across several summer days:

- afternoons become hot and humid
- clouds build later in the day
- short storms form by evening
- mornings after the storms are cooler

The question is:

What system interactions can explain this repeating weather pattern?

## How Weather Forms
Weather is driven by moving energy and water.

Important parts of the system include:

- sunlight warming Earth's surface
- water evaporating into the air
- warm air rising and cool air sinking
- condensation forming clouds
- moving air masses and changing pressure

This matters because weather is not caused by one single factor. It comes from interacting parts of a system.

## Weather Versus Climate
Students often confuse weather and climate.

Weather is:

- today's storm
- this week's temperature
- tomorrow's wind

Climate is:

- the usual winter pattern in a region
- a long record of rainy or dry seasons
- the average conditions over many years

One unusual day is weather. A repeated long-term pattern is climate.

## Worked Example 1: Explaining Rain Formation
Suppose warm, moist air rises and cools.

What is a strong explanation?

"Cooling can cause water vapor to condense into clouds, which may lead to precipitation."

This is stronger than saying only, "It rained because there were clouds." The explanation connects the parts of the system.

## Worked Example 2: Weather or Climate?
Compare these two statements:

- "It snowed yesterday."
- "This region usually has snowy winters."

Which statement is about climate?

"The second statement is about climate because it describes a long-term pattern."

## Worked Example 3: Matching Evidence to the Claim
A student says:

"This town had one very hot week, so its climate has changed."

Why is that weak?

A stronger explanation is:

"One hot week is weather evidence, not enough by itself to prove a climate trend."

Climate claims need data over much longer time periods.

## Common Mistake: Using One Event to Explain the Whole System
Students sometimes rely on one observation only.

A stronger method is:

1. identify several interacting variables
2. decide whether the question is about weather or climate
3. use evidence that matches that time scale

## Summary
Weather and climate both depend on Earth systems, but they describe different time scales.

Key ideas from this lesson:

1. weather forms through interactions among air, water, and energy
2. climate describes long-term patterns, not single events
3. strong claims use evidence that matches the question

When you study a pattern in the atmosphere, ask whether it is weather or climate and what evidence best supports your claim.
