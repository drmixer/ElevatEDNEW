# Scatterplots & Correlation

## Learning Goal
- I can describe the direction and strength of an association in a scatterplot.
- I can use an informal line of fit to model a trend and make reasonable predictions.
- I can identify when an outlier changes how I interpret the data.

## Introduction
By Grade 8, scatterplots become tools for modeling. You are not just describing whether points rise or fall. You are also looking for a line that captures the overall trend and deciding how well the data fits that model.

This matters because real data is rarely perfect. The points usually cluster around a pattern instead of lying exactly on one line.

In this lesson, focus on three questions:

1. What direction and strength does the scatterplot show?
2. What line of fit would represent the data reasonably well?
3. Are there any outliers that affect the interpretation?

## Key Vocabulary
**Scatterplot**: A graph of paired numerical data.

**Correlation**: The direction and strength of association between two variables.

**Line of fit**: A line drawn to model the overall pattern of data in a scatterplot.

**Outlier**: A data point that lies far from the overall pattern.

**Prediction**: An estimate based on the pattern represented by the data or line of fit.

## Describing Correlation
Suppose a scatterplot compares study time and test score.

If the points rise from left to right and stay fairly close to a line, the graph shows positive correlation.

If the points fall from left to right, the graph shows negative correlation.

If the points are spread without a pattern, the graph shows little or no correlation.

Strength also matters:

- points clustered closely around a line suggest stronger correlation
- points spread widely around a line suggest weaker correlation

## Worked Example 1: Interpreting a Scatterplot
Suppose the data pairs are:

- `(1, 58)`
- `(2, 64)`
- `(3, 70)`
- `(4, 76)`
- `(5, 81)`

The points rise as `x` increases, so the direction is positive.

They also stay close to a straight path, so the correlation appears fairly strong.

A good interpretation is:

"The scatterplot shows a strong positive correlation. As study time increases, test score tends to increase."

## Worked Example 2: Drawing an Informal Line of Fit
Suppose these points describe hours practiced and performance score:

- `(1, 60)`
- `(2, 65)`
- `(3, 71)`
- `(4, 74)`
- `(5, 80)`

An informal line of fit should:

- follow the middle of the data cluster
- leave roughly the same amount of scatter above and below the line

A reasonable line might pass near `(1, 60)` and `(5, 80)`.

Find the slope:

`(80 - 60) / (5 - 1) = 20 / 4 = 5`

So the line rises about `5` score points per practice hour.

A simple equation model could be:

`y = 5x + 55`

Check:

If `x = 1`, then `y = 60`

If `x = 5`, then `y = 80`

This model is not exact for every point, but it represents the trend well.

## Worked Example 3: Using the Line of Fit for Prediction
Use the model:

`y = 5x + 55`

Predict the score for `6` practice hours.

Substitute `x = 6`:

`y = 5(6) + 55`

`y = 30 + 55`

`y = 85`

So the predicted score is about `85`.

This prediction is reasonable because `6` hours is close to the original data range.

## Worked Example 4: Interpreting an Outlier
Suppose most points follow an upward trend:

- `(1, 62)`
- `(2, 68)`
- `(3, 74)`
- `(4, 79)`

but one point is:

- `(5, 50)`

That point is far below the pattern.

It is an outlier.

An outlier matters because it can:

- weaken how strong the correlation looks
- affect where you place the line of fit
- suggest that one data record is unusual

A strong interpretation might be:

"The scatterplot still shows positive correlation overall, but the point `(5, 50)` is an outlier and does not match the main pattern."

## When Predictions Are Reasonable
Predictions from a line of fit are strongest when:

- the correlation is fairly clear
- the prediction stays near the observed data range

Predictions are weaker when:

- the scatter is wide
- the data has strong outliers
- the prediction is far beyond the measured `x` values

For example, if the data only covers `1` to `6` hours of practice, a prediction for `5.5` hours is more reasonable than a prediction for `20` hours.

## Common Mistake: Expecting Every Point to Lie on the Line
Students sometimes think a line of fit is wrong unless every point lies exactly on it.

That is not the purpose of the line.

A line of fit is a model of the overall pattern. It summarizes the trend even when the points vary.

The goal is not perfect matching. The goal is a reasonable representation of the data.

## Summary
Grade 8 scatterplot work combines interpretation and modeling.

To analyze a scatterplot well:

1. describe the direction and strength of the correlation
2. draw or imagine a reasonable line of fit
3. use the line to make careful predictions
4. check whether outliers change the way the data should be interpreted

Scatterplots become more powerful when you use them not only to see a pattern, but also to build a model of that pattern.
