# Plate Tectonics

## Learning Goal
- I can explain how evidence from earthquakes, volcanoes, fossils, and seafloor patterns supports plate tectonics.
- I can compare convergent, divergent, and transform boundaries using evidence from real Earth features.
- I can make a claim about a tectonic setting and support it with scientific reasoning.

## Introduction
Earth's surface looks stable on a human timescale, but over long periods it changes dramatically. Mountains rise. Ocean floors spread. Volcanoes and earthquakes cluster in narrow zones instead of appearing randomly.

Plate tectonics explains these patterns by showing that Earth's outer shell is broken into moving plates. The theory matters because it connects many different observations into one system.

In this lesson, ask:

1. What evidence suggests that plates move?
2. How do different plate boundaries create different patterns?
3. How can I use evidence to identify what kind of boundary I am looking at?

## Key Vocabulary
**Tectonic plate**: A large moving section of Earth's lithosphere.

**Plate boundary**: The place where two tectonic plates meet.

**Convergent boundary**: A boundary where plates move toward each other.

**Divergent boundary**: A boundary where plates move apart.

**Transform boundary**: A boundary where plates slide past each other.

**Subduction**: The process in which one plate sinks beneath another.

## Evidence That Plates Move
Scientists did not accept plate tectonics because of one clue. They built the explanation from many kinds of evidence.

Important clues include:

- earthquakes and volcanoes are concentrated in long belts
- continents show matching fossils and rock layers across oceans
- mid-ocean ridges form where new crust appears
- ocean-floor rocks are younger near ridges and older farther away

Each clue matters, but the full theory becomes convincing because the clues support one another.

If continents had always stayed where they are now, matching land-organism fossils on distant continents would be difficult to explain. If the seafloor were not spreading, scientists would not expect the youngest ocean-floor rocks to appear near ridges.

## Boundary Patterns
Plate tectonics becomes easier to understand when you compare boundary types.

### Convergent boundaries
Plates move toward each other. This can create:

- trenches
- volcano chains
- large earthquakes
- mountain building

If one plate is oceanic and one is continental, the denser oceanic plate may subduct.

### Divergent boundaries
Plates move apart. This often creates:

- mid-ocean ridges
- rift valleys
- new crust
- lines of volcanic activity

### Transform boundaries
Plates slide past each other. These often create:

- frequent earthquakes
- fault lines
- little or no volcanic activity compared with some convergent and divergent zones

## Worked Example 1: Reading a Pattern
Suppose scientists observe:

- a deep ocean trench
- a line of volcanoes inland
- many earthquakes

What boundary is most likely?

A strong answer is:

"This pattern suggests a convergent boundary with subduction."

Why?

- trenches form where one plate bends downward
- earthquakes happen as the plates grind and shift
- volcanoes can form above the subducting plate where melting produces magma

The important science move is matching the evidence to the mechanism, not just naming a boundary from memory.

## Worked Example 2: Seafloor Spreading
Scientists measure ocean-floor rocks on both sides of a ridge and find this pattern:

- youngest rocks are nearest the ridge
- older rocks appear farther away on both sides

What does that suggest?

It supports seafloor spreading at a divergent boundary.

Reasoning:

- if new crust is forming at the ridge, it should be youngest there
- as more crust forms, the older crust should be pushed outward
- the mirrored age pattern on both sides fits that explanation

This is stronger than simply saying, "The ridge is important." The explanation must connect the evidence to the process.

## Case Study: Identifying a Boundary From Evidence
Consider this location:

- strong earthquakes occur along a narrow fault line
- there is no major trench
- there is no chain of volcanoes
- plates move sideways relative to one another

This evidence best matches a transform boundary.

The sideways movement is the key clue. Transform boundaries release stress through sliding motion instead of creating new crust or major subduction features.

## Common Mistake: Matching One Feature Instead of the Whole Pattern
A student might say:

"There are earthquakes, so it must be convergent."

That is too quick. Earthquakes happen at all three major boundary types.

A stronger method is:

1. list the full evidence pattern
2. identify which boundary best fits all of the clues
3. explain the mechanism that links the pattern together

Science arguments become stronger when they use more than one piece of evidence.

## Claim, Evidence, Reasoning Model
Here is a model scientific explanation:

"The boundary is most likely convergent. The evidence includes a trench offshore, frequent earthquakes, and a volcanic arc on land. This pattern supports subduction because an oceanic plate sinking beneath another plate can create all three features together."

That response is strong because it includes:

- a clear claim
- multiple pieces of evidence
- reasoning that explains how the process causes the pattern

## Summary
Plate tectonics explains broad Earth patterns by linking evidence to plate motion.

Key ideas from this lesson:

- earthquakes, volcanoes, fossils, and ocean-floor rock ages support plate movement
- convergent, divergent, and transform boundaries produce different evidence patterns
- strong scientific explanations use claim, evidence, and reasoning together

When you study a tectonic setting, do not ask only, "What feature do I notice?" Ask, "What full pattern do I see, and which plate interaction best explains it?"
