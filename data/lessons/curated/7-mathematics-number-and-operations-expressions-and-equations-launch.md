# Expressions & Equations

## Learning Goal
- I can distinguish between an expression and an equation and explain what each one shows.
- I can write and solve multi-step equations using inverse operations.
- I can model a real-world problem with an equation and check whether my solution makes sense.

## Introduction
In Grade 7, algebra becomes more than finding one missing number. You need to read expressions, decide what they represent, and write equations that model a situation precisely.

An expression describes a quantity. An equation makes a claim that two quantities are equal. That difference matters because solving an equation is not just doing arithmetic. It is keeping both sides balanced while isolating the variable.

In this lesson, focus on three questions:

1. What quantity does the expression describe?
2. What equation matches the situation?
3. Which operations undo the structure of the equation in the correct order?

## Key Vocabulary
**Expression**: A mathematical phrase with numbers, variables, and operations, but no equals sign.

**Equation**: A statement that two expressions are equal.

**Variable**: A symbol that represents an unknown number.

**Equivalent expressions**: Expressions that have the same value for all values of the variable.

**Inverse operations**: Operations that undo each other, such as addition and subtraction or multiplication and division.

**Solution**: A value that makes an equation true.

## Expressions Describe; Equations Compare
Suppose a theater sells student tickets for `$6` each and charges a one-time online fee of `$4`.

If `t` is the number of tickets, the expression

`6t + 4`

describes the total cost.

It does not ask a question yet. It only represents the quantity.

Now suppose the total charge was `$34`. Then the situation becomes the equation

`6t + 4 = 34`

Now there is something to solve because the equation tells you that the total cost equals `34`.

This distinction helps with word problems:

- use an **expression** when you are writing a quantity
- use an **equation** when you are matching a quantity to a known value

## Building Equivalent Expressions
Equivalent expressions help you see structure before you solve.

Example:

`4(x - 3) + 2x`

Distribute first:

`4x - 12 + 2x`

Combine like terms:

`6x - 12`

So

`4(x - 3) + 2x` and `6x - 12`

are equivalent expressions.

This matters because rewriting can make the next step easier. In algebra, you often simplify first so the equation becomes clearer.

## Worked Example 1: Solving a Multi-Step Equation
Solve:

`3(x - 4) + 5 = 20`

Step 1: Distribute the `3`.

`3x - 12 + 5 = 20`

Step 2: Combine like terms on the left.

`3x - 7 = 20`

Step 3: Add `7` to both sides.

`3x = 27`

Step 4: Divide both sides by `3`.

`x = 9`

Check:

`3(9 - 4) + 5 = 3(5) + 5 = 15 + 5 = 20`

The solution is `x = 9`.

Notice the logic:

- simplify the expression side first
- undo addition or subtraction
- undo multiplication or division
- check the original equation, not just the simplified one

## Worked Example 2: Rational Numbers in an Equation
Solve:

`-2.5x + 7 = -8`

Step 1: Subtract `7` from both sides.

`-2.5x = -15`

Step 2: Divide both sides by `-2.5`.

`x = 6`

Check:

`-2.5(6) + 7 = -15 + 7 = -8`

So the solution is `6`.

This example shows why sign attention matters. Because both sides were negative before dividing, the final answer becomes positive.

## Worked Example 3: Writing an Equation From Context
A streaming service charges a one-time setup fee of `$12` and then `$5` for each month. Maya paid `$47` in total. For how many months did she pay?

Let `m` represent the number of months.

Write the equation:

`12 + 5m = 47`

Now solve.

Subtract `12` from both sides:

`5m = 35`

Divide by `5`:

`m = 7`

Interpret the answer:

Maya paid for `7` months.

Reasonableness check:

- `5 x 7 = 35`
- `35 + 12 = 47`

The result fits the context exactly.

## Common Mistake: Undoing Operations in the Wrong Order
Students sometimes look at

`3(x - 4) + 5 = 20`

and divide by `3` immediately.

That does not work because the left side is not a single factor of `3`. The `+ 5` is outside the parentheses, so the expression must be simplified first.

A stronger method is:

1. rewrite the side with structure by distributing or combining like terms
2. remove addition or subtraction that is outside the variable term
3. remove multiplication or division attached to the variable

Another common error is checking only the final simplified equation instead of the original equation. A correct answer must make the original statement true.

## Summary
Expressions and equations do different jobs in algebra. An expression represents a quantity. An equation shows that two quantities are equal.

To solve multi-step equations well:

1. simplify each side carefully
2. use inverse operations in a logical order
3. track signs and coefficients closely
4. check the solution in the original equation

When you can move from a context to an equation and then solve it clearly, you are doing the central work of Grade 7 algebra.
