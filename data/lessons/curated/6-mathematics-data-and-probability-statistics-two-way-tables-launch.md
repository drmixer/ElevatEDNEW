# Two-way Tables

## Learning Goal
- I can read a two-way table and identify what each row and column represents.
- I can find row totals, column totals, and the overall total.
- I can use the counts in a table to answer comparison questions correctly.

## Introduction
A two-way table helps organize data about two categories at the same time.

For example, a class survey might record:

- favorite snack
- grade level

or

- pet ownership
- after-school club choice

Instead of reading many separate facts, a two-way table lets you see how the categories connect in one place.

In this lesson, focus on three questions:

1. What does each row and column mean?
2. How do I find totals from the table?
3. How do I answer questions without mixing up part totals and whole totals?

## Key Vocabulary
**Two-way table**: A table that organizes data using two different categories.

**Row**: A horizontal line of entries in a table.

**Column**: A vertical line of entries in a table.

**Row total**: The sum of the values across one row.

**Column total**: The sum of the values down one column.

## Reading the Structure of a Two-Way Table
Suppose a school surveyed students about whether they brought lunch from home or bought lunch at school.

| Grade | Brought lunch | Bought lunch | Total |
| --- | ---: | ---: | ---: |
| 6 | 14 | 10 | 24 |
| 7 | 11 | 13 | 24 |
| Total | 25 | 23 | 48 |

What do the categories mean?

- the rows show grade level
- the columns show lunch choice

Each interior number tells how many students fit both categories.

Example:

The `14` means:

- Grade 6 students
- who brought lunch

This is the first skill with two-way tables: reading one entry accurately.

## Worked Example 1: Finding Totals
Use the table above.

How many Grade 7 students were surveyed?

Look across the Grade 7 row:

`11 + 13 = 24`

So the row total is `24`.

How many students bought lunch?

Look down the "Bought lunch" column:

`10 + 13 = 23`

So the column total is `23`.

How many students were surveyed altogether?

Use the grand total:

`48`

The totals help you answer broader questions without recounting every entry.

## Worked Example 2: Reading a Specific Comparison
Use the same table.

Which group is larger:

- Grade 6 students who brought lunch
- Grade 7 students who bought lunch

Compare the two matching entries:

- Grade 6 and brought lunch: `14`
- Grade 7 and bought lunch: `13`

Since `14 > 13`, the first group is larger by `1` student.

This kind of comparison works only if you match the correct row and column before reading the number.

## Worked Example 3: Completing a Missing Entry
Suppose this table shows club choices.

| Grade | Art Club | Robotics Club | Total |
| --- | ---: | ---: | ---: |
| 6 | 9 | ? | 15 |
| 7 | 8 | 11 | 19 |
| Total | 17 | 17 | 34 |

Find the missing Grade 6 Robotics Club entry.

Method 1: Use the Grade 6 row total.

`9 + ? = 15`

`? = 6`

Method 2: Use the Robotics column total.

`? + 11 = 17`

`? = 6`

Both methods give the same answer, which is a good check.

## How to Answer Table Questions Carefully
A two-way table can answer different kinds of questions.

Examples:

- "How many Grade 6 students bought lunch?"
- "How many students were in Grade 7?"
- "How many students brought lunch altogether?"

These are not the same type of question.

One asks for:

- a single interior cell

Another asks for:

- a row total

Another asks for:

- a column total

Strong table readers identify what kind of number the question asks for before searching the table.

## Common Mistake: Using the Wrong Total
Students often see a question about one group and answer with the grand total.

Example:

Question: "How many Grade 6 students bought lunch?"

Wrong answer:

`48`

Why is this wrong?

`48` is the total number of students surveyed, not the number in that one category.

The correct answer is the single matching cell:

`10`

Always ask:

- Do I need one cell?
- a row total?
- a column total?
- the grand total?

## Worked Example 4: Interpreting the Data
Use the lunch table again.

Is bringing lunch from home more common than buying lunch?

Compare the column totals:

- brought lunch: `25`
- bought lunch: `23`

Since `25` is greater than `23`, bringing lunch is slightly more common in this survey.

This conclusion comes from the totals across all grades, not from looking at only one row.

## Summary
Two-way tables organize data for two categories at once.

To read them correctly:

1. identify what the rows represent
2. identify what the columns represent
3. decide whether the question asks for a cell, row total, column total, or grand total
4. compare the correct counts before drawing a conclusion

Strong table reading is mostly about precision. If you match the right row and the right column, the table becomes much easier to use.
