# Poetry & Drama (PD)

## Learning Goal
- I can explain how a poem and a drama scene create meaning in different ways.
- I can use evidence from lines and stage directions to support an interpretation.
- I can identify a shared theme across two literary forms.

## Introduction
Poetry and drama can explore similar ideas, but they use different tools. A poem may use imagery and line breaks. A drama scene may use dialogue, action, and stage directions.

Strong readers pay attention to the form of the text. In this lesson, you will read a short poem and a short drama scene about speaking up when something feels unfair.

As you read, ask:

1. What does the poem emphasize through its wording and lines?
2. What does the drama scene show through dialogue and action?
3. What idea do both texts seem to share?

## Key Vocabulary
**Imagery**: Language that helps the reader picture, hear, or feel something.

**Line break**: The place where one line of poetry ends and the next begins.

**Dialogue**: The words spoken by characters.

**Stage direction**: A note in a play that shows action, movement, or tone.

**Theme**: An important idea or message in a text.

## Text 1: Poem
Read the poem below. The line numbers will help you point to evidence.

### Poem: "One Question"
**Line 1.** The room went quiet  
**Line 2.** after the list was read.  
**Line 3.** My question waited  
**Line 4.** like a folded note in my hand.  
**Line 5.** Then one voice rose,  
**Line 6.** small at first,  
**Line 7.** but clear enough  
**Line 8.** to make the silence answer.

## Text 2: Drama Scene
Read the scene below.

### Drama Scene: "After Class Meeting"
The teacher has just announced that recess will be shortened for the whole class because of noise in the hallway.

**MS. REYES:** If anyone has something to say, speak now.

No one answers. Several students look down.

**TALIA:** (quietly) But most of us were not even there.

**MS. REYES:** What are you asking, Talia?

**TALIA:** I am asking if it is fair to punish everyone for something only a few students did.

The room becomes still, and two other students nod.

## How the Poem Builds Meaning
The poem focuses on one moment when silence is broken.

The most important evidence is in Lines 5-8:

- "one voice rose"
- "small at first"
- "clear enough"
- "to make the silence answer"

These lines matter because they show that speaking up begins quietly, but still changes the room.

## How the Drama Scene Builds Meaning
The drama scene reveals the same kind of moment through dialogue and action.

Strong evidence includes:

- "No one answers"
- Talia speaks quietly at first
- other students nod after she speaks

These details show that one question can make others think differently about the situation.

## Model Comparison
Suppose the question is:

"What theme do the poem and the drama scene share?"

A strong answer is:

"Both texts show that one voice can change a silent room. The poem shows this through imagery and line breaks, while the drama scene shows it through dialogue and the reactions of other characters."

That answer works because it:

- identifies the shared theme
- uses evidence from both texts
- explains how each form creates meaning differently

## Common Mistake: Comparing Topic Only
A weak response might say:

"Both texts are about school."

That is true, but it is too broad.

A stronger comparison explains the shared idea and the different form.

## Summary
When you compare poetry and drama:

1. identify the shared theme
2. choose evidence that fits each form
3. explain how the form shapes meaning

In this lesson, both texts showed that speaking up can change a quiet room, but one used poetic imagery while the other used dialogue and stage action.
