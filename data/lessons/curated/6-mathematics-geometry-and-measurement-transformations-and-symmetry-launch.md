# Transformations & Symmetry

## Learning Goal
- I can identify translations, reflections, and rotations.
- I can describe what changes and what stays the same when a figure is moved.
- I can recognize when a figure has line symmetry.

## Introduction
Geometry is not only about measuring shapes. It is also about describing how shapes move.

A figure can:

- slide
- flip
- turn

These movements are called transformations.

In Grade 6, the main goal is to recognize the type of movement and notice what stays unchanged. Symmetry is closely related because it shows when part of a figure matches another part after a reflection.

As you read, focus on three questions:

1. Is the figure sliding, flipping, or turning?
2. What parts of the figure stay the same?
3. Does the figure have a line that divides it into matching halves?

## Key Vocabulary
**Transformation**: A movement of a figure on the plane.

**Translation**: A slide that moves every point the same distance and direction.

**Reflection**: A flip across a line.

**Rotation**: A turn around a point.

**Line symmetry**: A property of a figure that can be folded across a line so both halves match.

## The Three Basic Transformations
Each transformation changes position in a different way.

- A **translation** slides a figure without turning or flipping it.
- A **reflection** flips a figure across a line, like a mirror image.
- A **rotation** turns a figure around a point.

Even though the figure moves, the size and shape stay the same in each of these transformations.

That is why the image still matches the original figure exactly.

## Worked Example 1: Identifying a Translation
A triangle is moved `4` units to the right and `2` units up.

Does this describe a translation, reflection, or rotation?

This is a translation.

Why?

- every point moves the same distance
- the figure does not turn
- the figure does not flip

A translation changes location only.

## Worked Example 2: Identifying a Reflection
A capital letter `A` is drawn on a grid. Then an image appears on the opposite side of a vertical line so that the image looks like a mirror view of the original.

What transformation happened?

This is a reflection.

Why?

- the image flips across a line
- the figure appears reversed left to right
- matching points lie the same distance from the line of reflection

Reflections create mirror images.

## Worked Example 3: Identifying a Rotation
A square is turned one-quarter turn clockwise around its center.

What transformation happened?

This is a rotation.

Why?

- the figure turns around a point
- the sides and angles stay the same
- the orientation changes

A rotation moves the figure by turning it instead of sliding or flipping it.

## What Stays the Same?
For translations, reflections, and rotations:

- side lengths stay the same
- angle measures stay the same
- the overall shape stays the same

This means the image is the same size and shape as the original.

The main thing that changes is the figure's position or orientation.

## Worked Example 4: Line Symmetry
Does a rectangle have line symmetry?

Yes.

A rectangle has:

- one vertical line of symmetry through the middle
- one horizontal line of symmetry through the middle

If you reflect the rectangle across either of those lines, the halves match.

Now consider a scalene triangle.

A scalene triangle usually has no line symmetry because no reflection line makes both halves match exactly.

## Common Mistake: Mixing Up Reflection and Rotation
Students often confuse flipping with turning.

If a figure appears as a mirror image, the transformation is a reflection.

If the figure keeps the same orientation pattern but turns around a point, the transformation is a rotation.

Use this check:

- mirror image -> reflection
- turning around a point -> rotation

## Summary
Transformations describe how figures move on the plane.

To identify them well:

1. decide whether the figure slides, flips, or turns
2. check what stays the same about the size and shape
3. use symmetry to test whether reflection creates matching halves
4. describe the movement precisely

In Grade 6, strong transformation reasoning begins with recognizing the movement and explaining it clearly.
